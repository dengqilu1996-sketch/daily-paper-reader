## Abstract 

We present our solution to the 2025 SoccerNet Monoc-ular Depth Estimation Competition Challenge. Predicting the relative depth in football scenarios is challenging, es-pecially with only thousands of training samples available. To address this issue, our method leverages the powerful zero-shot capabilities of models pretrained on large-scale datasets to learn metric depth for effective relative depth prediction, achieving a score of 2.68 × 10 −3 on the chal-lenge set. 

## 1. Introduction 

SoccerNet-Depth [4] is a recently introduced video-based monocular depth estimation (MDE) dataset covering both basketball and football scenes. Accurate depth estima-tion serves as a fundamental pillar for broader 3D scene un-derstanding, with wide-ranging applications such as indoor depth completion [16, 17] and robust autonomous driving perception [5, 6, 19, 21]. In the specific context of sports videos, reliable spatial and depth cues are highly beneficial for advanced video understanding tasks, including 3D hu-man pose estimation [9], action quality assessment [13, 14], temporal action localization [20], and comprehensive mul-timodal video analysis (e.g., sports video captioning, re-trieval, and classification) [10–12]. The dataset provides relative depth annotations, as the depth data from the graph-ics engine encodes only relative, not absolute, values. In contrast, metric depth uses real-world units (e.g., meters), but for many vision tasks, the ordinal relationships between points (relative depth) are more important than absolute scale, allowing metric depth to serve as a proxy for relative depth in evaluation and training. In this work, we finetune the Depth Anything model [18]—a strong zero-shot MDE model pretrained

on large-scale datasets—for metric depth estimation. Re-cently, large-scale foundational frameworks have demon-strated extraordinary zero-shot generalizability and robust feature representation across various physical reasoning and dense segmentation tasks [8, 15]. Motivated by these ad-vancements, we treat the relative depth values in football games as metric depths within a 0–256 meter range. Since all samples are from football games with similar viewpoints ,the depth distribution is consistent, making it reasonable to interpret relative depths as metric values for finetuning and evaluation. 

## 2. Method 

In this section, we present the details of our method, in-cluding the network architecture, training objectives, and data augmentation strategies. 

2.1. Network Architecture 

Depth Anything Encoder. The Depth Anything Model is a high-performance, zero-shot relative depth estimation model, which is built on the DinoV2 [7] encoder and is pre-trained on 62 million unlabeled images. It further enhances performance by incorporating semantic constraints from the DinoV2 model during training. 

ZoeDepth Decoder. Following [18], we freeze the pow-erful encoder of the Depth Anything Model and adopt a LocalBins-based decoder, improved by ZoeDepth [2], to convert relative depth predictions to metric depth. The de-coder is randomly initialized and is the only trainable net-work module. 

2.2. Training objectives 

To supervise the model at the pixel level, we adopt the scale-invariant logarithmic loss [1], and omit chamfer loss for bin prediction due to its high memory cost and limited performance gain following ZoeDepth [2]. 1

2.3. Data augmentation 

We use only horizontal flipping for both train-time and test-time augmentation, as other methods like color jittering and random rotation reduced performance. At test time, we average predictions from the original and flipped images to improve results. 

## 3. Experiments 

In this section, we first present the implementation de-tails, followed by an explanation of the rationale behind cer-tain hyper-parameters and the data augmentation techniques used. 

3.1. Implementation Details 

The original image size of 1080 × 1920 is resized to 

384 × 704 as the network input. The network outputs a prediction of the same size as the input, which is then up-sampled to the original resolution using bilinear interpola-tion for evaluation. The entire network is optimized using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 6.44 × 10 −4

and is trained for 100 epochs with a batch size of one on 8 RTX 4090 GPUs. The learning rate is kept constant for the first 70% of epochs, after which it is reduced to 1/10 , 000 

of the initial value using cosine annealing. 

3.2. Results 

Our method achieves an RMSE of 2.68 × 10 −3 in the competition using DepthAnything as the backbone. During training, we observed that certain hyperparameters and data augmentation techniques have significant effects on perfor-mance. 

Learning Rate and Training Epoch. As shown in Table 1, we experimented with different learning rates and training epochs. The results indicate that a higher learning rate and longer training iterations are beneficial, reducing the RMSE from 1.60 × 10 −3 to 1.37 × 10 −3 on the 1/2 test set. Additionally, we visualize the evaluation results dur-ing training in Fig. 1, which shows that a lower learning rate results in fewer outliers but has less potential for fur-ther reduction. This observation inspired us to increase both the learning rate and the training schedule, leading to bet-ter convergence, as shown in the figure. Furthermore, we trained a DepthAnythingV2 model using the learning rate recommended by the authors, but it achieved worse results, suggesting that further hyperparameter tuning may be nec-essary. 

Train-time Data Augmentation. Table 2 reports an abla-tion study on data augmentation, evaluated by RMSE on the full test set after 2 epochs. We restricted random rota-tion to ±15 ◦ and masked invalid regions after rotation when computing loss. However, rotation significantly worsened performance, and color jittering also had a slight negative effect, likely due to distribution mismatch with the test set. Thus, we only apply horizontal flipping for data augmenta-tion during training. 

Test-time Data Augmentation. We also applied multi-scale test-time augmentation (with scales of 0.5, 1.0, and 1.5). As shown in row 7 of Table 2, this strategy led to a decrease in performance. 

Ensemble. Following [3], we performed an ensemble of two different models with similar RMSE performance. Specifically, we first aligned the scale and shift of the two predictions and then averaged their outputs. However, as shown in row 8 of Table 2, this method also resulted in de-graded performance.
