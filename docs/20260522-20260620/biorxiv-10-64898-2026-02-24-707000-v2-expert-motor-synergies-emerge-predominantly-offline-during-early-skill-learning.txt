# Summary 55 Everyday skilled actions depend on the formation of coordinated motor synergies that 56 integrate multiple digits into stable, low-dimensional control units. Although initial 57 practice of a new skill leads to rapid performance improvements, it is unclear whether 58 the underlying movement kinematics reorganize on a similar timescale and in a way that 59 directly relates to these gains. It also remains uncertain whether such reorganization 60 occurs mainly during active practice or instead during brief rest breaks. Here, we 61 tracked the temporal evolution of multi-digit synergy formation during early learning of a 62 naturalistic keypress skill. Initial practice rapidly sculpted the motor repertoire 63 toward higher-order, temporally compressed and overlapping multi-digit synergies. 64 These synergies emerged after only minutes of practice and continued to be expressed 65 along the full training session. Notably, they were primarily shaped across brief rest 66 breaks and robustly predicted individual skill proficiency. Across learning, distinct 67 synergy subtypes emerged, differing in their heuristic prevalence . Rarely expressed 68 synergies reflected transient novice patterns, synergies expressed 69 at intermediate levels could index exploratory and trial-initiation strategies, and highly 70 expressed synergies emerged later to dominate performance, reflecting the 71 consolidation and expansion of skilled motor control. Together, these findings indicate 72 that skilled performance is supported by the early formation of a compact repertoire of 73 expert multi-digit synergies that emerge preferentially across rest periods and 74 predict subsequent skill gains. They further raise the hypothesis that explicitly training 75 such expert synergies alongside task goals could enhance learning in domains such as 76 the arts, sport, and neurorehabilitation. 77 78

# Introduction 79 Activities of daily living, from walking and handwriting to athletic play, depend on the 80 precise orchestration of temporally ordered motor sequences 1-4 . Fluency develops as 81 the motor system learns to coordinate fingers in new ways. These task-specific 82 movement patterns link motions within and across digits so that they work together to 83 meet the demands of the object, the required force, timing, and accuracy 5,6 . With 84 practice, discrete keypresses become coarticulated into coordinated multi-digit 85 synergies, reducing effective control dimensionality and stabilizing execution, thereby 86 optimizing performance across skills such as grasping 7-9 , typing 10 , finger spelling 11 ,87 and haptic exploration 12 . Studying how motor synergies emerge and reorganize 88 provides a direct window into the neuromotor mechanisms that support the acquisition, 89 refinement, and generalization of skilled actions 13 .90 91 Early learning of naturalistic skills is characterized by prominent initial performance 92 improvements 14-16 . Over the course of learning, skill performance fluctuates during 93 practice and brief rest periods as a result of the dynamic interplay between 94 performance-enhancing processes, such as learning, and performance-limiting 95 mechanisms, including motor or cognitive fatigue (sometimes referred to collectively as 96 reactive inhibition) 17,18 . However, how these competing processes are expressed in the 97 kinematic signatures that drive the transition from novice movement patterns to expert 98 multi-digit synergies, particularly during the earliest phases of skill acquisition, is not 99 known. Early gains in skill may arise from several, non-mutually exclusive, kinematic 100 adjustments: faster execution 19 , greater temporal overlap among digit movements 101 (coarticulation) 20-22 , reparameterization of velocity profiles 23 , improved spatial 102 accuracy 24 or their combination. In the temporal domain, the formation of multi-digit 103 synergies characteristic of expertise could unfold rapidly, on the order of seconds, or 104 accrue more slowly over minutes to hours 25,26 . Such reorganization may also proceed 105 during brief rest intervals 27 or instead manifest primarily during active practice, in 106 parallel with expansion of memory capacity 28 . This pattern could suggest a dissociation 107 between the time course of behavioral improvement and the optimization of underlying 108

# Discussion 239 Here we show that complex, multi-digit synergies emerge rapidly after practice onset 240 and increase progressively during the early phases of naturalistic skill learning, with 241 their most prominent development occurring across intervening rest breaks. The 242 synergies that supported acquisition of the trained skill were characterized by small, 243 rapid, and overlapping digit movements that gradually crystallized over early trials and 244 robustly predicted subsequent performance gains. Notably, we did not observe fatigue-245 dependent reductions in multi-digit synergy use. 246 247

10 Motor learning, from everyday skills such as typing to highly trained behaviors like 250 musical performance, relies on the production of temporally precise action sequences 251 that form the basis of fine motor expertise 16,44-46 . These sequences are not controlled 252 muscle by muscle, but through coordinated motor synergies, making their 253 reorganization central to skilled performance. Accordingly, examining how motor 254 synergies reorganize with learning provides a direct window into the neuromotor 255 processes that support the acquisition, refinement, and generalization of skilled actions. 256 257 To address this question, we recorded movements with a high-speed camera while 258 subjects learned a new sequential motor skill. Markerless pose estimation software 47 259 and a dimensionality reduction strategy 47 allowed us to characterize synergies (unique 260 combinations of covarying four-digit movements over one to five keypresses), as the 261 new skill was learnt. Multi-digit synergies are the ultimate expression of efficient 262 modular control strategies used by the central nervous system to execute sequential 263 skills, like playing musical instruments 48-53 . We observed that synergy morphology 264 evolved remarkably quickly, changing within seconds of training onset. Early practice 265 rapidly sculpted the motor repertoire toward temporally compressed, overlapping, 266 higher-order multi-digit synergies, whose dominance from approximately the twelfth 267 practice trial onward, when performance near plateaued, robustly predicted the degree 268 of skill acquisition and likely reflected an expansion of underlying memory capacity 28 .269 270 Synergies involving single digits, which predominated during the earliest trials, 271 progressively transitioned into coordinated multi-digit patterns as training advanced. 272 This rapid reorganization of synergy structure closely mirrored the time course of 273 behavioral improvement. This transition may parallel the rapid shift from stimulus-bound 274 responding to internally generated sequence production, in which actions are guided by 275 an internal memory representation rather than discrete external cues, as is typical of 276 reaction-time–based paradigms 54 . The swift emergence of this internally driven mode of 277 control suggests that developing motor kinematics, associated with an expanding skill 278 memory 28 , may become primary constraints on behavior. 279 280

Mechanisms Underlying the Formation of Expert Synergies 281 282 How might multi-digit synergies emerge preferentially across rest rather than during 283 practice? We found that behavioral improvements occurred primarily across the brief 284 rest periods interleaved with practice (i.e., micro-offline gains), consistent with prior 285 reports 15,19,27,34,55-57 . In the context of challenging skills, micro-offline skill gains are up 286 to four times larger than those identified following overnight sleep 15 , are reproducible 57-287 61 and persist even when practice bouts are shortened to as little as 5 seconds, arguing 288 against recovery from performance fatigue as their primary source 58 . It has been 289 proposed that this form of micro-offline gains represent a form of memory 290 consolidation 15,54,62 , a view supported by mechanistic work 34 60,61 62 .291 292 Our results extend prior work by demonstrating that motor synergy maps can reorganize 293 across rest periods, revealing a previously unrecognized neural–motor correlate of 294 micro–offline learning. More broadly, our findings suggest that practice and rest make 295 distinct yet complementary contributions to naturalistic skill learning. Practice itself may 296 primarily encode elemental action representations and provide the feedback necessary 297 to optimize movement kinematics 63,64 . Rest intervals interleaved with practice may 298 promote the binding of individual action components into higher-order synergies, 299 effectively compressing the action space and freeing capacity for further online 300 learning 28 . This interpretation accords with prior accounts suggesting that the relative 301 contributions of online and offline processes to learning are shaped by task 302 configuration 28 and context 65 , and that these processes are complementary rather than 303 competitive 18 .304 305 Although our design followed published guidelines to minimize it 42 , accumulating 306 fatigue with continued practice 41 could, in principle, reduce the use of expert multi-digit 307 synergies either within individual 10-s practice trials or cumulatively across the 36-trial 308 session 66,67 . We found no evidence this occurred. Once multi-digit synergies were 309 established by the end of early learning, their use showed no decline within trials at the 310 end of training (when fatigue would be maximal in the session, Figure S3 ) or across the 311

Do Distinct Synergy Subtypes Subserve Early Naturalistic Learning? 316 317 To aid interpretation, we classified synergies by the proportion of training time during 318 which they were expressed. These categories were intended as descriptive rather than 319 as discrete synergy classes. Because prevalence varied continuously across the 320 session and showed no clear natural boundaries, we grouped synergies into four 321 heuristic bins (<5%, 6–20%, 21–49%, and >50%) that balanced interpretability with 322 adequate sampling. This framework distinguished relatively rare from more predominant 323 synergies and allowed us to consider whether expression frequency may relate to 324 distinct functional roles during skill acquisition. A set of synergies expressed for <5% of 325 total training time (1–2 keypresses/synergy) appeared predominantly in the first few 326 trials and then vanished ( Figure 4C , green arrow; Figure 5E ). These “novice” synergies 327 likely reflect provisional kinematic solutions to the demands of a newly acquired skill, 328 which are rapidly supplanted by higher-order, multi-digit synergies as learning unfolds. 329 A second subgroup consisted of synergies expressed for 6–20% of training time (1–2 330 keypresses per synergy), which emerged and faded mid-practice ( Figure 4C, dark blue 331 arrow; Figure 5D ). These “exploratory” synergies may reflect kinematic experimentation 332 during the transition from stimulus-driven to internally generated execution of the 333 naturalistic skill. Synergies expressed 21–49% of training time (1–4 keypresses per 334 synergy) appeared consistently at trial onset as performance neared asymptote and 335 remained present until the end of the session ( Fig. 4C, red arrow ; 5C ). These “trial-336 initiation” synergies may index the contribution of advance planning to the earliest 337 phase of trial performance, a possibility consistent with estimates of the preplanning 338 capacity of 2-4 keypresses in sequence learning 68-70 , 71 .339 340 Synergies expressed for >50% of total training time (up to five keypresses per synergy) 341 constituted the “predominant/expert” class: they first appeared around mid-training and 342

Limitations and Future Directions 359 360 Several limitations of the present work suggest fruitful avenues for future research. First, 361 employing multi-camera motion capture would permit full three-dimensional kinematic 362 reconstruction, enabling a more comprehensive characterization of movement structure 363 than was possible here. Second, extending synergy analyses beyond distal digit 364 segments to include additional digits and proximal effectors that support manual 365 function, such as the wrist, arm, and shoulder, may uncover further kinematic 366 contributions to skilled performance and clarify how distal and proximal control are 367 integrated. Finally, future studies should test whether the kinematic mechanisms 368 underlying sequence learning generalize to multi-limb coordination, postural control, and 369 other forms of motor learning, an issue central to evaluating their broader relevance for 370 neurorehabilitation of skilled function. 371

ONLINE AND OFFLINE LEARNING CONTRIBUTIONS 446 Microscale changes in skill performance were decomposed into online and offline 447 components using correct sequences per second (cs/s) as the behavioral metric. Micro-448 online gains were defined as the within-trial change in cs/s, computed as the difference 449 between the average performance in the first and last second of each 10-s practice trial. 450 Micro-offline gains were defined as the between-trial change in cs/s, computed as the 451 difference between the last second of a given trial and the first second of the 452 subsequent trial. These measures were calculated over the first 12 trials, corresponding 453 to the early learning phase. Cumulative micro-online and micro-offline changes were 454 then summed across this interval to estimate their relative contributions to overall skill 455 acquisition. 456 To characterize structural changes in motor behavior, we examined trial-level synergy 457 distributions based on HDBSCAN-derived cluster labels assigned to each video frame. 458 For each 10-s trial, we extracted the first and last 1-s windows (120 frames each at 120 459 fps) and quantified the composition of behavioral clusters (synergies) within each 460 window as a normalized histogram, excluding frames labeled as noise. Jensen– 461 Shannon divergence (JSD) was computed between the first and last second of each 462 trial to index online synergy reorganization, and between the last second of one trial and 463 the first second of the next to index offline reorganization. This approach enabled a 464 direct comparison between kinematic reconfiguration and skill change at the microscale. 465

CLUSTERING OF BEHAVIOURAL DATA 488 To identify motor synergies, we applied HDBSCAN (Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial 489 Clustering of Applications with Noise) to the two-dimensional UMAP embedding of the 490 wavelet-transformed pose data 78 . JENSEN-SHANNON DIVERGENCE AND PERMUTATION TESTING 509 

510 To quantify how motor synergy distributions changed with learning, we computed the 511 Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) between behavioral label distributions at different 512 trial time points. JSD was chosen because it is symmetric, bounded, and robust to zero 513 probabilities 79 . Further, JSD captures proportional changes across all labels in a scale-514 invariant and interpretable way, avoids directional bias, and uses smoothing that 515 prevents divergence due to missing cluster labels. 516 517 For each participant, synergy labels within a trial were converted into a probability 518 distribution by computing the relative frequency of each cluster. For each pair of trials, 519 JSD was then calculated between these distributions to index changes in synergy 520 composition. To assess whether observed JSD values exceeded chance, we used a 521 nonparametric permutation test: cluster labels were randomly reassigned 100,000 times 522 between the two distributions, recomputing JSD on each iteration to generate a null 523 distribution. The empirical p-value was defined as the proportion of permuted JSD 524 values greater than or equal to the observed value, preserving marginal structure while 525 randomizing label correspondence. 526 527 For group-level inference, individual p-values were transformed to z-scores using the 528 inverse standard normal distribution and combined across participants using Stouffer’s 529 method, yielding a single aggregate z-statistic and Stouffer’s p-value. 530
