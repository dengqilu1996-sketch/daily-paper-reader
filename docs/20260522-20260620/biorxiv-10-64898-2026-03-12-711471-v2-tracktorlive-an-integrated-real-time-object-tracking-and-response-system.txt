Abstract 14 

Real -time tracking and automated response systems are essential for standardising 15 

experiments, reducing observer bias, and improving reproducibility in studies of 16 

movement and behaviour. However, existing solutions face significant challenges: AI -17 

based tracking systems require expensive hardware and impose computational delays, 18 

creating challenges for closed -loop experiments; existing real -time tracking tools lack 19 

standardised implementations for response delivery; and steep learning curves limit 20 

accessibility for users without programming or computer vision expertise. Here, we 21 

introduce TracktorLive, an open -source Python package designed to overcome these 22 

challenges through concurrency and a modular, ‘cassette’ -based architecture. 23 

TracktorLive leverages traditional computer vision techniques to perform image -based 24 

object detection without the need for expensive hardware or deep learning. By 25 

parallelizing object tracking and response delivery into separate , concurrent server and 26 

client processes, the software minimizes frame processing time, enabling rapid, real -time 27 

analysis and response delivery. User -friendly 'cassettes' —portable code snippets that 28 

can be copy -pasted into scripts —enable users with minimal programming experience to 29 

implement complex workflows for use in experiments and practical applications. We 30 

demonstrate TracktorLive's utility through several applications, including microcontroller -31

1. Introduction 48 

Data collection during studies involving movement and behaviour often relies on human 49 

intervention and direct observation, which introduces bias (Box 1), can lead to 50 

experimenter -driven disturbances  [1 –3] , and requires a significant investment of time and 51 

effort on the part of the experimenter. A common approach to increasing measurement 52 

accuracy and reducing bias is to record subjects on video and implement post hoc 53 

tracking using manual or automated solutions  [4 –6] . However, many experiments also 54 

necessitate dynamic interactivity between the experimenter, the arena, and the subjects. 55 

For instance, a shrew may need to be released into a maze at a specific time, and a 56 

reward dispensed if it attempts to solve the maze [7] . Similarly, a stimulus might need to 57 

be delivered when a school of fish enters a specific area of the experimental arena [8,9] .58 

In more complex setups, a continuous display might need to be updated dynamically so 59 

an insect perceives simulated movement in a virtual landscape [10,11] . Such experiments 60 

will benefit from real -time tracking and automated responses [12,13] , which offer a 61

In the last fifteen years, major advances have been made in tracking the positions of cells 65 

[14,15] and animals  [16 –22]  from video data. These tracking tools have helped overcome 66 

the constraints of traditional observational methods and resulted in a bloom of proprietary 67 

and open -source solutions in fields ranging from experimental biology to pest 68 

management  [16,23 –26] .69 

70 

Recent advances in automated, image -based tracking have resulted in the development 71 

of a host of tracking techniques, both with and without Artificial Intelligence ( AI ). However, 72 

tools are lacking that allow experimenters to design a response system that can decide 73 

on and execute real -time responses based on the tracked locations of objects in video. 74 

When researchers have had to adopt automated response delivery systems, they have 75 

typically developed a specific solution —such as the use of infrared beams or RFID tags —76 

that may not be applicable to other experimental setups [12,27,28] . Most recently, 77 

methods focussing specifically on real -time animal tracking have become available, which 78 

rely on deep learning -based approaches [21,29,30] . While highly accurate and robust, 79 

these solutions are computationally intensive and may impose a recurrent lag for 80 

processing each frame. As a result, AI -based tracking has improved post hoc analyses. 81 

However, numerous challenges need to be overcome for their widespread applicability in 82 

closed -loop experiments, where stimuli must be delivered immediately in response to 83 

location or behavioural changes, particularly when conducted on lower -end hardware. 84 

Some real -time tracking software can interface with external hardware, such as 85 

Raspberry Pi or Arduino boards, to facilitate stimulus delivery [9,26,29] , but these systems 86 

are often designed for specific experimental setups and lack standardised 87 

implementations for closed -loop experiments, limiting their generalisability, accessibility, 88 

and broader adoption. 89

Box 1. Reducing observer bias in experimental studies involving animal 

movement and behaviour 

Minimizing observer bias has been a long -standing challenge for studies involving 

animal movement and behaviour —in ecology, medicine, neuroscience, and 

experimental biology, among other fields  [31 –33] . In particular, data collection during 

behavioural experiments often relies on direct observation, which is prone to bias and 

experimenter -driven disturbances  [1 –3] . A common approach for reducing these effects 

is video recording experiments to avoid the presence of an observer, and using ‘blind’ 

protocols, in which the experimenter is unaware of a subject’s identity and treatment 

group  [2,34 –36] .

Despite the benefits of blind protocols  [32,33,37 –39] , blinding is often impractical —for 

example, when a single researcher runs an experiment, or in field conditions where time 

and resources to conduct experiments are limited. Similarly, the presence of an 

experimenter near test animals is sometimes unavoida ble, particularly when a stimulus

must be presented or triggered —for instance, when deploying a decoy predator to elicit 

an escape response [8,40] , or for delivering food as positive reinforcement [41] . Even 

when a stimulus is operated remotely , experimenters may still introduce bias due to 

individual variability in training and experience, their subjective perceptions and 

interpretations of animal behaviours, and variability in reaction times when triggering 

stimuli. Additionally, subtle gender biases have been documented among 

experi menters, leading to different behavioural outcomes in test subjects [42,43] . Given 

these challenges, real -time tracking and automated stimulus delivery offer a promising 

approach to standardizing experiments and reducing observer bias . See

automated response delivery using an early version of TracktorLive. The results show 

a clear benefit of using automated response delivery, which reduced the experimental

2. Software 107 

2.1. How TracktorLive works (software architecture) 108 

TracktorLive is available for installation as a one -command pip -installable python 109 

package ( pip install tracktorlive ). As a fully -fledged python package, the 110 

capabilities of TracktorLive are vast. To fully leverage this growing range of capabilities, 111 

TracktorLive is designed with a command -line focus, avoiding users and developers 112 

being restricted by operating -system specific norms in graphics. TracktorLive is primarily 113 

designed for GNU/Linux operating systems like Ubuntu or Pop! OS, but the software, the 114 

examples, and the ‘cassettes’ (see below) have all been installed and tested on Windows 115 

11 (using the Windows Subsystem for Linux, WSL). Detailed installation instructions and 116 

relevant notes for all operating systems are available on the GitHub repository. 117 

118 

The utility of TracktorLive is derived from three important design choices to overcome key 119 

challenges of implementing real -time tracking and response software. 120 

121

First, at its core, TracktorLive runs Tracktor [16] , an image -based object detection and 122 

tracking freeware that does not rely on deep -learning. Instead, Tracktor adopts traditional 123 

computer vision techniques including adaptive thresholding (to segment the object of 124 

interest from the background) and the Hungarian algorithm (to link individual identities 125 

between frames), making it relatively lightweight and eliminating the need for expensive 126 

hardware or specialised user training. Thanks to Tracktor’s minimalistic design, 127 

TracktorLive can easily be expanded with additional pre -processing steps such as 128 

masking and background subtraction to improve tracking performance. 129 

130 

Second, TracktorLive parallelises object tracking and actions through a semaphore -131 

protected shared memory architecture, which substantially reduces the time taken for 132 

processing each incoming video frame (Fig. 1). ‘Semaphore’ locks prevent multiple 133 

parallel processes from simultaneously trying to write or read data from the shared 134 

memory (in what are called ‘race’ conditions), ensuring that only one process has access 135 

to tracking data at any time. The primary parallel process, a ‘Tracktor Server’, handles all 136 

tasks related to object tracking in incoming video frames, while separate parallel 137 

processes (‘Tracktor Clients’) are initiated to adjudicate response delivery. As a result of 138 

this parallelisation, real -time tracking and response is possible without incurring 139 

significant computational delay between consecutive frames. 140 

141 

Third, TracktorLive is readily accessible to those unfamiliar with computer vision or 142 

advanced programming. The software has been designed to work with ‘cassettes’. 143 

Cassettes are small, portable snippets of code designed to be ‘dropped in’ scripts to 144 

achieve particular tasks (Fig. 2), such as video processing and response delivery. 145 

TracktorLive includes a command -line utility that automates simple tasks and circumvents 146 

the need for writing code for basic applications of the software (Fig. 2). The software also 147 

comes with a graphical user interface (GUI) that facilitate s tuning the object detection and 148 

segmentation parameters prior to tracking. These features make implementing real -time 149 

tracking and responses accessible to users with minimal experience with the python 150 

programming language. A detailed description of TracktorLive’s architecture and design 151 

is provided as Supplementary Information and in Fig. S1 .152

2.2. Running TracktorLive 154 

The standard way of running TracktorLive is to write python scripts (Fig s. 2 and S2 ) that 155 

declare a Tracktor Server (which does all the object tracking in parallel, behind the 156 

scenes) and, optionally, a number of Tracktor Clients, which handle response delivery. 157 

‘Cassettes’ are modular code snippets that can be copy -pasted into scripts and stacked 158 

in any user -defined order to change the behaviour of the program to achieve specific 159 

goals. We currently provide over 30 cassettes for a broad range of applications (Table 1). 160 

161 

The behaviours of both the Tracktor Server and Tracktor Clients are fully programmable 162 

by inserting cassettes into the script. Experienced users and developers can easily 163 

expand existing cassettes and create new ones to accomplish additional tasks. In the 164 

spirit of open -source software, we explicitly welcome cassette contributions from users to 165 

be added to the Library of Cassettes. Cassette creators are specified and credited at the 166 

start of the code for each cassette. We expect TracktorLive’s capabilities to increase 167 

further as the community begins to adopt it and more cassettes are contributed. 168 

3. Applications 169 

Applications of TracktorLive’s real -time capabilities are vast, ranging from stimulus 170 

delivery when objects are in a given location or perform certain behaviours; recording and 171 

tracking movements only under specific conditions; live plotting data as an experiment 172 

unfolds; and activating mechanisms to open doors or deliver food; to implementing 173 

conditional workflows to alert experimenters by email or phone (Table 1). 174

4. Limitations 242 

TracktorLive uses Tracktor [16] under the hood, and therefore shares both its strengths 243 

and limitations. Tracktor can reliably track single individuals under varying experimental 244 

conditions, including in situations with uneven lighting and/or where the background is not 245 

static. It can also simultaneously track multiple individuals in the same or in distinct 246 

arenas. However, Tracktor —and by extension, TracktorLive —is not designed to solve 247 

crosses, occlusions and/or a change in the number of individuals in the arena: situations 248 

where multiple animals walk, fly or swim above or below each other, enter and leave the 249 

arena, or are occluded from the camera (for example, when entering a shelter or exiting 250 

the camera’s field of view). This is because Tracktor maintains the individual identities of 251 

unmarked objects using the Hungarian algorithm, a highly reliable technique provided 252 

there are no crosses or occlusions. In scenarios where crosses or occlusions can occur 253 

or the number of individuals is dynamic, TracktorLive can be used reliably only when 254 

tracking and response delivery does not depend on the identities of the individuals 255 

tracked. 256 

257 

In scenarios where maintaining individual identities is essential, the use of a traditional 258 

(non -AI) tracking algorithm becomes infeasible. However, the implementation of Tracktor 259 

within TracktorLive occurs within a single method ( Server._eachframe ) in the Tracktor 260 

Server object class, and an experienced developer could easily replace this core tracking 261 

method with a different, AI -based method (for instance, based on the “You Only Look 262 

Once ” (YOLO) framework [45] ). Given the modularity of TracktorLive, this modification 263 

would maintain the integrity and benefits of TracktorLive’s architecture as well as the use 264 

of the library of cassettes and command -line utility. Similarly, developers could modify 265 

TracktorLive’s tracking method to detect ArUco markers —open -source simplified QR 266 

codes that can be mounted on animals [25] —allowing to maintain individual identities 267 

without the use of AI when crosses or occlusions occur, or when the number of individuals 268 

in the arena changes. 269 

270

cassettes risks affecting the processing speed of the software. If users create and stack 272

5. Future prospects 276 

Numerous potential applications of TracktorLive exist that we have not yet explored. 277 

Specifically, the integration of TracktorLive with low -cost embedded systems (e.g., 278 

Arduino), could enable a variety of closed -loop systems (setups where a biological 279 

process is monitored in real -time, and information is used to immediately adjust a 280 

treatment or stimulus). In organismal biology, these enhanced setups could enable better 281 

experimental design in the study of escape responses, animal cognition, or decision -282 

making. Applications also extend across a wide range of domains beyond the study of 283 

animal movement and behaviour, from cell biology to ecological monitoring. For instance, 284 

closed -loop systems leveraging TracktorLive could facilitate experiments in which neural 285 

or cellular activity of labelled cells is linked to state changes (e.g. fluorescence) or 286 

movement, allowing real -time modification of environmental conditions based on detected 287 

signals [46] . Integrating TracktorLive with deep learning tools could also help support 288 

wildlife surveys and monitoring through automated recording and analysis of occurrences 289 

and activity. 290 

291 

TracktorLive can also be helpful to automate and optimize lab protocols. Using cassettes, 292 

a researcher can be notified when an experiment ends and schedule automatically 293 

triggered analyses scripts or the secure transfer of data and results to a remote location, 294 

digital repository or website. TracktorLive could also help improve animal welfare ; for 295 

example, by automatically detecting sick/dead animals based on kinematics, or 296 

automatically regulating oxygen levels and water replacement, or providing 297 

environmental enrichment. Finally, enthusiasts could develop offline, privacy -friendly 298 

home applications for day -to -day use, or for educational purposes to promote scientific 299 

interest. The possibilities are vast (see Table S 2). 300

6. Conclusion 301 

TracktorLive addresses a critical gap in experimental biology by providing a low -cost, 302 

readily accessible solution for real -time tracking and responses to movement and 303 

behaviour. The modular architecture and efficient parallelised processing can support a 304 

diversity of experimental designs without expensive hardware or advanced programming 305 

skills. As the research community adopts TracktorLive, we anticipate the Library of 306 

Cassettes will continue to expand through user contributions that extend the software's 307 

capabilities, including into new domains. The integration of TracktorLive with emerging 308 

technologies including, advanced imaging techniques, embedded systems and machine 309 

learning holds particular promise for developing innovative applications. Ultimately, by 310 

lowering the financial and technical barriers to real -time tracking and response systems, 311 

TracktorLive provides a scalable, accessible, and reproducible solution for studying 312 

movement and behaviour across diverse systems. 313

Figure 1 : An overview of the TracktorLive pipeline . Frames from a live feed (A) recording 480 

subjects (here, a fish in a tank) are passed to a Tracktor Server, for which tracking parameters 481 

can be specified through a custom GUI (B) . The Tracktor Server’s behaviour is specified by the 482 

addition of ‘cassette functions’ to its on -board cassette deck (C) . The tracked location of each 483 

animal is shared with multiple Tracktor Clients (D1, D2) , which can drive response delivery. For 484 

example, client D1 can send messages to an Arduino board (E) , which triggers a mechanical 485 

stimulus (F) to elicit an escape response from the fish when it is in a certain location. Meanwhile, 486 

Client D2 can play a video (G) to trigger a looming stimulus when, for instance, the fish moves 487 

below a certain velocity for a specified duration. Sometimes, users may want to toggle a switch 488 

to save video dynamically in response to the animals’ positions. The Video Chunker cassette (H) 489 

can toggle whether video is saved to disk in response to the fish’s location, preserving disk space 490 

by saving video only when it is of interest. The figure illustrates only a small subset of the types 491 

of experiments TracktorLive can facilitate. 492

Figure 2 : The standard s tructure of a TracktorLive script . Every TracktorLive python script 494 

starts with importing tracktorlive as trl (by convention) (L2). Then, after reading tracking -495 

related parameters (L5), the Tracktor Server is declared (L7 -8). Along with the server, a 496 

semaphore manager ( semm ) is automatically spawned. The user then customises the behaviour 497 

of the server by inserting server cassettes. These could, for instance, control when video storage 498 

is turned on, or edit the incoming video stream on -the -fly to improve the tracking performance (for 499 

example, adjust the brightness and contrast). Then, optionally, one or more clients are declared 500 

(L53). As with the server, the behaviour of the client is customised by adding client cassettes, 501 

which can handle visualisation, interfacing with other devices, or running commands in response 502 

to the movements of tracked animals. Finally, the server, semaphore manager, and client are all 503 

initialised together at the end of the script (L93 -94). See Fig S2 for a visualisation of an actual 504 

script with complete code. 505
