Abstract 

There is more to musculotendon path modeling than aligning a cable to reflect the geometric features of a muscu-lotendon unit. From the perspective of simulation accuracy, the key is to replicate the length– and moment arm–joint angle relations of the target muscle. In this study, we propose an e ff ect-oriented approach of automated path model-ing, via hybrid calibration based on muscle surface mesh and moment arm. The task is formulated as a least-squares optimization problem with a threefold objective for the path to: (1) pass through multiple ellipses representing mus-cle cross-sections, (2) yield moment arms that match experimental measurements, and (3) yield moment arms in the expected functional directions. We demonstrate the performance of our optimization framework with the muscu-loskeletal surface mesh from the Visible Human Male and moment arm datasets from literature—producing 42 paths that are anatomically realistic and biomechanically accurate within 20.1 minutes. Our optimization framework is gradient-specified, which is faster and more accurate than using the default numerical gradient, making it applicable for large-scale subject-specific uses. 

Keywords: Gradient, Model calibration, Moment arm, Musculoskeletal model, Muscle geometry, Muscle path, Optimization 

1. Introduction 1

In musculoskeletal modeling, a musculotendon path is a simplification of the 3-D musculotendon geometry, and 2

often it is a cable that reflects the shape of a musculotendon unit 1 as well as its tendinous attachments on the bones 3

(Garner and Pandy, 2000; Hicks et al., 2015; Modenese and Kohout, 2020). Depending on how simplified the mus-4

culotendon geometry needs to be, the path is configured based on anchor points (i.e., the origin, via, and insertion 5

points) and wrapping obstacles (e.g., cylinders): The simplest path is a straight cable defined by the origin and inser-6

tion, while via points and wrapping obstacles may be used for extra geometric features such as turnings and curvatures 7

(Delp et al., 1990; Garner and Pandy, 2001; Ma’touq et al., 2019). Importantly, the locations of the anchor points (and 8

obstacles) are not defined with respect to the same bone, so the path geometry will change due to joint rotation as the 9

musculotendon geometry does. 10 

Despite such geometric simplification, a well-modeled path should retain two properties of the muscle across 11 

di ff erent joint configurations, namely its length and its moment arm (Garner and Pandy, 2001; Hicks et al., 2015). 12 

These are crucial properties that the accuracy of musculoskeletal simulations depends on: In a forward simulation, 13 

given the neuromuscular signal, muscle length is related to how much force can be exerted (Guo et al., 2022; Millard 14 

et al., 2024), and muscle moment arm determines how much joint moment (hence what motion and how much end-15 

eff ector force) is to be generated from this force (McFarland et al., 2023; Ong et al., 2016; Zajac, 1993). In an inverse 16

joint reaction forces and muscle activations (Beaucage-Gauvreau et al., 2019; Erdemir et al., 2007; Hamner and Delp, 18 

2013; Karabulut et al., 2020; Prilutsky and Zatsiorsky, 2002). Therefore, the mission of muscle path modeling is in 19 

some sense to replicate the length–joint angle relation as well as the moment arm–joint angle relation of the target 20 

muscle. 21 

In general, there are two approaches to replicate these relations. One is the cause -oriented approach, where the 22 

bones and adjacent tissues are regarded as the cause of muscle deformation (Lloyd et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2025). In 23 

other words, the wrapping obstacles and via points should represent specific anatomical structures. The assumption 24 

is, with the right origin and insertion, so long as the anatomical obstructions are correctly reflected, the paths should 25 

deform at appropriate joint configurations as real muscles do; accordingly, the length– and moment arm–joint angle 26 

relations should be accurate. On the other hand, if length or moment arm data are available, they can be directly used 27 

to calibrate the muscle path as an eff ect -oriented approach (Chen et al., 2026; Livet et al., 2022; Modenese and Kohout, 28 

2020). That is, the length– and moment arm–joint angle relations are regarded as the e ff ect of muscle deformation. 29 

Regardless of whether the anatomical obstructions are reflected in the muscle path, as long as the key relations are 30 

replicated, the simulations should be accurate. 31 

In Chen et al. (2026), we demonstrated an automated method of e ff ect-oriented muscle path calibration: With 32 

artificial moment arm data from a reference model as the target value, a muscle path is configured to yield almost 33 

the same moment arm–joint angle relation as in the reference path. The process of parameter tuning for matching 34 

moment arms is formulated as a least-squares optimization problem, and our method features specifying the gradient 35 

of the cost function in its analytical form, which enables e ffi cient computation and rapid convergence compared with 36 

using the numerical gradient. 37 

Nevertheless, there are two issues remaining to be accounted for. First, although we succeeded in replicating the 38 

high-dimensional moment arm–joint angle relation using in silico data (Chen et al., 2026), it is crucial that the method 39 

remains e ff ective with experimental measurements, which are generally less comprehensive. The moment arm–joint 40 

angle relation is high-dimensional in the sense that a muscle may actuate multiple degrees of freedom (DoF): It may 41 

have multiple moment arms (McCullough et al., 2011; Vaarbakken et al., 2014; Wretenberg et al., 1996), and each 42 

moment arm is susceptible to the motion in each of the relevant DoFs (Delp et al., 1999; Hintermann et al., 1994; 43 

Wolfram et al., 2018). However, moment arm measurement is generally limited to the dominant DoF (e.g., ankle 44 

plantarflexion for the triceps surae or knee flexion for the hamstring), and the available moment arm–joint angle 45 

relation is mostly a curve (Chen and Franklin, 2025). Moreover, small muscles such as the gluteus minimus, the 46 

pectineus, and the gemelli have no moment arm measurement at all (Chen and Franklin, 2025). It is not entirely clear 47 

how our method would perform based only on the dominant moment arms, and alternative sources of data are needed 48 

to model muscles lacking moment arm measurements. Second, if moment arm is the only property to be calibrated, 49 

then length may not be well matched, and the resultant muscle path may be partially unrealistic in appearance. Notice 50 

that muscle moment arm is the partial derivative of muscle length with respect to the correspondent joint angle (An 51 

et al., 1984; Sherman et al., 2013), and matching muscle moment arm only guarantees matching the derivative of 52 

muscle length, not the property itself. 53 

With regard to these issues, it is important that morphological information is brought into the optimization prob-54 

lem; e.g., MR images of the muscles. Such information is useful in two perspectives: First, with 3-D mesh recon-55 

structed from medical images, we may confine the muscle path to pass through the mesh volume, making it appear 56 

anatomically realistic and have its length fall in a reasonable range. Second, for muscles with few or no moment 57 

arm measurements, the geometric features as well as the alignment relative to the bones are a valuable indication of 58 

how the moment arms should be (An et al., 1984; Herzog and Read, 1993). Recently, Modenese and Kohout (2020) 59 

proposed a method of automated path modeling based on musculotendon surface mesh: By extracting cross-sections 60 

from the 3-D mesh, one or multiple paths can be defined by connecting anchor points defined at the same relative 61 

positions in each cross-section (imagine the hollow pathways in a lotus root). Essentially, this method aims to match 62 

muscle length, and the paths modeled in this fashion will appear anatomically realistic; yet the moment arm–joint 63 

angle relations are not necessarily correct. Although they manipulate the kinematics of the anchor points to avoid 64 

aberrant path deformation, the related parameters are not tuned based on muscle length or moment arm. In other 65 

words, the visual resemblance of the path does not guarantee biomechanical accuracy across a vast range of motion 66 

(RoM). 67 

In this work, we develop a hybrid calibration based on muscle surface mesh and moment arm for path modeling 68

2.2. Composite objective optimization 96 

Muscle path modeling is formulated as a least-squares problem with the composite objective cost function 97

the bone meshes, the muscle meshes are also obtained in the fixation position. 131

2.5. Calibration 198 

With the analytical form of ∂J/∂ ppp, we used the nonlinear least-squares solver ( lsqnonlin ) in MATLAB to solve 199 

Eq. 1. Notice the outcome of Eq. 1 is a ff ected by the composition of ppp, which depends on how the path is structured. 200 

To find the best possible solution, the calibration was programmed to iterate through each path structure unless the 201 

optimization cost reaches a su ffi ciently small level (e.g., 0.03): For any muscle, we start with OI—the most simple 202 

structure—and incrementally increase structural complexity if necessary, progressing in the order of OVI, OV 1V2I, 203 

OCI, and OC 1C2I. Then, from all available solutions, the final solution is selected considering the trade-o ff between 204 

calibration accuracy and structural simplicity. More complex path structures incur higher cost penalties: 1.25 for OVI, 205 

1.5 for OV 1V2I, 2 for OCI, and 4 for OC 1C2I. 206 

The optimization was performed on a 2.9-GHz Intel Core i9 with 64 GB RAM and 14 CPU cores using parallel 207 

computing ( parfor ). For each path structure, we arranged parallel computing to optimize 14 initial points simulta-208 

neously, with the aim to reduce the risk of local minimum. See Appendix E for other configuration details and the 209 

generation of the initial points. 210 

For surface mesh calibration, we extracted four ellipses from each muscle mesh. For moment arm calibration, we 211 

selected appropriate measurements from the literature as the target values ( rtarget ) and determined muscle functions 212 

based on Gilroy et al. (2020) and Platzer (2015) for the target signs ( starget ). See Appendix F for the target values and 213 

target signs in detail. All calibration data can be found in Supplementary Materials. 214

4. Discussion 224 

4.1. Muscle with Su ffi cient Moment Arm Data 225 

As we reported in Chen and Franklin (2025), available moment arm measurements are mainly found in the sagittal 226

4.2. Muscle with Limited Moment Arm Data 236 

For muscles with more actuating DoFs, there are more practical issues. Take the example of the soleus, a major 237 

ankle plantarflexor with a role in ever-/inversion. In theory, its plantarflexion moment arm is dependent on both the 238 

dorsi-/plantarflexion and ever-/inversion angles; in other words, the relation is depicted by a surface. In our case, due to 239 

data scarcity, the calibration is based on only two curves, one with the dorsi-/plantarflexion angle as the variable (while 240 

ever-/inversion is neutral) and another with varying ever-/inversion (neutral dorsi-/plantarflexion); see Fig. 7 (top right, 241 

blue curves). Although these two “orthogonal” curves form the “backbone” of the 2-D relation, it is still possible that 242 

the plantarflexion moment arm is inaccurate in other joint configurations; e.g., when the ankle is dorsiflexed while 243 

inverted. To e ff ectively calibrate such a moment arm relation, the ideal data would be measurements spreading across 244 

the ankle RoM, or should at least cover joint configurations involved in the musculoskeletal simulation. A similar issue 245 

exists with the ever-/inversion moment arm of the soleus, where only three curves are available for the calibration of 246 

a surface (Fig. 7, bottom right). 247 

A key message here is that, as the moment arm–joint angle relation gets more complex, the amount of data required 248 

for calibration increases drastically. If the available data are limited, it is di ffi cult to tell if the uncalibrated part of the 249 

moment arm relation is accurate. In particular, when an uncalibrated configuration is far from the calibrated ones in 250 

the joint space, there is a higher risk of inaccuracy—in the worst case, the muscle may even function as an antagonist. 251

4.3. Muscle with No Moment Arm Data 266 

In the complete absence of moment arm data, the calibration must reply on the surface mesh and the designated 267 

muscle functions. If a muscle is simple (e.g., the gemelli) and the interested RoM is not too large, the moment arms 268 

are practically “clamped” in the correct signs, once the path is configured to go through the muscle mesh. That is, the 269 

muscle mesh provides enough information to determine the appropriate functions—notice this is partially the rationale 270 

behind the cause-oriented approach of path modeling—and the sign-based cost term could be somewhat redundant. 271 

Nevertheless, such mesh-based “clamping” is often not reliable. If a muscle is large, there will be multiple 272 

possibilities of path alignment, some of which could lead to abnormal moment arms. There is a similar issue if 273 

the interested RoM covers distant area in the joint space, such as when the joint is posed in extreme positions. Also, 274 

a muscle may actuate a motion in both directions, and it will be di ffi cult to tune the path for its moment arm sign to 275 

“flip” at certain joint configurations. In these cases, similar to the example of the gluteus maximus, it is necessary to 276 

establish additional rules for the moment arm signs. 277 

For example, according to Gilroy et al. (2020), the adductor longus functions as a hip flexor when hip flexion is 278 

below 70 ◦, otherwise it functions as an extensor. With this, we set it as an objective for moment arm calibration, and 279 

as shown in Fig. 9 (top right, gray surface), the function of the adductor longus switches from hip flexion to extension 280 

when hip flexion is around 70 ◦. Furthermore, considering the major function of the addutor longus, another objective 281 

is that it remains a hip adductor throughout the joint space, and the result is shown in Fig. 9 (bottom right). 282

4.4. Joint Kinematics 283 

The muscle moment arm rrr(qqq, ppp) depends not only on the path configuration ppp, but also on the joint kinemat-284 

ics qqq. Hence, before moment arm calibration is possible, the joint kinematics must be su ffi ciently accurate. More 285 

specifically, the rotation center and axis of each modeled motion should match those of the actual motion. 286 

In this work, we transplanted the joint kinematics from the Arnold model (Arnold et al., 2010) to the VHM skeleton 287 

(Andreassen et al., 2023): The rotation centers were manually determined by matching the relative positions in the 288 

bones, and the rotation axes were replicated. This approach works better for the hip, since the acetabulofemoral joint 289 

is a simple ball-and-socket joint. For the knee and the ankle, there are no specific bony landmarks that the rotations 290 

center around, and the axes may also di ff er across individuals. If the motions (or poses) are su ffi ciently recorded, 291 

the modeling of the joint kinematics is not particularly prone to error. Unfortunately, the images for the VHM were 292 

obtained in only one pose (Ackerman, 1998), so we cannot determine if (and how) the transplanted kinematics should 293 

be corrected. 294 

For the most part, the rotation axes from the Arnold model need no adjustment for the motions to appear natural 295 

and for the bone meshes not to overlap within the RoM. However, there is one exception where we must slightly 296 

adjust the rotation axis: Regardless where we place the rotation center, the VHM patella does not slide along the 297 

patellar surface of the VHM femur as the knee flexes. When moment arm calibration is based on this inappropriate 298 

kinematics, the paths for quadriceps insert not onto the patella, but the adjacent void space. Interestingly, when we 299 

adjusted the rotation axis so that the patella slides properly on the femur, the insertion points became anatomically 300 

realistic (Fig. 4, bottom left). 301 

When it comes to moment arm calibration, there is one kinematics-related problem that can be easily overlooked: 302 

The motions (or poses) used to collect moment arm data might not match those used for kinematic modeling. For 303 

instance, depending on how ankle ever-/inversion is defined, the motion under this name may have di ff erent rotation 304 

axes. In the default OpenSim model Gait2392 (Delp et al., 1990) or the Arnold model, the ever-/inversion axis is 305 

defined as some combination of all three cardinal axes according to Inman (1976). Nevertheless, when McCullough 306 

et al. (2011) and Hintermann et al. (1994) measured ankle moment arms, they mounted ankle specimens on a device 307 

to apply motion along each cardinal axis, which is no longer “natural.” So in these experiments, ever-/inversion is 308 

defined as the rotation along the coronal axis and essentially a di ff erent motion from that modeled in Gait2392 or the 309 

Arnold model. Simply put, the same joint configuration (e.g., 20 ◦ inversion) does not lead to the same joint position, 310 

and the moment arm will also di ff er. 311 

If we use the moment arm data from McCullough et al. (2011) and Hintermann et al. (1994) to calibrate a model 312 

with a “natural” ever-/inversion, paths such as the extensor hallucis longus may route in an unrealistic fashion—e.g., 313 

floating above the foot—to compensate the moment arm deficit caused by the kinematic mismatch. To accommodate 314 

these data, we modeled an extra set of ankle rotations, which match those used in the experiments. Figure 5 shows the 315 

paths for the long digital extensors and flexors, which run along the foot without much deviation. 316 

4.5. Identification of Attachment Sites 317 

For path modeling methods such as Modenese and Kohout (2020) and Wang et al. (2025), a prerequisite for 318 

full automation is automatic identification of the points (or areas) of origin and insertion. This would require the 319 

morphological information of the tendon, which is not always available since the tendon can be thin and hard to 320 

distinguish from the surrounding tissues (Andreassen et al., 2023). More importantly, if the mesh of a long tendon 321 

is missing, accurate path generation is no longer possible with Modenese and Kohout (2020) or Wang et al. (2025). 322 

With our hybrid calibration method, the absence of tendon mesh is not a serious problem, because path alignment is 323 

also guided by the moment arms. Take the example of the long digital extensors and flexors, although the meshes of 324 

their long tendons are not reconstructed from 3D MRI, the paths still take the appropriate turns in order to yield the 325 

correct moment arms (Fig. 5, bottom). 326 

Nonetheless, the lack of tendon mesh is not without an impact on our method. When a path is designated to go 327 

through the mesh of the muscle belly, its origin and insertions points are free to locate in a large space. Even with the 328 

objective to match moment arm values and signs, the resultant path may still have abnormal attachments, sometimes 329 

away from the bones. To make sure that the path attachments are anatomically reasonable, we made estimations based 330 

on the meshes of the muscle belly and the relevant bones (see Appendix A) and used them to bound the optimization. 331 

If a tendon is long, the absence of its surface mesh will make the estimation inaccurate, which then needs to be 332

4.6. Strengths and Limitations 348 

Distinct from recently proposed muscle path modeling methods that are automated (Livet et al., 2022; Lloyd et al., 349 

2021; Modenese and Kohout, 2020; Wang et al., 2025), our method is e ff ect-oriented based on the hybrid calibration 350 

of both muscle surface mesh and moment arm. The comparison of these methods is shown in Table 1. 351 

The method of Lloyd et al. (2021) is cause-oriented and is characterized by the direct computation of path wrap-352 

ping on arbitrary surfaces, which eliminates the need to manually define wrapping obstacles that approximate anatomi-353 

cal obstructions. However, because obstacle contact must be evaluated for a series of points along the path, the method 354 

is computationally demanding and may be less practical for large-scale simulations. A simplified cause-oriented ap-355 

proach is proposed by Wang et al. (2025): Instead of computing obstacle contact for a series of points along the path, 356 

a set of vertices is identified as wrapping points such that the path length is minimized. In essence, dynamic via points 357 

are used to approximate wrapping behavior and thereby reproduce muscle deformation. Nevertheless, the resulting 358 

path length varies discretely as wrapping points switch between mesh vertices, which prevents the derivation of an 359 

analytical expression for the moment arm. 360 

The method of Modenese and Kohout (2020) may be regarded as e ff ect-oriented in the sense that the generated 361 

path is expected to reproduce the muscle length, since the path is posed consistently with the musculotendon mesh. 362 

However, the calibration explicitly target the moment arm–joint angle relation, and empirical tuning is required for 363 

the path to deform reasonably across RoM. Moment arm–based calibration was presented in Livet et al. (2022). Their 364 

optimization framework is not gradient-specified, which limits computational e ffi ciency, and the performance was 365 

demonstrated with artificial datasets and relatively simple via point–based paths. It therefore remains unclear how 366

A key advantage of our method is the use of gradient-specified optimization, which improves calibration speed 369 

and accuracy compared with using the default numerical gradient (Chen et al., 2026). In a 6-DoF lower-limb model, 370

mated method did. Especially for muscles actuating multiple DoFs, the moment arm relations are high-dimensional, 375 

meaning that they are di ffi cult to visualize, let alone manually calibrate. 376 

In the spirit of automation, we made an e ff ort to remove as much manual work as possible, including the estimation 377 

of the origin and insertion points. As we previously emphasized, due to the lack of tendon surface mesh, it could be 378 

di ffi cult to make accurate estimations for certain attachment points using the method in Appendix A. Despite the 379 

possibilities to correct the estimations automatically, the solutions are format-wise less neat and the applicability 380 

is uncertain due to subject variability. In the future, we look to develop automatic identification of the origin and 381 

insertion points (or areas) with machine learning. This would not only simplify the preparation step in our method, 382 

but also make the methods of Modenese and Kohout (2020) and Wang et al. (2025) fully automatic. 383 

Apart from the morphological data, there are also issues related to the moment arm data. First, our model is 384 

based on the musculoskeletal geometry from the VHM, but the moment arm data for calibration come from other 385 

studies—the di ff erence in the subject group might induce errors for our hybrid calibration. Second, the moment 386 

arm data may also come from di ff erent experiments, each with its own subject group, method for measurement, and 387 

nuance in the definition of joint rotations. At last, many muscles have limited or even no data for calibration. These 388 

data-related issues are currently inevitable, and the paths in this study will need to be validated or recalibrated, if more 389 

comprehensive datasets are available in the future. 390 

On the topic of validation, this is unfortunately absent in our work. Ideally, validation of the paths would need 391 

muscle surface meshes or moment arms in additional joint configurations, but it can be technically challenging to 392 

collect either type of data across a large RoM. However, if we are to envision, the more practical goal would be to 393 

focus on the morphological data. Our modeling results of musculoskeletal geometry (Figs. 3–5) are based on images 394 

obtained in only one pose. If the meshes from a series of diverse poses are available, then the path may be configured 395 

to go through multiple sets of cross-sectional ellipses. The geometric features of the muscle will naturally be better 396 

reflected in a wider RoM. More importantly, the moment arms of such a path are somewhat reliable even without 397 

calibration. The rationale is that, by going through the muscle mesh in each joint configuration, the length of the path 398 

changes in a similar fashion as that of the muscle does. In other words, the derivative of muscle length with respective 399 

to joint angle (namely moment arm) is similar, and this is essentially the mechanism of the tendon excursion method 400 

for moment arm measurement (An et al., 1984; Maganaris et al., 2000). 401 

5. Conclusion 402 

A gradient-based optimization method is proposed for automated muscle path modeling and tested with experi-403 

mental data from literature. As an e ff ect-oriented approach, this method is based on the hybrid calibration of muscle 404 

surface mesh and moment arm, and the resultant paths are both anatomically realistic and biomechanically accurate. 405 

With its speed and accuracy, our automated framework is suitable for large-scale subject-specific applications. 406

Appendix B. Ellipses for Muscle Cross-Sections 

With the entry and exit points ( uuuM,en and uuuM,ex in Appendix A) as boundary constraints, we apply the method of Dong et al. (2005) to construct a harmonic scalar field h(·) over the muscle mesh. Depending on the relative position between the entry and exit points, each of the NM vertices is assigned a scalar value h(uuuM,n) ∈ [0 , 1], forming a series of field isolines. Similar to Modenese and Kohout (2020), we treat these isolines as the contours of muscle cross-sections, but we do not thread the path through any certain location (e.g., the geometric center) on multiple cross-sections. Instead, we only expect them to be intersected by the path, so that the path has enough room to be tuned for moment arm calibration. For computational convenience, we simplify each cross-section as an ellipse, which is geometrically similar but computationally easier to implement. Appendix D. Cost Normalization 

The optimization problem we formulate for muscle path modeling is multi-term objective: Each of the three objectives targets at a di ff erent property, and the cost terms need to be normalized so that their contributions can be meaningfully combined. The first key of normalization is to ensure that the cost terms have the same units. In Eq. 2, the cost term for muscle surface mesh JSM is based on the quantity of intersected ellipses, which is dimensionless. When matching moment arm values, JvMA in Eq. 7 is based on moment arm length, with the unit of mm. For this, the di ff erence of model and measured moment arms is divided by a tolerance value, also with the unit of mm—this ratio is dimensionless. When matching moment arm signs, JsMA in Eq. 8 is based on the quantity of matched signs, which is already dimensionless and does not require normalization. Then, the magnitude of each cost term needs to be scaled as an approach to balance the importance of each objective. In Eq. 2, we assign a factor of 1/2Nell so that the maximum of JSM is 1 /2 when none of the Nell ellipses are intersected. This sets a baseline for the “worst-case scenario” in surface mesh calibration, and the other two cost terms may be weighted with respect to this baseline. In this study, we intend to treat surface mesh and moment arm as equally important. In other words, the sum of JvMA and JsMA should be scaled to 1 /2 when the value-based calibration and the sign-based calibration are both in their “worst-case scenarios.” In the sign-based moment arm calibration, the “worst-case scenario” is simply when none of the signs match, and the cost term will reach its maximum

Appendix E. Initialization and Configuration for Optimization 

In order for the optimization to proceed su ffi ciently fast without compromising accuracy, we follow the configu-ration in our recent work (Chen et al., 2026): 

• trust-region-reflective with SpecifyObjectiveGradient ;

• 10 −4 for StepTolerance , and 2 .5 × 10 −16 for FunctionTolerance and OptimalityTolerance ;

• 10 2 for MaxIterations , and Inf for MaxFunctionEvaluations .When ppp was initialized for Eq. 1 and later optimized, the locations of the origin and insertion points were bounded within a uniformly distributed range of ±10 mm from ˜ cccB1 and ˜ cccB2 (see Appendix A), respectively. This is because ˜ cccB1 

and ˜ cccB2 correspond well to the anatomical landmarks of the tendinous attachments, and we include this constraint to speed up the calibration. Importantly, without this constraint, the origin and insertion points might be tuned away from the bones. Although this does not necessarily interfere with moment arm calibration, the path will appear anatomically unrealistic. For other parameters, there are no constraints during the optimization. 20
