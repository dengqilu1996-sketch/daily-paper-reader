Abstract 13 

Running can improve mood, but the proximal processes linking exercise-induced bodily 14 

changes to subjective affective changes remain unclear. Interoception, the sensing and 15 

interpretation of internal bodily signals, is central to how bodily states are integrated into 16 

emotional experience and may therefore link exercise-induced bodily changes to mood. Here 17 

we tested whether interoception links acute running to mood improvement. Twenty-seven 18 

healthy young adults completed a crossover experiment consisting of a 15-min moderate-19 

intensity treadmill running condition and a seated rest condition, each performed on separate 20 

days. Interoceptive accuracy was assessed using heartbeat counting and tapping tasks. 21 

Present-moment interoceptive sensibility was monitored using a state-adapted Body 22 

Perception Questionnaire, and mood state was monitored using the Profile of Mood States. 23 

Compared with seated rest, running reduced total mood disturbance and tension–anxiety, 24 

increased vigor–activity, and enhanced heartbeat-signal discrimination and interoceptive 25 

sensibility. Running-related increases in interoceptive sensibility were associated with greater 26 

reductions in total mood disturbance and tension–anxiety. Mediation analyses further 27 

indicated that changes in interoceptive sensibility partially mediated the effect of running on 28 

total mood disturbance, with weaker evidence for tension–anxiety. These findings suggest 29 

that enhanced interoceptive sensibility may represent one proximal process through which 30 

acute running improves mood states. 31

Introduction 34 

Acute aerobic exercise has been widely studied as a strategy for improving mood, 35 

particularly by reducing anxiety. Previous studies have reported that even a single bout of 36 

exercise can significantly reduce state anxiety relative to pre-exercise levels (Ensari et al., 37 

2015). Recent meta-analytic evidence has also shown that moderate- to high-intensity 38 

exercise is associated with reduced anxiety in healthy adults (Ensari et al., 2015; Tian et al., 39 

2024; Weinstein et al., 2024). However, not all individuals benefit from acute exercise to the 40 

same extent (Weinstein et al., 2024), suggesting that the anxiolytic effects of exercise may 41 

not be uniform across individuals. Therefore, to establish exercise more firmly as a strategy 42 

for promoting mental health and reducing anxiety-related mood disturbances, it is necessary 43 

to clarify the proximal mechanisms underlying exercise-induced reductions in anxiety, as well 44 

as the factors that contribute to individual differences in these effects. 45 

A growing body of physiological evidence has identified several neurological 46 

mechanisms through which exercise may improve mood states, including the regulation of 47 

monoaminergic systems and the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis, the release of 48 

endogenous opioids and endocannabinoids, and the expression of neurotrophic factors such 49 

as brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) (Basso & Suzuki, 2017; Desai et al., 2022; 50 

Heyman et al., 2012; Kuwamizu et al., 2023). Psychological mechanisms, such as enhanced 51 

self-efficacy and attentional distraction, have also been proposed (Anderson & Shivakumar, 52 

2013; Salmon, 2001). However, these mechanisms explain only certain aspects of the 53 

complex responses induced by exercise, and it remains difficult to account for, within a single 54 

pathway, the proximal process by which exercise-induced changes in bodily states are 55 

perceived, evaluated, and ultimately linked to subjective changes in mood states (Fang et al., 56 

2025). These considerations raise the possibility that individual differences in the mood-57 

enhancing effects of exercise may depend not only on the magnitude of physiological 58 

changes induced by exercise, but also on how these bodily changes are perceived, 59 

interpreted, and integrated into subjective emotional experience. 60

Accordingly, the present study focused on interoception. Interoception is defined as the 61 

process by which the nervous system senses, interprets, and integrates internal signals from 62 

bodily systems, such as the cardiovascular, respiratory, and gastrointestinal systems, to form 63 

representations of bodily states (Craig, 2003; Khalsa et al., 2018). From a neuroanatomical 64 

perspective, interoceptive processing is supported by a network centered on the insular 65 

cortex (insula) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), both of which are deeply involved in 66 

emotion generation and subjective emotional experience (Craig, 2003, 2009; Terasawa et al., 67 

2013). In particular, the integration of bodily signals in the insula has been associated with 68 

subjective anxiety (Critchley et al., 2004; Terasawa et al., 2013), suggesting that 69 

interoception may constitute a fundamental mechanism linking bodily states to emotional 70 

experience. 71 

Acute aerobic exercise produces salient changes in internal bodily states, including 72 

increases in heart rate (HR) and respiration. Such exercise-induced bodily signals are 73 

thought to engage interoceptive pathways involving afferent feedback from the body and 74 

brain regions such as the insula and ACC, thereby influencing cognitive, motivational, and 75 

emotional processes (McMorris, 2021). Such changes may make bodily signals more 76 

noticeable and alter how individuals perceive and attend to their bodily states, thereby 77 

leading to changes in interoceptive processing (Smith et al., 2021). Importantly, anxiety has 78 

been linked to altered interoceptive processing, particularly heightened attention to bodily 79 

sensations, negative evaluation of these sensations, and difficulties describing bodily signals 80 

and emotions (Paulus & Stein, 2010; Clemente et al., 2024). Intentional activities such as 81 

exercise may provide a context in which bodily signals are generated in a relatively clear and 82 

predictable manner. By increasing the salience and interpretability of bodily signals, exercise 83 

may alter interoception and facilitate the integration of bodily changes into subjective 84 

emotional experience, thereby contributing to changes in mood states (Critchley & Garfinkel, 85 

2017; Khalsa et al., 2018). Indeed, previous studies have suggested that acute physical 86 

activity can improve performance on heartbeat perception tasks (e.g., Wallman-Jones et al., 87 

2022), possibly by increasing the salience of cardiac signals (Jones & Hollandsworth, 1981; 88 

Montgomery et al., 1984). However, it remains unclear whether exercise-induced changes in 89 

interoception are associated with changes in mood states, and whether interoception can 90 

account for individual differences in mood improvement after acute exercise. In particular, 91

few studies have tested the exercise–interoception–mood pathway using a mediation model. 92 

Based on this background, the present study examined the effects of acute moderate-93 

intensity aerobic exercise, specifically treadmill running, on mood states and interoception in 94 

healthy university students. Moderate-intensity exercise is commonly recommended for 95 

health promotion (Pescatello, 2014), and the present study operationalized moderate-96 

intensity running as 50% of estimated peak oxygen uptake (V̇O ₂peak ). Treadmill running was 97 

selected because recent evidence suggests that the effects of exercise on mood may differ 98 

depending on exercise modality. For example, several studies have reported that cycling 99 

exercise increases arousal, whereas its effects on pleasant mood are relatively limited (Byun 100 

et al., 2014; Kujach et al., 2020; Yamazaki et al., 2018). In contrast, running has been shown 101 

to increase both arousal and pleasant mood (Damrongthai et al., 2021, 2024). Although the 102 

mechanisms underlying these modality-specific effects remain unclear, one possible 103 

explanation is that running-specific mechanical stimulation, such as head acceleration, may 104 

contribute to changes in mood and cognition (Damrongthai, 2024; Ryu et al., 2020). 105 

Therefore, the present study hypothesized that changes in interoception induced by 106 

moderate-intensity running would contribute to reductions in negative mood states and 107 

broader improvements in mood. We tested this hypothesis using a quasi-randomized 108 

crossover design and examined whether running-related changes in interoception were 109 

associated with, and statistically accounted for, changes in mood states. 110 

Results 111 

We used a quasi-randomized crossover design to test whether acute moderate-intensity 112 

running alters interoception and mood states. Twenty-seven healthy young adults completed 113 

both a 15-min moderate-intensity treadmill running condition (RUN) and a seated resting 114 

control condition (CTL) on separate days, with interoceptive and mood measures assessed 115 

immediately before and after each condition (Fig. 1). 116

Interoceptive sensibility consistent with an indirect pathway to mood improvement 259 

We examined whether changes in present-moment interoceptive sensibility statistically 260 

accounted for the effect of moderate-intensity running on mood improvement. Because 261 

changes in the mediator and outcome were assessed over the same pre–post interval, these 262 

mediation analyses were interpreted as preliminary and as testing whether the data were 263 

statistically consistent with an indirect pathway. Mediation analyses showed that changes in 264 

BPQ score partially mediated the effect of running on reductions in TMD score (ACME = 265 

−1.36, 95% CI [−3.04, −0.01], p = .04; ADE = −2.33, 95% CI [−4.11, −0.54], p = .01; total 266 

effect = −3.69, 95% CI [−5.82, −1.56], p = .002; proportion mediated = 0.36, 95% CI [0.03, 267 

0.78], p = .04(Fig. 4C,D). These findings suggest that increased present-moment 268 

interoceptive sensibility may partly account for the reduction in TMD after running. 269 

We also conducted an exploratory mediation analysis for TA, given its significant 270 

association with running-related changes in BPQ score. Changes in BPQ score showed a 271 

significant indirect association between running and reductions in TA score (ACME = −0.36, 272 

95% CI [−0.90, −0.01], p = .046). However, the ADE was not significant (ADE = −0.60, 95% 273 

CI [−1.32, 0.13], p = .12), and the proportion mediated did not reach conventional statistical 274 

significance (proportion mediated = 0.35, 95% CI [−0.002, 1.31], p = .052)(Fig. 4E). Thus, 275 

the mediation result for TA should be interpreted as modest and exploratory. 276 

Discussion 277 

The present study examined whether changes in interoception contribute to the mood-278 

enhancing and anxiety-reducing effects of acute moderate-intensity running. The main 279 

findings were as follows. First, consistent with previous evidence that acute aerobic exercise 280 

improves mood states (Ensari et al., 2015; Damrongthai et al., 2021, 2024), moderate-281 

intensity running reduced TMD and TA and increased VA compared with the seated resting 282

indexed by HTT d′, and increased present-moment interoceptive sensibility, as indexed by 284 

BPQ scores. Third, running-related increases in BPQ scores were associated with reductions 285 

in POMS TMD and TA scores. Finally, mediation analyses provided preliminary evidence 286 

statistically consistent with an indirect pathway from running to reduced TMD through 287 

increased BPQ scores, with weaker evidence for a similar pattern for TA. These findings 288 

suggest that subjective changes in interoceptive sensibility may be one candidate proximal 289 

process linking acute moderate-intensity running to mood improvement. 290 

The present study found that moderate-intensity running led to selectively increased 291 

HTT d′, whereas the HCT score and the HTT-derived md and mSI did not show significant 292 

condition × time interactions. This pattern suggests that running did not uniformly enhance 293 

interoceptive accuracy across all indices. Rather, the increase in HTT d′ may reflect greater 294 

discriminability of heartbeat-related signals under conditions of elevated cardiac activity, 295 

when cardiac signals were likely more salient. Thus, this finding should be interpreted as an 296 

exercise-related increase in heartbeat-signal discriminability rather than as definitive 297 

evidence of a generalized improvement in interoceptive accuracy. This interpretation is 298 

consistent with earlier evidence that heartbeat discrimination can improve when cardiac 299 

activity is increased by exercise (Jones & Hollandsworth, 1981) and with the view that 300 

physical activity can acutely modulate interoceptive processing by increasing visceral-301 

afferent input and the salience of bodily signals (Wallman-Jones et al., 2021). In contrast, the 302 

absence of a significant change in HCT score appears to differ from Wallman-Jones et al. 303 

(2022), who reported improved HCT performance after acute cycling exercise. However, their 304 

protocol involved high-intensity cycling that exceeded the anaerobic threshold during the final 305 

phase, whereas the present study used moderate-intensity running. Because exercise 306 

intensity may influence the salience of interoceptive cues, differences in intensity and 307 

modality may partly explain the discrepancy in HCT findings. 308 

This selective pattern is also consistent with the idea that different interoceptive accuracy 309 

indices capture partly distinct aspects of cardiac interoception. HCT performance may be 310 

influenced by time estimation, prior knowledge about heart rate, and estimation strategies 311 

(Desmedt et al., 2018; Murphy et al., 2018; Ring et al., 2015). In contrast, HTT-derived indices 312

A key finding of the present study is that running-related increases in BPQ scores were 321 

associated with improvements in mood states and statistically accounted for part of the effect 322 

of moderate-intensity running on TMD. Although running also increased HTT d ′ , the 323 

mediation results suggest that changes in subjective interoceptive sensibility, rather than 324 

heartbeat perception accuracy alone, were more closely related to reductions in TMD. A 325 

similar, although more modest and exploratory, pattern was observed for TA. These findings 326 

are consistent with the possibility of an exercise–interoception–mood pathway, in which 327 

exercise-induced changes in subjective interoceptive sensibility contribute to mood 328 

improvement. Physical activity has been proposed to increase visceral-afferent input and the 329 

salience of bodily signals entering the interoceptive system, thereby influencing how bodily 330 

changes are processed and integrated with cognitive, motivational, and affective responses 331 

(Craig, 2003; Critchley & Garfinkel, 2017; McMorris, 2021; Wallman-Jones et al., 2021). Thus, 332 

acute running may have produced salient bodily changes, including increased HR and 333 

respiration, and individuals who became more aware of these changes may have been better 334 

able to integrate them into subjective emotional experiences. This interpretation is also 335 

consistent with interoceptive exposure accounts, which suggest that salient physiological 336 

arousal induced by physical activity may increase familiarity with bodily arousal cues and 337 

reduce catastrophic or negative interpretations of these sensations (Sabourin et al., 2015). 338 

Accordingly, subjective interoceptive sensibility may be one proximal process through which 339 

moderate-intensity running contributes to improvements in mood states, although it likely 340 

operates alongside other physiological and psychological mechanisms, such as 341 

neurochemical changes, autonomic regulation, self-efficacy, and attentional distraction 342 

(Basso & Suzuki, 2017; Salmon, 2001). 343

The present findings may also be considered in relation to predictive processing or active 344 

inference accounts of interoception and emotion, although the present study was not 345 

designed to directly test these frameworks. From this perspective, emotional experience is 346 

not determined solely by peripheral bodily changes themselves but by how the brain 347 

interprets, weighs, and updates internal bodily signals (Barrett & Simmons, 2015; Seth, 2013). 348 

Moderate-intensity running produces clear and salient changes in bodily states, such as 349 

increased HR and respiration. Importantly, these bodily changes occur in a context in which 350 

their causes are identifiable and predictable, namely the physiological demands of physical 351 

activity. One speculative interpretation is that exercise-induced bodily signals may provide 352 

relatively clear interoceptive evidence for updating the subjective representation of bodily 353 

states, rather than remaining ambiguous internal sensations. This framework may help 354 

explain why changes in BPQ scores, but not all indices of interoceptive accuracy, were 355 

associated with mood improvement: what may be critical is not simply detecting cardiac 356 

signals more accurately, but becoming subjectively aware of bodily changes in a context in 357 

which those signals can be meaningfully interpreted and integrated into emotional experience 358 

(Critchley & Garfinkel, 2017; Khalsa et al., 2018). However, because the present study did 359 

not directly measure bodily appraisal, neural prediction errors, precision weighting, or brain 360 

activity, this interpretation should be regarded as a theoretical account rather than direct 361 

evidence for predictive coding mechanisms (Owens et al., 2018; Seth, 2013). 362 

In conclusion, a single bout of moderate-intensity treadmill running improved mood 363 

states and enhanced aspects of interoception in healthy young adults. Specifically, running 364 

reduced TMD and TA, increased VA, and increased heartbeat-signal discrimination and 365 

present-moment interoceptive sensibility. Importantly, running-related increases in 366 

interoceptive sensibility were associated with reductions in mood disturbance and tension– 367 

anxiety, and were statistically consistent with an indirect pathway linking running to reduced 368 

TMD. These findings suggest that enhanced interoceptive sensibility may represent one 369 

proximal process through which acute running improves mood. Together, these findings 370 

support the exercise–interoception–mood pathway and suggest that running engages a 371 

dynamic body–mind connection linking exercise-induced bodily changes to subjective mood 372 

states. 373

Methods 374 

Participants 375 

Thirty young adults participated in this study. All participants were native Japanese 376 

speakers, right-handed, and had normal or corrected-to-normal vision and normal color 377 

vision. No participants were taking medication affecting the central nervous and/or endocrine 378 

systems. Participants were excluded if they had severe depressive symptoms, defined as a 379 

Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) score of 29 or higher (n = 1). In addition, two 380 

participants were excluded because they did not follow the task instructions correctly. 381 

Consequently, 27 participants (14 female) were included in the final analysis. The 382 

characteristics of participants are presented in Table 1. 383 

This sample size was considered acceptable for the following reasons. Based on 384 

Green’s (1991) guideline, at least 31 observations are required for multiple regression 385 

analysis with three predictors when assuming a large effect size (f² > 0.35), as used in causal 386 

mediation analyses. In the present within-subject crossover design, 27 participants 387 

completed both the RUN and CTL conditions, yielding 54 condition-level observations for the 388 

mediation analyses. 389 

This study was approved by the Institutional Ethics Committee of the Graduate School 390 

of Social and Industrial Science and Technology, Tokushima University (approval number 391 

367), and the protocol was conducted in accordance with the latest version of the Declaration 392 

of Helsinki. Complete information about the study was provided to participants before written 393 

informed consent was obtained from all participants. 394

Study design and procedure 399 

The study consisted of a baseline assessment day and two main experimental sessions. 400 

On Day 1, participants completed the single-stage submaximal treadmill walking test 401 

(SSTWT) to estimate maximal oxygen uptake and determine the individualized treadmill 402 

workload corresponding to 50% of estimated peak oxygen uptake (V̇O 2peak ) for the RUN 403 

condition. On the same day, participants also completed the Body Perception Questionnaire– 404 

Body Awareness Very Short Form, Japanese version (BPQ-BAVSF-J), the Beck Depression 405 

Inventory-II (BDI-II), and anthropometric measurements of height and weight. 406 

The main experiment consisted of two conditions: the moderate-intensity running 407 

condition and a seated resting control condition. All participants completed each condition on 408 

separate days after the baseline session, using a quasi-randomized crossover design. The 409 

order of the RUN and CTL conditions was quasi-randomized across participants to reduce 410 

potential order effects. The two main experimental visits were separated by at least 48 h. 411 

To control for external influences, participants were instructed to refrain from exercise 412

Single-stage submaximal treadmill walking test (SSTWT) 416 

Participants performed an SSTWT to estimate V̇O 2peak and to determine an 417 

individualized moderate-intensity exercise workload corresponding to 50% intensity, in 418 

accordance with the guidelines of the American College of Sports Medicine (Ebbeling et al., 419 

1991). The SSTWT, originally developed by Ebbeling and colleagues, is a widely used 420 

method for estimating V̇O 2peak (Chu et al., 2017), as previous studies have demonstrated a 421 

strong association between predicted and directly measured V̇O 2peak values ( r = 0.96, with a 422 

multiple correlation 0.86)(Noonan & Dean, 2000). The test consisted of two consecutive 4-423 

minute stages. During the initial stage, each participant walked on a treadmill at a self-424 

selected speed ranging from 2.0 to 4.5 mph with no incline, serving as a warm-up period. 425 

Then, treadmill speed was gradually adjusted until the participant’s heart rate reached 426 

approximately 60–70% of their age-predicted maximal heart rate (HR max ), calculated using 427 

the equation 207 − (0.67 × age). Throughout the second 4-min stage, participants were 428 

required to maintain the speed they had established during the first stage while the treadmill 429 

was raised to a 5% grade. Steady-state heart rate (SSHR) was calculated as the average 430 

heart rate during the last 2 min of the second stage and, together with age, sex, and final 431 

treadmill speed, was used to estimate V̇O ₂peak using the following equation: V̇O 2peak 432 

(mL/kg/min) = 15.1 + 21.8 × speed (mph) − 0.327 × SSHR (bpm) − 0.263 × speed × age 433 

(years) + 0.00504 × SSHR × age + 5.98 × sex (Ebbeling et al., 1991), where speed indicates 434 

the final treadmill speed in mph and sex is coded as 0 for female and 1 for male. Then, based 435 

on the ACSM metabolic equation for treadmill running (Glass et al., 2007), the treadmill 436 

running speed corresponding to 50% of the estimated V̇O 2peak at 0% incline was individually 437 

determined for each participant (Table 1). This intensity corresponds to the moderate-438 

intensity range according to the ACSM guidelines for exercise prescription (Riebe et al., 439 

2018). 440

Interoceptive accuracy tasks 454 

Heartbeat counting task (HCT) 455 

The HCT used in the present study was based on the Mental Tracking Method originally 456 

proposed by Schandry (1981). Participants completed three trials with intervals of 25, 35, 457 

and 45 s, presented in a randomized order across participants. A 20-s rest period was 458 

provided between trials. The beginning and end of each trial were signaled by the 459 

appearance and disappearance of a fixation point presented at the center of the screen. 460 

Participants were instructed to remain seated quietly and to count their own heartbeats 461 

as accurately as possible during each interval, even if they were uncertain about their 462 

responses. After each trial, they reported the number of counted heartbeats by entering the 463 

value on an on-screen numeric keypad using a mouse, with no time limit for responding. 464 

Before the experimental trials, one 10-s practice trial was administered to ensure that 465 

participants understood the task instructions and became familiar with the procedure. 466 

Heartbeat tapping task (HTT) 467 

Although the HCT is widely used to assess interoceptive accuracy, its performance can 468 

be influenced by non-interoceptive factors, such as prior knowledge or beliefs about one’s 469 

own heart rate and estimation strategies, rather than solely by the perception of actual 470 

heartbeat sensations (Murphy et al., 2018). Therefore, in addition to the HCT, the present 471 

study included an HTT, which requires participants to tap in synchrony with their perceived 472

A fixation point was continuously presented at the center of the screen throughout each 477

Interoceptive sensibility 485 

The BPQ was originally developed in English as a measure of bodily awareness (Kolacz, 486 

Holmes, & Porges, 2018; Porges, 1993). In the present study, we used the validated 487 

Japanese version of the BPQ-BAVSF (Kobayashi et al., 2021), a concise 12-item scale 488 

designed to assess individual differences in sensitivity to internal bodily sensations and 489 

physiological functions. The items cover bodily experiences related to respiration, 490 

cardiovascular activity, muscle tension, gastrointestinal processes, and other internal bodily 491 

cues. Responses are rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from “Never” to “Always.” Total 492 

scores range from 12 to 60, with higher scores indicating greater sensitivity to bodily 493 

sensations. For clarity and brevity, scores derived from the BPQ-BAVSF-J are referred to as 494 

“BPQ scores” throughout this study. 495 

The standard BPQ-BAVSF-J is administered in a trait-based format, asking respondents 496 

to rate their awareness of bodily sensations during most situations. However, because the 497 

present study aimed to examine state-related changes in interoceptive sensibility before and 498 

after the experimental sessions, the wording of the instructions was modified with permission 499 

from the first author of the Japanese version (Kobayashi et al., 2021). In the modified version 500 

used in this study, participants were asked to rate each item according to their present-501 

moment bodily experience at the time of assessment. For example, an item such as “My 502 

mouth being dry” was rated with reference to the participant’s current bodily state. This 503

Mood states 517 

Mood states were assessed using the Japanese version of the Profile of Mood States 518 

2nd Edition–Adult Short Form (POMS 2-A Short; Heuchert & McNair, 2012; Yokoyama & 519 

Watanabe, 2015). The POMS 2-A Short is a 35-item self-report instrument designed to 520 

evaluate transient mood states. It yields scores on seven mood dimensions: anger–hostility 521 

(AH), confusion–bewilderment (CB), depression–dejection (DD), fatigue–inertia (FI), 522 

tension–anxiety (TA), vigor–activity (VA), and friendliness (F). Participants rated each item 523 

on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 0 (“not at all”) to 4 (“extremely”). 524 

Higher scores on the VA and F subscales reflect more positive mood states, whereas 525 

higher scores on the remaining five subscales indicate greater severity of negative mood 526 

symptoms. In addition to the subscale scores, an overall index of mood disturbance, the TMD 527 

score, was computed. The TMD score was calculated as AH + CB + DD + FI + TA − VA, with 528 

higher values representing greater TMD. 529

Perceptual measures 547 

The HCT score was calculated across the three trials using the following formula: HCT 548 

score = 1/3 Σ (1 − |actual number of heartbeats − self-reported number of heartbeats| / actual 549 

number of heartbeats). Higher scores were interpreted as indicating greater interoceptive 550 

accuracy. 551 

For the HTT, perceptual performance was evaluated using three indices adapted from 552 

the general framework proposed by Fittipaldi et al. (2020): mean distance (md), modified 553 

Schandry index (mSI), and d′. The md index quantifies the discrepancy between cardiac 554 

frequency and tapping frequency across overlapping time windows, with lower values 555 

indicating closer correspondence between heartbeat rhythm and tapping rhythm. The mSI 556 

quantifies the correspondence between the total number of detected R-peaks and the total 557 

number of taps, with higher values indicating greater response-count agreement. The d′558 

index, based on Signal Detection Theory, quantifies participants’ sensitivity in discriminating 559 

heartbeat-related signals from non-signal responses, with higher values indicating greater 560 

sensitivity. Detailed calculation procedures for these indices are provided in Supplementary 561

Statistical analyses were performed in R version 4.3.2. Interoceptive accuracy, 564 

interoceptive sensibility, and mood scores were analyzed using repeated-measures two-way 565 

ANOVAs with condition (RUN, CTL) and time (Pre, Post) as within-subject factors. These 566 

analyses were conducted using the afex package (version 1.4.1), with Type III sums of 567 

squares and partial eta squared ( ηp ²) reported as the effect size. When significant condition 568 

× time interactions were observed, Bonferroni-corrected post hoc comparisons were 569 

conducted using the emmeans package (version 1.11.2). In addition, for complementary 570 

purposes, change scores for each condition were calculated as Post − Pre, and paired t-tests 571 

were used to compare ΔRUN and ΔCTL. To examine the association between running-572 

related changes in interoception and mood, contrast scores representing running-related 573 

effects were computed as ΔRUN − ΔCTL, and Pearson’s correlation analyses were 574 

conducted between contrast scores for interoceptive measures and those for POMS 575 

outcomes. All statistical tests were two-sided, and statistical significance was set a priori at 576 

p < .05. 577 

Mediation analyses were performed using the mediation package (version 4.5.0; Tingley 578 

et al., 2014) to examine whether running-related changes in interoceptive accuracy or 579 

present-moment interoceptive sensibility were statistically consistent with an indirect 580 

pathway linking running to mood improvement. Because the present study used a within-581 

subject crossover design, mediation analyses were conducted using fixed-effects linear 582 

models with participant ID included as a fixed effect. For each participant and condition, 583 

condition-specific change scores were calculated as Post − Pre. The exposure variable was 584 

condition, coded as 1 for RUN and 0 for CTL. In each mediation model, the mediator was 585 

defined as the condition-specific change in an interoceptive measure, and the outcome was 586 

defined as the condition-specific change in the corresponding mood score. The mediator 587 

model included condition and participant fixed effects, and the outcome model included 588 

condition, the mediator, and participant fixed effects. The total effect, average direct effect 589 

(ADE), average causal mediation effect (ACME), and proportion mediated were estimated 590 

using quasi-Bayesian confidence intervals based on 5,000 simulations. Mediation analyses 591 

were conducted for TMD score as the primary mood outcome. Additional exploratory 592 

mediation analyses were performed for POMS subscales that showed significant correlations 593 

with running-related changes in interoceptive measures. Because changes in the mediator 594
