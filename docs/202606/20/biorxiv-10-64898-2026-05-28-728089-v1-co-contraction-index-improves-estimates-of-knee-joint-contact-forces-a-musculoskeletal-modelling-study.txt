## ABSTRACT 

Healthy individuals are hypothesized to adopt muscle coordination strategies that minimize energy expenditure. However, under pathological conditions, these patterns often change, as patients adopt alternative strategies to enhance stability. In musculoskeletal modeling, such changes in muscle coordination are often not accounted for when estimating internal quantities such as knee joint contact forces. To address this, we adapted the objective function of a muscle redundancy solver to inform muscle activations from electromyography measurements, minimizing errors in both muscle activations and co-contraction levels. The resulting estimates of knee joint contact force were compared with in vivo measurements across three activities. The co-contraction index-informed approach achieved the lowest root-mean-square-error of 0.31 body weight, averaged across all subjects and activities, improving the results of the minimum activation solver by 7% body weight. Notably, root-mean-squared-error increased with the level of co-contraction with the minimum activation approach ( β = 1.55 , 95% confidence interval [0.79, 2.30]), whereas the co-contraction index-informed approach remained less sensitive ( β = 0.78 , 95% confidence interval [0.14, 1.43]). These findings suggest that objective functions based on minimum muscle activation may be insufficient to accurately estimate knee joint forces in individuals with altered muscle coordination. Incorporating co-contraction information is therefore essential to capture subject-specific adaptations. 

## Introduction 1

Non-pharmacological rehabilitation programs for patients with knee osteoarthritis (KOA) primarily focus on therapeutic 2

exercises to increase muscle strength, improve gait function, and enhance balance 1. Currently, these exercise recommendations 3

are largely based on expert opinion, and there is only limited evidence supporting specific movement programs 2. Therefore, one 4

question that often arises for both physicians and patients is the appropriate amount and intensity of exercise that is safe and 5

beneficial for the patient 3. While exercise interventions have been shown to reduce pain and improve both functional outcomes 6

and quality of life compared to no intervention 4, 5 , MRI findings indicate no corresponding reduction in joint inflammation 4.7

Excessive knee joint loading is closely associated with cartilage inflammation, which is thought to be a factor in the progression 8

of KOA 6–8 . This suggests that conventional training protocols are not sufficiently focused on reducing knee joint loading during 9

daily activities. 10 

Gait retraining has been reported as an effective approach to redistributing or reducing knee joint forces 9–11 . Strategies for 11 

adapting walking patterns described in the literature include altering the foot progression angle 10, 12 , using a medial thrust 12 

gait 13 , or using trunk lean 13 . Furthermore, adapting muscle coordination strategies, such as activating the soleus rather than 13 

the gastrocnemius during the push-off phase of walking, has been reported to decrease knee joint contact forces at the second 14 

peak 9. These studies, therefore, indicate how essential it is to track the subject’s knee joint loading to evaluate the effect of 15 

using gait retraining during rehabilitation and to assess the progression of KOA. Since direct measurements of knee joint forces 16 

in an intact knee are not feasible, musculoskeletal (MSK) modeling is a viable approach for estimating knee joint contact forces 17 

during gait retraining. 18 

One key challenge in MSK modeling is solving the muscle redundancy problem. This problem arises because the human 19 

musculoskeletal system has more muscles than the number of degrees of freedom required to produce a specific movement. 20 

Consequently, different muscle activation strategies can produce the same motion but result in varying internal quantities, such 21

Healthy adults 22 

are thought to select a neuromuscular activation strategy that minimizes metabolic cost during tasks such as walking 14 . This 23 

strategy is implemented by computing the muscle activation pattern that minimizes the sum of squared muscle activations 15 .24 

However, KOA patients may optimize muscle coordination strategies, for example, to reduce pain or enhance joint stability, 25 

thereby reducing the risk of falls or trips 16 . This adaptation in muscle coordination strategy is supported by previous findings 26 

indicating greater muscle co-contraction in some individuals with early-stage KOA than in healthy controls 17, 18 . Increases 27 

in muscle co-contraction, commonly quantified using a co-contraction index (CCI), elevates knee joint loading and should 28 

therefore be considered in knee joint contact force estimations of KOA patients 19, 20 . To account for differences in the muscle 29 

coordination strategy, the simulation framework must be informed of the patient-specific differences in the muscle activation 30 

pattern. The most effective non-invasive approach to measure patient-specific muscle activation patterns is electromyography 31 

(EMG). EMG-informed simulations integrate neuromuscular control strategies by utilizing surface EMG signals recorded 32 

from selected muscles, thereby providing subject-specific insight into muscle activation dynamics. The application of current 33 

EMG-informed approaches in out-of-laboratory settings remains limited, primarily due to the requirement for EMG data 34 

from ideally all relevant muscles 21 or, at a minimum, from eight muscles 22 . In addition, these approaches typically require 35 

subject-specific model calibration, which can be computationally demanding 23, 24 .36 

In this study, we explore an alternative approach by incorporating EMG measurements directly into the objective function 37 

of the muscle redundancy solver 9, aiming to reduce the need for extensive EMG recordings and time-consuming calibration 38 

procedures. The primary objective of this study is to test estimated knee contact forces (KCF) from MSK modeling during level 39 

walking, ramp descent, and stair descent, against in vivo measurements from instrumented implants 25, 26 .40 

## Methods 41 

To achieve this, we used the measurements from the publicly available Comprehensive Assessment of the Musculoskeletal 42 

System (CAMS) dataset 26 as inputs to our MSK modeling simulation pipeline to estimate KCF. The resulting KCF estimates 43 

obtained from minimum muscle activation, EMG-informed, and CCI-informed solvers were compared to the in vivo data and 44 

then quantitatively evaluated. An overview of the methods is presented in Figure 1. 45 

Experimental Data. The CAMS knee dataset 26 provides in vivo measurements of the three force and three moment components 46 

(90–100 Hz acquisition frequency) acting on the tibial implant. The dataset includes measurements of five males and one 47 

female (68 ± 5 years old, 88 ± 12 kg, and 173 ± 4 cm height), who received cemented INNEX knee implants (Zimmer, 48 

Switzerland; FIXUC). We analyzed all gait-related activities including level walking, ramp descent, and stair descent. For 49 

level walking, simulations were conducted on eight trials per subject. For ramp and stair descent, four trials per subject were 50 

analyzed. Data from subject K3R during ramp descent were excluded due to invalid ground reaction force measurements, as 51 

both feet contacted a single force plate during these trials. Whole-body kinematics were measured using 75 skin markers and a 52 

26-camera motion-capture system (Vicon, UMG, UK) at 100 Hz. Ground reaction forces were collected at 2000 Hz with six 53 

force plates embedded in the walkway (Kistler Instrumentation, Winterthur, Switzerland). Bilateral muscle activity for eight 54 

major lower limb muscles (rectus femoris, vastus medialis, vastus lateralis, tibialis anterior, semitendinosus, biceps femoris 55 

long head, medial gastrocnemius, and lateral gastrocnemius) was detected using a 16-channel wireless EMG system (Trigno, 56 

Delsys, USA) with a signal delay of 48ms. The raw EMG was high-pass filtered at 100 Hz, full-wave rectified, and low-pass 57 

filtered at 4 Hz to obtain EMG envelopes, as per ISEK 27 . The 48 ms signal delay was corrected. Furthermore, the ground 58 

reaction forces were low-pass filtered (zero-phase shift Butterworth filter, 4th order, 12Hz). 59 

Musculoskeletal Modeling. We used a generic full-body MSK model described by Rajagopal et al. 28 , with adaptations 60 

from Lai et al. 29 and Uhlrich et al. 9, incorporating 37 degrees of freedom (DOF), 80 musculotendon actuators for the lower 61 

extremities, and 17 ideal torque actuators for the upper body. The model included six DOF between the pelvis and the ground, 62 

three rotational DOF between the pelvis and torso, three rotational DOF at each hip, one rotational DOF at each knee that 63 

parametrized the remaining rotational and translational DOF of the tibiofemoral and patellofemoral joints with respect to knee 64 

flexion, and one rotational DOF at each of the ankle, subtalar, and metatarsophalangeal joints. 65 

MSK modeling and simulation were performed using OpenSim 4.5 30, 31 , with the simulation workflow automated using 66 

the Snakemake workflow management system 32 . The generic model was scaled using the OpenSim Scale Tool to match 67 

subject-specific anthropometric measurements obtained from a standing static trial, during which virtual model markers were 68 

adjusted to align with experimental marker locations. Model scaling proportionally adjusted muscle optimal fiber lengths, 69 

tendon slack lengths, and muscle moment arms according to segment-specific scaling factors, while preserving the ratio of 70 

tendon slack length to optimal fiber length for each muscle. Model kinematics were estimated from experimental marker data 71

Statistical Analysis and Evaluation Metrics. Three statistical measures were utilized to evaluate the correspondence between 122 

the simulated and in vivo KCF. The root mean squared error (RMSE) quantifies the overall magnitude of the error between the 123 

simulated and measured forces across all time steps within each trial. The coefficient of determination R 2 describes how well 124 

the temporal waveform of the estimated forces corresponds to the in vivo measurements. In addition, the peak force error was 125 

defined as the difference at the second peak of the stance phase, computed as the maximum absolute value within 40–60% 126 

of the trial for both the measured and simulated knee joint compressive forces. For each metric, we calculated the mean and 127 

standard deviation over all trials, including level walking, ramp descent, and stair descent. Statistical differences were assessed 128 

using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired, non-normally distributed data, with Holm–Bonferroni correction applied to 129 

account for multiple comparisons across muscle redundancy solvers. A corrected p-value below α = 0.05 was considered 130 

statistically significant. Additionally, we analyzed the relationship between RMSE and CCI for the four muscle redundancy 131 

solvers. First, a linear mixed-effects regression model was specified with a subject-specific random intercept to account for 132 

between-subject variability in baseline RMSE across repeated trials. However, the model did not converge consistently across 133 

analyses and therefore did not yield reliable estimates for inference. As a result, we evaluated the relationship between RMSE 134 

and CCI using linear regression with cluster-robust standard errors to account for within-subject dependence in the data. The six 135 

subjects were treated as clusters. From this analysis, we report the slope ( β , in BW per unit CCI), cluster-robust standard errors, 136 

95 % confidence intervals (95 % CI), p-values, and the number of observations ( N). Statistical significance was defined as 137 

p < 0.05 . All statistical analyses were performed in Python (version 3.10.17) using the SciPy library (version 1.15.2). During 138 

post-processing, the compressive KCF was smoothed using a Savitzky-Golay filter (window length = 41 frames, polynomial 139 

order = 3) to reduce numerical artifacts in the estimated KCF. 140 

## Results 141 

In this study, we compared the estimated compressive KCF obtained from four muscle redundancy solvers - SO, minimum 142 

activation (RMR), EMG-informed, and CCI-informed – to the in vivo measured KCF from all six subjects in the CAMS dataset. 143 

The comparison was performed across three activities: level walking, ramp descent, and stair descent. To ensure consistency 144 

across simulations, a fixed weighting scheme was applied in the objective function of the RMR solver. Specifically, the weight 145 

of the muscle activation minimization term was used as the baseline reference and set to 1, and all other cost function terms 146 

were scaled accordingly. Therefore, the reserve actuator term was assigned a weight of 10, while the EMG-informed and 147 

CCI-informed cost function terms were assigned each a weighting factor of 2. 148

## Discussion 207 

The aim of this study was to evaluate the performance of four different muscle redundancy solvers for estimating KCF from 208 

motion capture measurements. We tested their estimates against in vivo joint force measurements obtained from an instrumented 209 

implant. Specifically, we included two commonly used muscle redundancy solvers, SO and RMR, which both minimize muscle 210 

activations. The key difference between these solvers is that passive muscle forces are incorporated in the RMR solver but 211 

not in the SO solver. To account for changes in muscle coordination strategies, we additionally proposed two EMG-based 212 

approaches. The EMG-informed approach minimizes the error between estimated and measured muscle activations. The 213 

CCI-informed approach minimizes the error in the level of co-contraction between the VL and MG muscle pair, thereby 214 

reducing the experimental setup required to account for changes in muscle coordination strategies. 215 

In this study, we used a fixed set of weights in the objective function of the RMR solver. The EMG- and CCI-specific terms in 216 

the objective function were assigned weights twice as large as those of the muscle activation minimization term to account 217 

for variations in muscle coordination strategies. Reserve actuators were strongly penalized by assigning weights 10 times 218 

higher than those of the muscle activation minimization term to ensure a minimal contribution of reserve actuators. For both 219 

EMG-informed and CCI-informed simulations, weighting factors were not tuned to improve agreement with measured in vivo 220 

knee joint contact forces, to avoid biasing the results toward the measured knee joint contact forces, which are not available in 221 

practice, and to preserve the generalizability of the proposed approach. 222 

Our results indicate the CCI-informed simulations performed most consistently across subjects, resulting in the best performance 223 

overall across all three evaluation metrics (RMSE, R2, and peak force error) for all three activities with the fixed set of weights in 224 

the objective function. Approximately 50–75% of the compressive KCF is contributed by tensile muscle forces acting across the 225 

joint 9, indicating that changes in muscle coordination strategies can substantially impact KCF 36 . By minimizing the CCI error 226 

in the objective function of the muscle redundancy solver, subject-specific coordination patterns of VL and MG are incorporated 227 

into the KCF estimate. This is reflected in the RMSE results for subjects K1L, K2L, K5R, and K7L, where a larger discrepancy 228 

between measured CCI and CCI estimated from minimum activation (RMR) simulations was observed. For these subjects, 229 

the CCI-informed approach resulted in significantly lower RMSE ( p < 0.01) and higher R2 values compared to the minimum 230 

activation (RMR) solver. These results are further supported by the correlation analysis between RMSE and CCI in Figure 5. 231 

In the combined plot across all activities (Figure 5a), the results indicate a positive correlation between CCI and RMSE for 232 

the minimum activation (RMR) simulations, whereas the correlation is reduced in the CCI-informed simulations. The effect 233 

of co-contraction becomes even more pronounced when each activity (Figure 5b-d) is analyzed separately, because median 234 

co-contraction levels increase progressively from level walking to ramp descent and stair descent in the EMG measurements 235 

(Figure 4b). Overall, the CCI-informed simulations effectively capture co-contraction variations, resulting in more consistent 236 

RMSE values across different co-contraction levels without further tuning of the cost function weights. 237 

Furthermore, when comparing the two EMG-based approaches, the EMG-informed simulations showed greater variability 238 

across subjects compared to the CCI-informed approach: for example, RMSE values were lowest for three subjects but highest 239 

for the remaining three, and we attribute the high variability to inaccurate MVC normalization, potentially due to muscle 240 

inhibition during MVC measurements 37 . Consequently, muscle activations in EMG-informed cases can be easily overestimated, 241 

leading to elevated KCF. In contrast, the CCI-informed approach is less sensitive to the absolute magnitude of the MVC used for 242 

EMG normalization than the EMG-informed solver, because the CCI is defined as a ratio of muscle activations. If MVC values 243 

are underestimated similarly across the muscle pair, the relative activation between the muscles remains largely preserved. 244 

Consequently, the CCI approach can be more robust than EMG-informed simulations that rely on absolute EMG amplitudes. 245 

Comparison of the two minimum activation approaches, SO and RMR, revealed that SO overpredicted the second KCF peak 246 

for subject K8L during level walking and for multiple subjects during ramp and stair descent (Figure 2). Whittington et al. 247 

previously reported that approximately 35% of the net hip flexor moment during push-off in level walking arises from passive 248 

hip flexor stretch 38 . Passive contributions from the ankle plantar flexors are around 10% 38 . When these passive forces are not 249 

accounted for, additional muscle activation is required, including biarticular muscles crossing the knee, thereby increasing 250 

muscle co-contraction and the resulting KCF, with the elevated co-contraction reflecting model limitations rather than actual 251 

muscle behavior. 252 

In addition, our results indicate that higher RMSE and peak force errors across all muscle redundancy solvers are estimated 253 

during stair descent compared to level walking and ramp descent (Figure 4 and Supplementary Material Table S2). One possible 254 

reason is that the prescribed secondary knee motions, derived from passive flexion measurements in cadavers 39 , may not be 255

model to simulate squatting from the CAMS dataset showed substantial improvement in the KCF RMSE compared to a study 257 

that used a 1-DOF model 40, 41 . Furthermore, Nejad et al. reported an overprediction of KCF, particularly in deep knee and hip 258

that more comprehensively represent multiplane joint motion across different activities, while not being as computationally 260 

demanding as current complex 6-DOF knee joint models 42 .261 

Our estimated KCF and corresponding RMSE values are similar to those reported in previous studies of the CAMS dataset. 262 

Pellegrinelli et al. 43 reported RMSE values of 0.37 BW for level walking using SO, which is very close to our results of 263 

0.35 BW during level walking. Slightly higher RMSE values of 0.44 BW and 0.48 BW in level walking have been reported by 264 

Guitteny et al. 44 and Nejad et al. 41 , respectively. This difference may be attributed to differences in the underlying MSK models 265 

used in both studies. Furthermore, Guo et al. 40 developed a subject-specific model for one participant from the CAMS dataset 266 

and reported strong agreement with in vivo measurements, with RMSE values below 0.16 BW for level walking and squatting. 267 

This high level of accuracy is likely attributable to subject-specific alignment and bone and implant positioning using computed 268 

tomography scans. In addition, the knee joint was modeled with 6 DOF at the tibiofemoral joint, and flexion–extension 269 

was prescribed based on fluoroscopy measurements. Together, these approaches enabled a more accurate representation of 270 

implant-specific kinematics. The knee kinematic improvements influence both muscle force and contact force predictions by 271 

altering muscle moment arms and the center of pressure location relative to the joint center, which in turn affects the muscle 272 

forces required to satisfy dynamic equilibrium and the resulting estimated KCF. As inaccuracies in joint kinematics have been 273 

shown to translate into errors in the estimated KCF, accounting for subject-specific anatomical and kinematic characteristics 274 

likely contributed to the high agreement with in vivo measurements 45 .275 

Compared to the previously developed EMG-assisted approach applied to the Knee Grand Challenge dataset 46 , the improvements 276 

observed in this study are of similar magnitude. Bennett et al. 23 and Esrafilian et al. 47 reported mean RMSE reductions of 277 

approximately 0.11 BW and 0.09 BW, respectively, when comparing SO with the EMG-assisted approach using a one-DOF 278 

knee model. This is comparable to the 0.10 BW improvement achieved in the present study. In contrast, Princelle et al. 279 

reported substantially smaller reductions, with mean RMSE improvements below 0.04 BW 48 . Notably, unlike the EMG-assisted 280 

approach, the CCI-informed approach does not require model calibration, a process that has been reported to be time-intensive 23 .281 

Thus, comparable improvements are achieved at a substantially lower computational cost and greater ease of use, while relying 282 

on fewer EMG input measurements. 283 

The present study has several limitations. First, the sample size was limited to six participants from the CAMS dataset. 284 

The availability of datasets with instrumented implants is limited by ethical and practical constraints, hindering validation 285 

across larger, more diverse populations. Nevertheless, the CAMS dataset remains one of the most comprehensive resources, 286 

providing multiple activities alongside motion capture, ground reaction forces, EMG, and fluoroscopy measurements. In 287 

addition, statistical inference was limited by the small number of data clusters, which may affect the reliability of standard error 288 

estimates, and results are therefore interpreted primarily in terms of effect direction and magnitude rather than formal statistical 289 

significance. Future simulations and comparisons using additional datasets, such as the Knee Grand Challenge dataset 46 , would 290 

further validate the approach. 291 

Second, our analysis was limited to level walking, ramp descent, and stair descent, while activities such as sit-to-stand and 292 

squatting were excluded. Previous studies have shown that performing the simulations with the one-DOF Walker knee joint 293 

tends to overpredict knee contact forces at deep knee and hip flexion angles, regardless of the changes in muscle coordination 294 

strategies 39, 41 . This is likely due to limitations in the prescribed knee kinematics, as accurate kinematic representation has 295 

been shown to improve agreement with estimated knee joint forces 40 . Further development of knee joint models that better 296 

capture natural internal rotation and abduction motion may help achieve similar accuracy in KCF estimation for activities 297 

involving high knee and hip flexion angles. Furthermore, this study focused solely on validating compressive KCF. In many 298 

weight-bearing tasks, compressive knee joint forces are larger than shear forces and are therefore the main contributor to overall 299 

knee joint loading 26 . However, additional validation of other force components is also necessary, as these may provide further 300 

insight into changes in knee joint loading associated with KOA. 301 

Finally, the CCI-informed approach targeted only the co-contraction between the VL and MG, as these muscles showed the 302 

largest discrepancies between measured and minimum-activation–predicted co-contraction in the studied subjects. Consequently, 303 

changes in coordination strategies of other muscle groups are only captured to a limited extent. Further investigation is required 304 

to determine whether similar patterns are observed in other patient populations, such as individuals with early-stage KOA. 305

In conclusion, our results demonstrate that the CCI-informed approach consistently achieves lower RMSE values for estimating 306 

KCF across all activities and subjects compared to the minimum activation and full EMG-informed methods. Furthermore, 307 

the CCI-informed approach captures changes in muscle coordination strategies that directly impact estimated KCF. Changes 308 

in coordination were often not detected by minimizing muscle activation alone, leading to higher RMSE values at higher 309 

co-contraction levels. In addition, the CCI-informed method is more feasible to apply, as it requires EMG measurements from 310 

only two muscles to account for changes in muscle coordination strategies, which could be measured in functional clothing. 311 

This makes it particularly promising for applications outside the laboratory to track rehabilitation methods, including gait 312 

retraining. 313
