Abstract 73 

Background and Purpose: Badminton imposes considerable cardiovascular 74 

and musculoskeletal stress. Physiological profiling can identify modifiable injury 75 

risk factors and inform exercise-based prevention and rehabilitation. This study 76 

compared cardiovascular recovery, neuromuscular activation, and limb strength 77 

between elite and recreational male university badminton players to derive 78 

preliminary physiological benchmarks for injury risk stratification and exercise 79 

rehabilitation guidance. Methods: Forty male athletes (20 elite: national/university 80 

representatives with ≥5 years of competitive experience; 20 recreational: <3 years 81 

of experience) completed assessments of heart rate recovery (HRR), biceps brachii 82 

surface electromyography (sEMG; SENIAM protocol), handgrip strength 83 

(JAMAR dynamometry), and maximal bodyweight squat repetitions. Independent-84 

sample t-tests with Cohen’s d (α = 0 .05) and Pearson correlations were applied. 85 

Results: Elite players demonstrated significantly greater handgrip strength 86 

(49.00±6.12 vs. 39.00±5.45 kg, p = 0 .001, d = 1 .72) and lower-limb (LL) strength 87 

(60.35±11.29 vs. 41.75±6.72 repetitions, p < 0.001, d = 1 .96). Normalized sEMG 88 

root mean square (RMS) was higher in elite athletes during flexion (11.56±4.16% 89 

vs. 7.26±5.15%, p = 0 .004, d = 0 .94) and extension (12.67±4.56% vs. 7.85±5.73%, 90 

p = 0 .003, d = 0 .94). HRR did not differ significantly between groups ( p = 0 .17, d91 

= 0.43, observed power = 0 .34). Elite players nonetheless showed a more 92 

favorable recovery distribution. sEMG -HRR correlations were weak and non-93 

significant in both groups. Conclusions: Elite badminton players exhibit a distinct 94 

physiological profile of greater strength and more efficient neuromuscular 95 

activation. These preliminary cross-sectional findings may support the design of 96 

exercise-based injury-prevention and rehabilitation in university badminton. 97 

Keywords: Surface electromyography; handgrip dynamometry; heart rate recovery; TRIPP 98 

framework; integrative neuromuscular training; athletic performance benchmarking; sport-99 

specific conditioning. 100

Introduction 101 

Badminton is a physiologically demanding racket sport requiring coordinated integration of 102 

aerobic endurance, anaerobic power, and neuromuscular precision [1], [2]. Elite competition 103 

involves explosive movements exceeding 6 m lunges, 60 cm vertical jumps, directional 104 

changes > 5 m/s, and overhead smashes > 400 km/h [1], [2]. Match durations frequently 105 

exceed 60 − 90 min, demanding sustained high-intensity performance in extended rallies 106 

while maintaining technical accuracy under progressive physiological fatigue [3], [4]. 107 

Recent systematic reviews report injury incidence rates of 2 .5−4 .4 per 1,000 training hours, 108 

with elite players aged 15 − 18 years exhibiting peak injury rates of 2 .91 − 3 .52 injuries per 109 

1,000 hours [5], [6]. LL injuries comprise 41–92% of total badminton-related injuries across 110 

published studies, with ankle (13.4–18.8%), knee (18.6–18.8%), and lower-back (10.0– 111 

12.3%) injuries predominating [5], [6]. The wide range in LL injury proportions (41–92%) 112 

reflects substantial methodological heterogeneity across the source studies, including 113 

differences in injury definitions, surveillance settings, participant age groups, competitive 114 

levels, and geographical populations. Despite this variance, the consistent predominance of 115 

LL injuries across all reviewed studies underscores the need for systematic LL-focused 116 

screening and exercise-based prevention strategies in badminton, regardless of competitive 117 

context. Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries, though less frequent (3–5% of total 118 

injuries), impose career-threatening consequences with prolonged rehabilitation periods, and 119 

approximately 70% occur through the non-contact mechanisms during deceleration and 120 

pivoting [7], [8]. Shoulder injuries affect 15−20% of competitive badminton players due to 121 

repetitive overhead strokes that generate glenohumeral joint reaction forces exceeding 1.5 122 

times bodyweight [2], [9] . These injury patterns highlight the need for systematic screening 123 

and exercise-based prevention strategies targeting modifiable physiological and 124 

neuromuscular risk factors. 125 

The Translating Research into Injury Prevention Practice (TRIPP) framework emphasizes 126 

six consecutive stages for effective injury prevention: (1) injury surveillance, (2) analysis of 127 

etiology and mechanism, (3) development of prevention strategies, (4) efficacy testing under 128 

ideal Conditions, (5) real-world implementation, and (6) effectiveness and scalability 129

imbalances, inadequate cardiovascular recovery, and biomechanical maladaptation, 131 

substantially contribute to injury sus-acceptability and represent viable targets for structured 132 

training interventions[12], [13]. 133 

Cardiovascular efficiency, particularly post-exertion recovery capacity, plays a dual role in 134 

badminton performance: sustaining high-intensity output across repeated rallies and mitigating 135 

injury risk associated with neuromuscular fatigue [3],[14], [15], [16] . HRR, defined as the 136 

rate of decline in heart rate following exercise cessation, serves as a validated non-invasive 137 

marker of parasympathetic reactivation and overall cardiovascular conditioning [17], [18] .138 

Elite athletes typically exhibit faster parasympathetic restoration compared with less-trained 139 

counterparts [19]. 140 

The strength of the hand grip reflects integrated forearm and intrinsic hand muscle function 141 

essential for racket stabilization during high-velocity strokes [20], [21], [12]. Strong 142 

correlations between grip strength and shoulder lateral-rotator strength suggest that grip 143 

assessment can serve as a [14], [22] (INT) protocols incorporating grip strengthening, rotator 144 

cuff exercises, and scapular stabilization have reduced shoulder injury rates by 25–35% in 145 

racket-sport athletes [23], [13]. 146 

LL function underpins movement efficiency and injury resilience. Athletes with greater 147 

lower extremity strength and balanced hamstring-to-quadriceps ratios demonstrate 43 − 73% 148 

lower ACL injury rates [24], [15]. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) confirm that 8–12 149 

weeks of INT combining balance training, plyometric, agility, and sport-specific exercises 150 

significantly reduce 151 

lower-limb injury rates while improving performance [23], [13], [25]. 152 

Surface EMG captures motor-unit recruitment, providing quantitative indices of muscle 153 

activation amplitude, timing, and neuromuscular control [17], [20], [26]. In racket sports, elite 154 

athletes exhibit greater motor-unit synchronization, reduced antagonist co-contraction, and 155 

more efficient force production than less-skilled players [21], [27]. Neuromuscular deficits, 156 

including delayed activation, excessive co-contraction, and limb asymmetry >10–15%, are 157 

established risk factors for lower-limb injury [28], [29]. 158

Despite extensive knowledge of isolated physiological and neuromuscular factors in 159 

badminton, existing studies have typically assessed cardiovascular recovery, strength, or 160 

neuromuscular activation in isolation rather than simultaneously within a single cohort and 161 

under an explicit TRIPP-aligned injury prevention framework. While Bisschoff et al [4] 162 

examined autonomic markers in elite badminton players, and Wylleman et al [5]. Reported 163 

physiological and neuromuscular responses during match simulation, neither study linked 164 

multi-domain profiling to structured injury-prevention benchmarks in a university-level 165 

athlete population. 166 

This study aimed to characterize and compare cardiovascular recovery (HRR), 167 

neuromuscular activation (biceps brachii sEMG), and upper- and lower-limb strength between 168 

elite and recreational male university badminton players, and to explore associations between 169 

neuromuscular activation and recovery capacity. 170

> 2.3

Anthropometric assessment 193 

Baseline anthropometric and body-composition measurements were obtained using a digital 194 

body-composition analyzer (Omron Karada Scan HBF-375; Omron Healthcare Co., Ltd., 195 

Kyoto, Japan) shown in Fig. 1. Assessments took place in the morning after a 12-h overnight 196 

fast. Participants followed standardized hydration instructions for 24 h prior to testing. 197 

Measured variables included height (cm), body mass (kg), body mass index (BMI, kg/m 2), 198 

body fat percentage, visceral fat level, and resting metabolic rate (kcal/day). All measurements 199 

adhered to the manufacturer’s guidelines. 200 

> 2.4

Handgrip strength assessment 201 

Handgrip strength of the dominant (right) hand was measured using a calibrated JAMAR 202 

hydraulic hand dynamometer (Patterson Medical, Warrenville, IL, USA) set at the second 203 

handle position shown in Fig. 2(a). Testing followed the American Society of Hand Therapists 204 

recommendations. Participants stood upright with the dominant arm alongside the body, 205 

shoulder in neutral rotation, elbow fully extended, and forearm in neutral rotation. 206 

After a brief familiarization, participants performed three maximal voluntary contractions, 207 

each separated by 30 s of rest. Strong verbal encouragement was provided to promote 208 

maximal effort. The highest value (kg) across the three trials was used for analysis. The 209 

dynamometer was calibrated before each testing session according to manufacturer's 210 

specifications. 211

> 2.5

Lower-limb strength assessment 212 

Lower-limb muscular endurance was assessed using a standardized bodyweight squat 213 

repetition test conducted under the supervision of trained exercise scientists shown in Fig. 214 

2(b). A bodyweight squat test was selected as a practical, field-friendly proxy for LL 215 

functional endurance relevant to repeated-sprint and lunge demands in badminton; this 216 

measure is noted as an endurance index rather than a direct measure of maximal strength or 217 

power, which is acknowledged as a study limitation. Participants performed a standardized 218 

warm-up consisting of 10 bodyweight squats and dynamic leg stretches, then completed as 219 

many technically correct full squats as possible across three trials (2 min rest between trials). 220 

Squat technique criteria included: (1) feet shoulder-width apart; (2) descent until thighs were 221 

parallel to the floor (knee angle ≈ 90 ◦

); (3) controlled tempo (1 s descent, 1 s ascent); and (4) 222 

maintenance of a neutral spine. Repetitions not meeting these criteria were excluded. The 223 

highest number of correctly executed repetitions across the three trials was used for analysis. 224 

> 2.6

Heart rate recovery protocol 225 

HRR was assessed in the Bir Shreshtha Hamidur Rahman Central Gymnasium and 226 

Conditioning Laboratory. Participants attended testing at a consistent time of day within a 2-h 227 

window to minimize circadian effects and avoided caffeine, alcohol, and strenuous exercise 228 

for 24 h beforehand. 229 

Resting heart rate was measured with a Polar H10 heart-rate monitor after 5 min of seated 230 

rest. Participants then performed a standardized dynamic warm-up followed by a graded 231 

treadmill protocol beginning at 6 km/h and increasing by 0.5 km/h every 30 s until moderate 232 

exertion (approximately 120 beats per minute) was reached. The data collection steps are 233 

graphically represented in Fig. 3. On Day 1, each participant’s submaximal intensity was 234 

identified as 70% of heart rate at the first kilometer. On Day 2, participants ran at this 235 

individualized intensity for exactly 5 min. Immediately after exercise, they sat quietly while 236 

heart rate was recorded every 30 s for 5 min. 237 

HRR was defined as the absolute heart rate recorded at 5 min post-exercise, with lower 238 

values indicating better parasympathetic recovery. It is acknowledged that a submaximal 239

> 2.7

Surface electromyography assessment 243 

2.7.1 Data acquisition 244 

sEMG data were collected in the Biomedical Signal Processing Laboratory using a BIOPAC 245 

MP45 system (BIOPAC Systems, Inc., Goleta, CA, USA) at a sampling rate of 1,000 Hz. 246 

Bipolar surface electrodes were placed over the biceps brachii of the dominant arm according 247 

to SENIAM recommendations. Skin was shaved, abraded, and cleaned to maintain impedance 248 

below 5 k Ω. Participants sat with the elbow flexed to 90 ◦

. They performed two elbow flexions 249 

followed by two elbow extensions at a comfortable pace, repeated three times with 30 s rest 250 

between sets.The complete process of recording sEMG signal acquisition has been shown in 251 

Fig. 4. A standardized flexion–extension task was deliberately selected over sport-specific 252 

movements to ensure high inter-session reliability and to provide a baseline measure of 253 

neuromuscular control capacity applicable across skill levels; the implications of this non-254 

sport-specific task are discussed in the Limitations section. The cleanest trial free of visible 255 

artifacts was selected for analysis. 256 

> 2.7.2

Signal processing and feature extraction 257 

Data were processed in MATLAB R2023b (MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA). A fourth-order 258 

Butterworth bandpass filter (20–450 Hz) removed motion artifacts and high-frequency noise, 259 

and a 50-Hz notch filter (quality factor Q = 30) suppressed power-line interference. Signals 260 

were full-wave rectified to obtain amplitude envelopes. Active muscle periods were identified 261 

using a threshold-based approach: segments exceeding 10% of the maximum rectified 262 

amplitude for at least 200 ms were considered active. Root mean square (RMS) amplitude was 263 

computed using a 250-ms moving window. RMS values were normalized to the maximum 264 

RMS recorded during a maximal voluntary contraction and expressed as percent of maximum 265

power to detect moderate effects with this sample size. 339

In elite players, the correlation between mean biceps brachii RMS activation and HRR was 341 

positive but non-significant ( r = 0 .29, p = 0 .21), as shown in Fig. 10 (left), indicating a 342 

negligible-to-low and statistically unreliable association. In recreational players, the 343 

correlation was slightly stronger but still non-significant ( r = 0 .37, p = 0 .10), as shown in Fig. 344

relationships. These weak, non-significant correlations suggest that neuromuscular and 346 

cardiovascular adaptations may develop through partially independent pathways in this 347 

cohort, though the small sample limits firm 348

Discussion 350 

This cross-sectional study compared cardiovascular recovery, neuromuscular activation, and 351 

upper- and lower-limb strength between elite and recreational male university badminton 352 

players with the aim of deriving preliminary physiological benchmarks for injury risk 353 

stratification and exercise rehabilitation guidance. With respect to the primary objective, HRR 354 

at 5 min post exercise did not differ significantly between groups ( p = 0 .17, d = 0 .43), and 355 

observed statistical power was modest (0.34), precluding firm conclusions about autonomic 356 

differences at this sample size. Regarding the secondary objectives, elite athletes 357 

demonstrated substantially greater handgrip strength (d = 1.72), lower-limb muscular 358 

endurance ( d = 1 .96), and normalized biceps brachii sEMG amplitude during both flexion ( d =359 

0.94) and extension ( d = 0 .94). The sEMG– HRR correlations were weak and non-significant 360 

in both groups (elite: r = 0 .29; recreational: r = 0 .37), consistent with the hypothesis that 361 

neuromuscular and cardiovascular adaptations may develop through partially independent 362

> 4.1

Handgrip strength and upper-limb injury risk 366 

Elite players exhibited approximately 26% higher handgrip strength ( d = 1 .72), reinforcing the 367 

importance of upper-limb strength for racket control, stroke velocity, and load attenuation at 368 

the wrist and elbow. Stronger grip and forearm musculature may improve shock absorption 369 

and force distribution, which may reduce lateral epicondylitis and other overuse injuries in 370 

racket sports[32]. Epidemiological evidence confirms that rotator cuff tendinopathy and 371 

impingement remain common in competitive badminton and are linked to deficits in strength 372 

and control 373 

within the kinetic chain[33], [34]. Incorporating grip-specific and rotator cuff–focused 374 

exercises into conditioning appears to be a practical, low-cost strategy to enhance stroke 375 

performance and 376 

Support injury-preventive rehabilitation objectives[23], [13]. 377 

> 4.2

Lower-limb strength, movement efficiency, and injury prevention 378 

Elite athletes performed about 45% more maximal squat repetitions ( d = 1 .96), indicating 379 

superior LL endurance that supports repeated accelerations, lunges, and directional changes in 380 

match play. This result is particularly relevant given that lower-extremity injuries, including 381 

strains, sprains, tendinopathies, and stress fractures that account for most badminton injuries 382 

across Levels of play[32], [5]. Evidence from RCTs shows that INT programs combining 383 

strength, balance, plyometric, and agility can improve functional movement, reduce limb 384 

asymmetry, and lower Injury risk by approximately 35–45% in racket-sport athletes[23], [13]. 385 

The large group differences in LL muscular endurance observed here support prioritizing LL 386 

resistance and plyometric training as core components of exercise-based injury-prevention and 387 

return-to-sport rehabilitation programs. 388

Neuromuscular activation, motor control, and eccentric capacity 389 

Elite players demonstrated about 60% higher normalized biceps brachii activation during both 390

eccentric control during arm deceleration. These findings align with EMG-guided stretch 392 

shortening-cycle training studies that report improved latency, reactive strength, and impulse 393

recognized as a key protective factor against tendon overload and muscle strain, and EMG-395 

based biofeedback can help correct asymmetries and improve movement quality during both 396 

training and rehabilitation[36], [37]. It must be noted, however, that these sEMG data were 397 

obtained during a standardized, non-sport-specific elbow task; direct inference to badminton-398 

specific overhead or smash mechanics would require further validation using sport-specific 399 

protocols. 400 

> 4.4

Heart rate recovery, autonomic function, and load management 401 

The primary hypothesis that elite players would demonstrate faster HRR kinetics was not 402 

supported at the current sample size ( p = 0 .17, d = 0 .43, observed power = 0 .34). This null 403 

result does not exclude a meaningful autonomic difference between groups. It reflects 404 

insufficient statistical power to detect a moderate effect with n = 40. A post-hoc power 405 

analysis indicates that detecting d = 0 .43 at α = 0 .05 with 80% power would require 406 

approximately 88 participants per group. The moderate effect size and more favorable HRR 407 

distribution in elite players nevertheless remain compatible with enhanced parasympathetic 408 

reactivation typically observed in trained athletes. 409 

> 4.5

Integrated implications for exercise-based injury prevention and rehabilitation 410 

The present findings indicate that elite badminton players combine greater strength, more 411 

efficient neuromuscular activation, and numerically more favorable recovery profiles. These 412 

combined differences may reflect a physiological profile associated with enhanced 413 

performance and potentially reduced injury susceptibility, though the cross-sectional design 414 

precludes causal conclusions. In applied practice, three exercise-based strategies are supported 415

> 4.6

Limitations and future research directions 423 

This study has several important limitations that should be considered when interpreting 424 

findings. First, the modest sample size ( n = 40) and cross-sectional design limit statistical 425 

power for detecting moderate effects (particularly for HRR, observed power= 0.34) and 426 

preclude causal inference about training adaptations or injury risk. Additionally, participants 427 

were recruited from a single university using a convenience sampling approach, which limits 428 

the generalizability of findings to other institutions, competitive levels, or cultural contexts. 429 

Second, the exclusively male cohort restricts generalizability to female athletes, who may 430 

exhibit distinct injury profiles and neuromuscular characteristics. Third, neuromuscular 431 

assessment was limited to biceps brachii sEMG during isolated tasks, and cardiovascular 432 

recovery was assessed after treadmill running rather than sport-specific intermittent play. 433 

Finally, the absence of prospective injury surveillance means that links between physiological 434 

markers and actual injury incidence remain Inferential. 435 

Future research should prioritize 6–12 month longitudinal RCTs integrating INT protocols 436 

with comprehensive injury tracking to confirm causal benefits and validate injury-prevention 437 

effectiveness. Studies should expand neuromuscular profiling to include LL and shoulder 438 

musculature during badminton-specific movements (e.g., lunges, overhead strokes) and 439 

establish sex specific normative data. Development of multivariable risk-prediction models 440 

and deployment of wearable sensors for real-time on-court monitoring may further refine 441 

personalized exercise-based injury-prevention and return-to-sport strategies. 442

Conclusion 443 

Elite badminton athletes in this university cohort exhibited substantially greater handgrip 444 

strength ( d = 1 .72), LL muscular endurance ( d = 1 .96), and neuromuscular activation ( d =445 

0.94) than recreational players, representing a distinct physiological profile that may be 446 

associated with reduced injury susceptibility through enhanced joint stability and load 447 

distribution. HRR did not differ significantly between groups, although a moderate effect size 448 

and more favorable recovery distribution were observed in elite players. To our knowledge, 449 

this study provides the first integrated cross-sectional profile combining cardiovascular 450 

recovery, sEMG-based neuromuscular assessment, and multi-limb strength evaluation within 451 

a university badminton cohort in South Asia, contextualized within the TRIPP framework. 452 

Based on the present cohort data, preliminary screening reference values of handgrip 453 

strength ≥ 45 kg, squat repetitions ≥ 55, and normalized sEMG RMS ≥ 10% MVC may 454 

warrant further investigation as candidate thresholds for injury risk stratification; however, 455 

these values are cohort-specific and should be validated in larger, multi-centre, prospective 456 

studies before adoption in routine screening or rehabilitation protocols. Routine physiological 457 

profiling incorporating handgrip strength, lower-limb muscular endurance, and sEMG-based 458 

neuromuscular assessment combined with structured INT implementation may provide an 459 

accessible, scalable strategy for reducing badminton’s injury burden while supporting 460 

performance excellence. The present data are preliminary and cross-sectional; confirmation by 461 

longitudinal, prospective, multi-centre studies with adequate statistical power is required 462 

before these benchmarks are adopted in routine clinical or rehabilitation screening. 463
