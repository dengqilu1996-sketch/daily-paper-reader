> Abstract 20
> Background 21
> The distinction between healthy and pathological ageing has led to the concept of vitality 22
> capacity (VC), which can be understood as the body’s physiological reserve. An individual’s 23
> VC can be estimated using 12 biomarkers spread across 3 domains: immune and stress 24
> response, energy and metabolism and neuromuscular function. Vitality capacity may be 25
> preserved by lifelong physical activity. This cross-sectional study aimed to examine the 26
> relationship between lifelong aerobic physical activity and VC. 27
> Methods 28
> VC of 20 lifelong active and 19 inactive healthy adults aged >55 years was assessed using 12 29
> biomarkers across the three VC domains. Domain-specific z-scores were calculated and 30
> averaged to derive a global VC score. Principal component analysis was performed and 31
> loadings extracted to estimate domains weight, and multiple correlations were conducted to 32
> identify associations among biomarkers, domains and VC scores. 33
> Results 34
> VC was higher in lifelong active participants (+0.2 z-score units, p = 0.006) and correlated 35
> with age (r = -0.53, p < 0.001). Neuromuscular domain contributed most to VC variability, 36
> and the immune and stress response domain was higher in the active group (+0.4 z-score 37
> units, p = 0.001) as energy/metabolism among female participants (+0.5 z-score units, p.adj 38
> = 0.006). 39
> Conclusion 40

> 1.  Introduction 62
> In 2015, the United Nations declared 2021-2030 the Decade of Healthy Ageing, promoting a 63
> shift from a disease-centered model of ageing toward the preservation of functional ability— 64
> the capacity of older adults to be and do the things they have reason to value 1. This global 65
> paradigmatic shift has led to new definitions of healthy ageing and highlights the need for 66
> appropriate reference populations to study it. 67
> Physically active older adults represent a particularly relevant model in this context, as the 68
> benefits of physical activity (PA) on health, ageing or longevity are well established and 69
> widely documented in numerous studies and systematic reviews 2–4 . Physically active older 70
> adults have been extensively studied and show increased longevity 5, longer telomeres 6 and 71
> improved inflammatory profile 7, particularly in the context of aerobic training. While 72
> resistance  training  also  provides  age-related  health  benefits,  particularly  through 73
> improvements in body composition 8, aerobic physical activity has the strongest associations 74
> with reduced mortality, cardiovascular protection, respiratory and metabolic health, and 75
> others broad systemic benefits 8–11 .76
> Beyond these traditional health outcomes, a recent study showed a positive association 77
> between moderate to vigorous physical activity (MVPA) and intrinsic capacity, a key concept 78
> in healthy ageing 12 . Intrinsic capacity is defined as the composite of all physical and mental 79
> capacities  that an individual can draw  on at any  point in  time  that,  together with 80
> environmental factors, would determine functional ability, and thus healthy ageing 13 . The 81
> physiological underlying domain of intrinsic capacity has been defined as vitality capacity 82
> (VC): a physiological state (due to normal or accelerated biological ageing processes) 83

> resulting from the interaction between multiple physiological systems, reflected in (the level 84
> of) immune and stress response, energy and metabolism, and neuromuscular function of the 85
> body 14 .86
> The potential contribution of physical activity (PA) to the preservation of VC has recently 87
> been reviewed 15 . Authors describe how PA, and particularly structured exercise, induces 88
> beneficial adaptations across the three domains of VC (i.e., immune and stress response, 89
> energy and metabolism, and neuromuscular function) thereby helping to maintain VC and 90
> support healthy ageing. Despite being reviewed, the relationship between PA and VC has not 91
> been demonstrated yet. Moreover, attempts to assess and/or establish VC score remain 92
> limited. A study reported positive associations between VC and cardiometabolic health and 93
> negative  associations  with  various  chronic diseases  and  impaired  physical  function 16 .94
> However, the scoring system proposed 16  was based on nine biomarkers among the 12 95
> suggested by the WHO working group 14 . A recent study studied two domains of VC (i.e., 96
> energy and metabolism, and neuromuscular function) to validate their use in VC assessment 97
> and examined their links with locomotor function and quality of life 17 . However, as stated by 98
> the authors, the absence of immune and stress response domain highlights the need for 99
> further studies encompassing all three domains. These limitations are consistent with 100
> findings from a scoping review 18 , which highlighted a lack of alignment with the WHO 101
> framework and emphasized the need for standardized tools and approaches consistent with 102
> WHO recommendations. 103
> Therefore, the present study operationalized a VC construct encompassing all three domains 104
> and  aligned  it  with  the  12  biomarkers  proposed  by  the  WHO  working  group 14 , by 105
> investigating the influence of lifelong aerobic PA practice on overall VC as well as the relative 106

> 2.  Method: 110
> 2.1. Participants 111
> This cross-sectional observational study included 40 healthy adults (20 males and 20 112
> females)  aged  over  55  years,  comprising  20  lifelong  active  and  20  lifelong  inactive 113
> participants. All participants were free of chronic disease, non-smokers (or abstinent >3 114
> years), and postmenopausal females had confirmed menopause for at least 6 months. 115
> Written informed consent was obtained from all participants, and the study was approved 116
> by the Comité de Protection des Personnes Ile de France II (approval no. 2024-A01310-47). 117
> Active individuals were defined as those who had engaged in structured aerobic activity ≥3 118
> times per week for the past 30 years, with no break longer than one week in the 3 months 119
> preceding the study and no break exceeding 12 consecutive months over that period. Their 120
> activity levels met or exceeded international recommendations for physical activity (WHO, 121
> 2020; ANSES, 2016), corresponding to ≥150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic 122
> activity, ≥75 minutes of vigorous activity, or an equivalent combination. 123
> Inactive individuals were defined as those who did not meet these criteria, reporting 124
> predominantly sedentary or low-intensity occupational histories, no regular sports or fitness 125
> activity, and ≤75 minutes per week of leisure-time physical activity or transportation-related 126
> physical activity over the past 30 years. 127

> 2.6. Vitality Capacity score construct 209
> To our knowledge, only one study proposed a VC score construct 16 . However, the unequal 210
> number of biomarkers across components (e.g., four energy and metabolism biomarkers, 211
> two neuromuscular function biomarkers) was not accounted, potentially biasing the final VC 212
> score towards energy and metabolism domain. We therefore propose a score construct that 213
> gives the same weight to each domain. 214
> Vitality capacity, defined as a 12-biomarker construct 14 , was assessed by normalizing each 215
> individual biomarker into z-scores and aggregating them within their respective domain. For 216
> instance, neuromuscular function domain was calculated as the mean of z-scored handgrip 217

> strength, knee strength, and spirometry. For perceived fatigue, because a high fatigue z-218
> score was associated with more fatigue, the score has been reversed to match with others. A 219
> same reverse logic has been applied to IL-6, TNF-α and HbA1c. As no specific blood 220
> biomarkers  were  predefined,  four  candidate  biomarkers  were  measured,  and  those 221
> demonstrating the best fit with the data were retained for inclusion in the final construct. To 222
> this end, we compared all combinations of two inflammatory biomarkers (IL-6, TNF-α) and 223
> two metabolic biomarkers (adiponectin, HbA1c), making four different pairs of biomarkers, 224
> each one added to the remaining ten standardized vitality capacity biomarkers. Structural 225

> adiponectin has been retained and TNF- α and HbA1c removed from the VC score construct. 230

> 4.  Discussion: 316
> This study aimed to measure and compare VC of lifelong aerobic active and inactive healthy 317
> older adults and estimate each domain weight in the VC construct. We report, for the first 318
> time, the operationalization of a VC score constructed from 12 biomarkers consistent with 319
> those proposed by the WHO working group on VC 14 . As hypothesized, lifelong aerobic active 320
> older  adults  had  higher VC than inactive counterparts. We  highlighted that, despite 321
> contributing substantially to overall VC variability, the neuromuscular function domain of VC 322
> did not significantly differ between groups. In contrast, the immune and stress response 323
> domain score, as along with some of its biomarkers (i.e., oxygen saturation, heart rate 324
> variability) showed the clearest distinction between lifelong aerobic active and inactive 325
> healthy older adults (Fig. 4A). 326
> The inverse correlation between age and VC is consistent with the existing literature on VC 16
> 327
> and, more broadly, with the decline of intrinsic capacity with age 27 . The positive correlation 328

> 5.  Conclusion 425
> In conclusion, our findings suggest that lifelong aerobic physical activity is associated with 426
> higher VC in older adults, supporting the concept that lifelong physically active individuals 427
> represent a relevant model for the study of healthy ageing. These results reinforce the 428
> World Health Organization framework of healthy ageing and highlight the potential of VC as 429
> an integrative measure of physiological ageing. Future longitudinal and experimental studies 430
> are  needed  to  confirm  these  associations,  refine  biomarker  selection,  and  establish 431
> reference values applicable at both individual and population levels. Expanding future 432
> research to more diverse cohorts will also be essential to improve generalizability across 433
> populations. Our findings further suggest that PA modalities may differently affect VC 434
> domains. While aerobic PA appears particularly relevant to improve the immune and stress 435
> domain, neuromuscular function may require complementary approaches such as resistance 436
> training. This, and the sex effect observed in the energy and metabolism domain, raises the 437
> possibility of more tailored PA programs according to individual physiological profiles. More 438
> broadly, this study emphasizes the fundamental role of sustained physical activity across the 439
> lifespan to preserve physiological functions, reinforcing the idea that maintaining movement 440
> is not only beneficial, but intrinsic to human healthy ageing. 441
