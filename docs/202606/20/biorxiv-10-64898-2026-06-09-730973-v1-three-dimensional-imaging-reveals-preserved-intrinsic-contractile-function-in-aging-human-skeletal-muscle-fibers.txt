ABSTRACT (Word Count: 249) 1

Age-related reductions in muscle fiber size and contractile function, particularly in fibers 2expressing fast myosin heavy chains, contribute to declines in whole-muscle power. However, 3methodological limitations in estimating fiber size during contractile experiments have likely 4contributed to conflicting findings regarding whether reduced single-fiber force and power in older 5adults reflects their smaller size and/or impaired intrinsic contractile function. To address this, we 6coupled single-fiber contractile experiments with 3D-imaging in 7 young (19-40yrs) and 6 older 7(69-84yrs) males to assess intrinsic contractile function and compare agreement between 3D-8derived cross-sectional area (CSA) and CSA estimates obtained either in air or solution. Fast fiber 9CSA from older males were ~28–45% smaller across measurement conditions compared with 10 young, whereas slow fiber CSA did not differ. Accordingly, absolute force and power of fast fibers 11 were 41% and 37% lower. When normalized to CSA from measurements in air or 3D-imaging, 12 size-specific force and power either did not differ or were greater in older adults, indicating 13 preserved intrinsic contractile function in both fiber types. This was supported by no age-related 14 differences in the rate of tension redevelopment (ktr ), a size-independent measure of intrinsic 15 contractile function. In contrast, size-specific force and power calculated using solution-based 16 CSA estimates were lower in older compared with young adults, and Bland-Altman analyses 17 demonstrated the poorest agreement between solution-based and 3D CSA measurements. These 18 findings indicate that intrinsic contractile function is preserved with aging and suggest that 19 methodological differences in CSA measurement contributes to the disparate findings in the 20 literature. 21

INTRODUCTION 22 

Aging is associated with a progressive decline in skeletal muscle mass and function 23 (strength and power) that leads to reduced mobility and quality of life in older adults. Notably, the 24 age-related decline in muscle power (~2-4% yr -1 ) occurs more rapidly than the loss of total muscle 25 mass (~0.5-1% yr -1 ) (Mitchell et al. , 2012; Alcazar et al. , 2020; Alcazar et al. , 2023), suggesting 26 that factors beyond muscle atrophy, such as impairments in the ability of the nervous system to 27 activate the muscle and/or factors disrupting intrinsic contractile function, must also contribute to 28 the loss in power. Importantly, the age-related decline in the ability to generate power is a stronger 29 predictor of physical impairments (Reid & Fielding, 2012) and mortality (Araujo et al. , 2025) than 30 muscle strength. Despite the importance of muscle power to mobility and physical function in 31 older adults, the mechanisms for the accelerated age-related loss in power relative to muscle mass 32 are not well understood. 33 

It was recently reported that the reduced ability of older adults to generate power in the 34 lower limb is due primarily to factors within the muscle, with neural factors potentially playing a 35 minor role in older females (Wrucke et al. , 2024). However, identifying the mechanisms within 36 the muscle remains particularly challenging, in part due to the fiber type heterogeneity and the 37 well-established differential effects of aging on the size and function of each fiber type (Lexell et 38 

al. , 1988; Nilwik et al. , 2013; Teigen et al. , 2020; Grosicki et al. , 2022; Teigen et al. , 2026). One 39 proposed mechanism underlying the accelerated age-related decline in whole muscle power is the 40 selective atrophy of the fibers expressing the fast myosin heavy chain (MyHC) isoforms, which 41 often exhibit marked reductions in force- and power-generating capacity with aging (Sundberg et 42 

al. , 2018a; Sundberg et al. , 2025). Indeed, a recent review of the aging single fiber literature found 43 that the fast fibers from older adults are typically smaller and generate lower absolute force and 44 power compared with young adults, whereas slow fiber size and contractile function are largely 45

However, when fast fiber force and power were normalized to 46 fiber size, which is the most commonly reported metric of intrinsic contractile function, the 47 findings are inconsistent, with studies reporting impaired intrinsic contractile function in older 48 adults (Larsson et al. , 1997; Frontera et al. , 2000; D'Antona et al. , 2003; Ochala et al. , 2007; Hvid 49

et al. , 2021; Sundberg et al. , 2025). Thus, despite nearly three decades of research, it remains 53 unknown whether the reductions in fast fiber force and power in older adults are attributed to their 54 smaller size and/or impaired intrinsic contractile function. 55 

One potential critical, yet underappreciated, factor contributing to this longstanding 56 inconsistency may be a fundamental methodological limitation in how muscle fiber cross-sectional 57 area (CSA) is estimated (Smith & Herzog, 2023; Mebrahtu et al. , 2024; Hessel et al. , 2026). In 58 chemically permeabilized and mechanically peeled fiber preparations, CSA is estimated from two-59 dimensional (2D) images by measuring the fiber diameter at multiple points while the fiber is 60 either submerged in relaxing solution or briefly suspended in air (< 5s). The use of air-based 61 measurements arose from the recognition that fibers in solution are not simple geometric shapes 62 (Blinks, 1965; Malakoutian et al. , 2021), but instead exhibit irregular cross-sectional profiles that 63 violate the conventional assumptions of circular or elliptical fiber geometry. Suspending fibers in 64 air was therefore intended to leverage surface tension and capillary forces to induce a more 65 uniform, cylindrical shape, thereby better approximating these geometric assumptions (Allen & 66 Moss, 1987; Metzger & Moss, 1987). However, even under these conditions, it is unknown how 67 well these measurements approximate the fiber CSA, and variations in the exposure time to air can 68

Furthermore, studies assessing muscle fiber shape using 70 immunohistochemical methods have reported that fibers from older adults exhibit greater 71 deviations from circularity, particularly the fast MyHC II fibers (Kirkeby & Garbarsch, 2000; 72 Barnouin et al. , 2017; Horwath et al. , 2024; Soendenbroe et al. , 2024). Collectively, these 73 limitations suggest that inaccuracies in CSA estimation from 2D images may contribute to the 74 inconsistent findings on intrinsic contractile function with aging. 75 

Continued advancements in cellular imaging and computational analyses, particularly 76 using confocal microscopy, have enabled high-resolution, three-dimensional (3D) renderings of 77 isolated human muscle fibers (Liu et al. , 2009; Cristea et al. , 2010), allowing more precise 78 measurements of fiber morphology. However, no study has combined single-fiber contractile 79 mechanics with 3D morphological analysis of the same fiber segments or directly compared 3D-80 derived CSA with conventional CSA estimates derived from 2D diameter measurements in 81 solution and air. Thus, the primary aims of this study were to: 1) determine whether intrinsic 82 contractile function is impaired with aging by assessing single fiber contractile mechanics 83 followed by 3D morphological analysis of the same fibers, and 2) compare the agreement between 84 CSA estimates obtained from 2D images in solution and air with those derived from 3D imaging. 85 We hypothesized that 1) reductions in fast fiber force and power with aging will be determined 86 primarily by their smaller size rather than altered cross-bridge mechanics, with no age differences 87 observed in slow fibers; 2) CSA estimates from 2D images obtained in both solution and air would 88 show bias and poor agreement with 3D-derived measurements; and 3) methodological differences 89 in fiber CSA assessment would account for much of the discrepancies in the literature regarding 90 whether aging alters intrinsic single-fiber contractile function. 91

METHODS 92 

Participants and ethical approval 93 

Seven young (20 – 40 yr) and 6 older males (69 – 84 yr) volunteered and provided written 94 informed consent to participate in this study. Participants underwent a general health screening and 95 were excluded from participation if they were taking medications that affect muscle mass or 96 neuromuscular function (e.g. hormone-replacement therapies, antidepressants, glucocorticoids, 97 etc.). Participants were healthy, community-dwelling adults free of any known neurological, 98 musculoskeletal, and cardiovascular diseases. All experimental procedures were approved by the 99 Marquette University Institutional Review Board and conformed to the principles in the 100 Declaration of Helsinki. 101 

Physical activity assessment 102 

Physical activity for each participant was quantified using a triaxial accelerometer (GT3X; 103 ActiGraph, Pensacola, FL, USA) worn around the waist for at least 4 days (2 weekdays and 2 104 weekend days) as reported previously (Hassanlouei et al. , 2017; Sundberg et al. , 2018a). The data 105 were recorded for each participant if the accelerometer was worn for a minimum of 8 hours on at 106 least 3 days (Hart et al. , 2011). 107 

Experimental overview 108 

Participants reported to the laboratory on two occasions, once for a muscle biopsy and 109 again to measure thigh lean mass and whole-muscle function of the knee extensor muscles. The 110 purpose of the whole muscle session was to determine whether older adults exhibited typical age-111 related differences in knee extensor mass and function, including lower thigh lean mass as well as 112 lower absolute and mass-specific mechanical force and power outputs. 113

Thigh lean mass 115 

Body composition and thigh lean mass were assessed with dual X-ray absorptiometry 116 (Lunar iDXA; GE, Madison, WI, USA). Thigh lean mass was quantified for the region of interest 117 using the manufacturer’s software (enCORE 14.10.022; GE), with the distal demarcation set at the 118 tibiofemoral joint and the proximal demarcation set as a diagonal bifurcation through the femoral 119 neck. DXA measures of thigh lean mass with these landmarks are strongly correlated with 120 measures from magnetic resonance imaging but underestimate the age-related loss in thigh muscle 121 mass (Maden-Wilkinson et al. , 2013). 122 

Whole-muscle knee extensor function 123 

Whole-muscle function of the knee extensors was measured using the same experimental 124 setup described previously (Sundberg et al. , 2018b). Briefly, participants were seated upright in 125 the high Fowler’s position on a Biodex System 4 dynamometer (Biodex Medical, Shirley, NY, 126 USA) with the dominant leg (preferred kicking leg) positioned at 90° knee flexion. Participants 127 were secured using the dynamometer’s four-point restraint system to minimize extraneous 128 movements and changes in hip angle, and they were prohibited from grasping the dynamometer 129 with their hands to ensure the measured torques and velocities were generated primarily by the 130 knee extensor muscles. 131 

Following a standardized warmup consisting of dynamic knee extension exercise lifting a 132 light load (1 Nm), participants performed a minimum of three brief (2-3 s) maximal voluntary 133 isometric contractions (MVCs) separated by a minimum of 60 s rest. Strong verbal encouragement 134 and visual feedback were provided via a 56 cm monitor mounted 1-1.5 m directly in front of the 135 participant. MVC torque was quantified as the average torque over a 0.5 s interval centered on 136 peak torque, and MVC attempts were continued until the two highest values were within 5% of 137

Muscle stimulation and electrically evoked contractile properties 141 

Involuntary contractile properties of the knee extensors were assessed using transcutaneous 142 electrical stimulation delivered through custom pad electrodes (6 × 8 cm), with the cathode 143 positioned approximately 8–12 cm proximal to the superior border of the patella and the anode 144 was positioned 10–13 cm proximal to the cathode. Stimulations were delivered using a constant-145 current stimulator (DS7AH, Digitimer, Hertfordshire, UK), and the stimulator intensity established 146 using single-pulse stimuli (400 V, 200 μs duration) beginning at 50 mA and increasing 147 incrementally by 50–100 mA until the amplitude of the unpotentiated resting twitch torque 148 plateaued. Stimulus intensity was then increased by an additional 20% to ensure supramaximal 149 stimulation. This intensity was subsequently used for all paired-pulse (100 Hz doublet) 150 stimulations during the voluntary activation and contractile property assessments. 151 

Participants performed MVCs during which a 100 Hz paired-pulse stimulus was delivered 152 both during the contraction and immediately following the MVC (< 5 s). Each MVC trial was 153 separated by at least 2 min of rest. The reported values for potentiated resting twitch amplitude 154 (Nm), half-relaxation time (ms), and peak rate of torque development (Nm·s -1 ) were calculated as 155 the median values from the four MVC trials. The peak rate of torque development was quantified 156 as the derivative of the torque signal as the highest rate of torque increase over a 10 ms interval. 157

Muscle biopsy 175 

A muscle biopsy from the vastus lateralis was obtained from each participant using the 176 modified Bergström technique as described previously (Bergstrom, 1975; Sundberg et al. , 2018a). 177 Participants were instructed to abstain from strenuous exercise of the lower limbs for 48 h prior to 178 the biopsy and arrive at the laboratory fasted and without the consumption of caffeine for ≥ 8 h. 179 Longitudinal bundles from the biopsy sample were immediately placed in ice cold glycerol 180 skinning solution (see below) and stored at -20 °C for up to 4 weeks. The remaining bundles of 181 the biopsy sample were orientated longitudinally on a small notecard, flash frozen in liquid 182 nitrogen-cooled isopentane and stored at -80 °C until sectioned for immunohistochemistry (IHC). 183

Single fiber morphology and contractile mechanics experiments 184 

Solutions: The composition of the relaxing (pCa 9.0, where pCa = -log[Ca 2+ ]) and 185 activating (pCa 4.5) solutions used for the single fiber contractile experiments were derived from 186 an iterative program using the stability constants adjusted for temperature, pH, and ionic strength 187 (Fabiato & Fabiato, 1979; Fabiato, 1988). The solutions contained (in mM): 20 imidazole, 7 188 EGTA, 4 MgATP and 14.5 creatine phosphate. Inorganic phosphate (P i ) was added as K 2 HPO 4 to 189 yield a concentration of 4 mM. The ionic strength was adjusted to 180 mM with KCl, and the pH 190 adjusted to 7.0 with KOH and HCl. The skinning solution was composed of 50% relaxing solution 191 and 50% glycerol (vol:vol). 192 

Experimental setup 193 

Single fiber segments ~5-10 mm in length were isolated from the biopsy sample and 194 prepared as described previously (Sundberg et al. , 2018a). Briefly, the ends of the fiber were 195 secured with 4.0 monofilament posts tied with 10.0 nylon sutures to a force transducer (400A; 196 Aurora Scientific, Aurora, Ontario, CA) and a high-speed servomotor (controller 312B; Aurora 197 Scientific) with ~2-3 mm of the fiber between the two attachment points. Fibers were suspended 198 in 100 μl of relaxing solution kept at 16 °C with a temperature-controlled Peltier unit for the 199 duration of the experiment, except when transferred into air for imaging or to a second Peltier unit 200 for activation at 20 °C. To view the fiber at 800x magnification, the microsystem was transferred 201 to the stage of an inverted microscope. The sarcomere length was adjusted to 2.5 μm using a 202 calibrated eyepiece micrometer, and the fiber length measured as the distance between the two 203 attachment points via a mechanical micrometer (Starrett; Athol, MA, USA). 204

Single fiber fluorescent staining 233 

Following single fiber contractile experiments, fibers were carefully transferred and the 234 ends secured with 10.0 nylon sutures between two small-diameter (800 μm) stainless-steel rods 235 within a custom-built imaging microchamber (Fig. 1). Each rod was fixed to maintain fiber 236 alignment between the attachment points but could be adjusted in two planes to control sarcomere 237 length and fiber height. Due to the mounting procedure on the single fiber contractile apparatus 238 (Moss, 1979), both ends of the fiber segments became crimped at the point of attachment, creating 239 visible landmarks that were used to ensure consistent positioning of the same fiber segment within 240 the 3D imaging microchamber for subsequent imaging. Fibers were then stored overnight in 241 relaxing solution at 4°C. 242 

On the day of staining and imaging, fibers were first incubated for 15 min in 0.1% Triton 243 X-100 (T9284; Sigma-Aldrich) diluted in relaxing solution at room temperature (RT) to facilitate 244 penetration of the fluorescent probe. After a 5 min wash in relaxing solution, fibers were incubated 245 for 60 min in Alexa Fluor 568 phalloidin (1:1000, A12830; Invitrogen) diluted in relaxing solution 246 at RT. Fibers were then washed 3x5 min in relaxing solution before sarcomere length was adjusted 247 for imaging. 248

DISCUSSION 474 

The present study tested whether intrinsic contractile function of human skeletal muscle 475 fibers is impaired in older adults by integrating single fiber contractile mechanics with 3D 476 morphological imaging of the same fibers. We also evaluated the agreement between conventional 477 methods of estimating fiber CSA from 2D images in solution and air with CSA-derived 478 measurements from 3D imaging (Fig. 3). As expected, peak power of the knee extensor muscles 479 was closely associated with the involuntary, electrically evoked contractile properties of the 480

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 12, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.09.730973 doi: bioRxiv preprint

potentiated twitch, but not with voluntary activation, suggesting the age-related decline in peak 481 power is due primarily to factors within the muscle (Wrucke et al. , 2024). Consistent with our 482 previous work (Sundberg et al. , 2018a; Teigen et al. , 2020; Sundberg et al. , 2025; Teigen et al. ,483 2026), absolute P o and peak power were markedly lower in fast MyHC II fibers from older adults 484 but did not differ with age in slow MyHC I fibers. However, when normalized to CSA estimated 485 from 2D images in air or derived from 3D imaging, size-specific force and power of both MyHC 486 I and II fibers either did not differ with age or were greater in older compared with young adults, 487 indicating preserved intrinsic contractile function with aging. In contrast, normalization to CSA 488 estimated from 2D images in solution resulted in lower size-specific power in MyHC I fibers and 489 lower size-specific P o in MyHC II fibers in the older adults (Figs. 6 and 7). Importantly, all 2D 490 methods systematically overestimated 3D-derived fiber CSA, with solution-based estimates 491 demonstrating the greatest bias and poorest agreement with 3D imaging (Fig. 3). These data 492 suggest that fast fiber atrophy is a major contributor to declines in whole muscle force and power 493 in older adults, and that conventional methods to estimate fiber CSA can influence conclusions 494 regarding age-related differences in intrinsic contractile function, providing a probable explanation 495 for the longstanding inconsistencies in the literature. 496 

Intrinsic contractile function is preserved with aging despite pronounced fast fiber atrophy 497 

A novel aspect of the present study was the development of a custom imaging 498 microchamber system (Fig. 1) that enabled integration of single fiber contractile mechanics with 499 high-resolution 3D morphological measurements of the same fiber segments at a fixed sarcomere 500 length (2.5 μm). This approach eliminated the reliance on assumptions of fiber cross-sectional 501 shape that is inherent to conventional single fiber methods and allowed CSA to be quantified along 502 the length of the fiber (~635 μm) at a spatial resolution of 0.38 μm 2 . Using this approach, we found 503

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 12, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.09.730973 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Consistent with this 505 interpretation, we also observed no age-related differences in V max or the rate of tension 506 redevelopment (k tr ), both of which reflect intrinsic contractile kinetics that are independent of fiber 507 size. Furthermore, by directly comparing 3D-derived CSA with estimates obtained from 508 conventional 2D imaging methods, the present study provides insight into the longstanding 509 inconsistencies in the single fiber literature regarding whether intrinsic contractile function is 510 impaired with aging. 511 

As expected, fast MyHC II fibers from older adults exhibited lower CSA (Fig. 4B), absolute 512 force (P o ) (Fig. 5B), and peak power (Fig. 7B), with no age-related differences observed in slow 513 MyHC I fibers, a finding consistent with our previous work (Sundberg et al. , 2018a; Teigen et al. ,514 2020; Sundberg et al. , 2025; Teigen et al. , 2026) and others (Gries et al. , 2019; Grosicki et al. ,515 2021). However, when CSA estimated from 2D images in solution was used for size normalization, 516 size-specific power in MyHC I fibers and size-specific P o in MyHC II fibers were lower in older 517 adults, which would suggest that aging impairs intrinsic contractile function (Figs. 6 and 7). 518 Notably, nearly all studies reporting lower size-specific P o in slow MyHC I or fast MyHC II fibers 519 from older adults also estimated fiber size in solution (Larsson et al. , 1997; Frontera et al. , 2000; 520 D'Antona et al. , 2003; D'Antona et al. , 2007; Ochala et al. , 2007; Yu et al. , 2007; Hvid et al. , 2013; 521 Lamboley et al. , 2015; Brocca et al. , 2017). In contrast, only one study using air-based 522 measurements reported an age-related reduction in size-specific P o in MyHC I fibers, with no 523 difference in MyHC IIa fibers (Harber et al. , 2012). In the present study, CSA estimated in solution 524 was markedly larger than values obtained from air-based measurements or 3D imaging (Fig. 4), 525 which would systematically reduce size-specific P o and power. Consistent with this observation, 526

Degens and Larsson (2007) reported that size-specific P o based on CSA estimated in solution 527 assuming a circular shape was less than 70% of that when assuming an elliptical shape or when 528 CSA was estimated in air. This is comparable to the present findings, in which size-specific P o and 529 power measured in solution were ~12-44% lower than values derived from air and 3D 530 measurements across both fiber types and independent of age. 531 

One potential explanation for the larger CSA values observed from 2D images in solution 532 is fiber swelling following chemical permeabilization, which is commonly reported to increase 533 fiber dimensions by ~20% (Godt & Maughan, 1977; Frontera et al. , 2000; Ochala et al. , 2007; Yu 534 

et al. , 2007), although the extent of swelling can vary considerably between individuals and fiber 535 types (Monti et al. , 2021). However, because both the solution-based CSA estimates from 2D 536 images and the 3D imaging in the present study were obtained under similar conditions (i.e., 2.5 537 μm sarcomere spacing in relaxing solution), any global swelling would be expected to affect both 538 approaches similarly. Additionally, among the studies that estimated CSA in solution and reported 539 lower size-specific P o in fibers from older adults, only 3 of 9 accounted for fiber swelling (Frontera 540 

et al. , 2000; Ochala et al. , 2007; Yu et al. , 2007), suggesting that swelling alone is unlikely to 541 explain the discrepancies in the literature. Instead, our data suggests that the most probable 542 explanation is due to limitations inherent to 2D projection-based measurements and assumptions 543 of fiber geometry. Indeed, fibers from older adults, particularly MyHC II fibers, deviate 544 substantially from simple cylindrical or elliptical geometries (Kirkeby & Garbarsch, 2000; 545 Barnouin et al. , 2017; Messa et al. , 2020; Horwath et al. , 2024; Soendenbroe et al. , 2024). 546 Consistent with this observation, transferring fibers from solution to air in the present study 547 resulted in a much greater reduction in CSA in fibers from older (26%) compared with young 548 adults (7%), especially in MyHC II fibers. These findings indicate that fibers, particularly those 549

In contrast to the findings obtained using CSA estimated in solution, air-based 553 measurements and 3D imaging yielded consistent evidence of preserved intrinsic contractile 554 function with aging. However, air-based measurements may also introduce systematic bias, albeit 555 in the opposite direction. Indeed, in the present study, size-specific power was greater in fast 556 MyHC II fibers from older compared with young adults, while 3D imaging indicated no 557 differences (Fig. 7). Notably, most studies reporting higher size-specific P o or peak power in slow 558 MyHC I or fast MyHC II fibers from older adults also estimated fiber size while fibers were 559 suspended in air (Trappe et al. , 2003; Sundberg et al. , 2018a; Gries et al. , 2019; Grosicki et al. ,560 2021; Sundberg et al. , 2025). In contrast, only two studies using solution-based CSA estimates 561 reported greater size-specific P o in MyHC IIa fibers from older adults (Miller et al. , 2013; Straight 562 

et al. , 2018). Importantly, both studies used a prism to obtain side-view images of the fibers and 563 estimated CSA assuming an elliptical geometry, suggesting that air-based measurements and 564 solution-based measurements incorporating elliptical assumptions using a prism may similarly 565 bias conclusions toward apparent enhancements in intrinsic contractile function with aging. These 566 findings, when interpreted together, provide compelling evidence that methodological differences 567 in CSA estimation, whether performed in solution or air, can bias the conclusions in potentially 568 opposite directions, and contribute to the longstanding inconsistencies in the literature regarding 569 altered intrinsic contractile function with aging. 570 

An important limitation of the present study is that CSA estimates from the 2D images 571 were calculated assuming fibers formed a circular cross-sectional shape, whereas many previous 572

For example, second harmonic generation microscopy of skinned rabbit 575 soleus fibers showed that CSA estimates using circular assumptions resulted in a modestly greater 576 overestimation of real CSA (5.3 ± 25.9%) compared with elliptical assumptions (2.8 ± 6.9%) 577 (Mebrahtu et al. , 2024). These findings are consistent with earlier work in isolated frog fibers 578 showing that CSA estimates based on a single width measurement are more prone to error than 579 estimates incorporating measurements from multiple axes (Blinks, 1965). However, a recent study 580 in human skeletal muscle fibers reported no age-related differences in size-specific P o when CSA 581 was estimated assuming either circular or elliptical geometry (Kalakoutis et al. , 2023). 582 Furthermore, a systematic review and meta-analysis demonstrated substantial inter-study 583 variability in size-specific P o of both fiber types from healthy young adults even after accounting 584 for factors such as fiber swelling, sarcomere length, and assumptions of cross-sectional shape 585 (Kalakoutis et al. , 2021). Collectively, these findings suggest that assumptions regarding fiber 586 geometry likely contribute to discrepancies in the literature regarding size-specific contractile 587 function. However, additional studies directly comparing the methodological approaches are 588 needed to determine the extent to which these inconsistencies reflect differences in measurement 589 techniques versus true biological variability. 590 

Despite the methodological implications with estimating fiber CSA from 2D images, the 591 convergence of findings from the 3D imaging, air-based CSA measurements, and size-independent 592 contractile measurements (k tr and V max ) provide compelling evidence that intrinsic contractile 593 function is largely preserved with aging, and that the primary age-related alteration at the single 594 fiber level is the markedly lower absolute force and power output of fast fibers (Figs. 5B and 7B). 595

Given that fast fibers generate ~0.3-2.5-fold greater size-specific force and power than slow fibers, 596 irrespective of the CSA measurement approach (Figs. 6C and 7C), their selective atrophy would 597 be expected to disproportionately reduce whole muscle force and power relative to the loss of 598 muscle mass. Accordingly, the power output of the fast MyHC II fibers was closely associated 599 with whole muscle power, whereas there was no association with slow MyHC I fiber power (Fig. 600 2B). When considered alongside the ~46% lower fast MyHC II proportional area in older adults 601 and the age-related reductions in mass-specific torque (~22%) and power (~31%), these findings 602 collectively implicate fast fiber atrophy as an important factor contributing to the reduced whole 603 muscle function with aging. Notably, multiple variables obtained from the involuntary, electrically 604 evoked twitch, but not voluntary activation, were closely associated with knee extensor power 605 output, providing direct evidence that the age-related loss in peak power is driven primarily by 606 factors within the muscle. Future studies focused on identifying the mechanisms contributing to 607 age-related fast fiber atrophy will be important for developing targeted therapeutic strategies to 608 attenuate the loss in muscle strength and power with advancing age. 609 

Conclusions 610 

This study demonstrates that methods commonly used in single fiber contractile mechanics 611 experiments for estimating CSA from 2D images overestimate values obtained from high-612 resolution 3D imaging, which has important implications for calculating size-specific force and 613 power. As expected, absolute force and power output were lower in fast fibers from older compared 614 with young adults, whereas no age-related differences were observed in slow fibers. Measurements 615 of size-specific force and power when CSA was estimated in solution were lower in the older 616 compared with young adults but were higher or not different when size was determined from air-617 based measurements or 3D imaging.
