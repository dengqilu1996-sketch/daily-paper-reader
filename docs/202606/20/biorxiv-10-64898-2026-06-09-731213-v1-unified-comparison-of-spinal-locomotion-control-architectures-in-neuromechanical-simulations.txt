## Introduction 45 

Neuromechanical simulations offer a powerful computational testbed for studying how the ner-46 

vous system generates and regulates human locomotion, a long-standing scientific problem with 47 

far-reaching implications for neurorehabilitation, assistive device design, and human performance 48 

enhancement (Song & Geyer, 2018). Experimentally identifying the underlying control algorithms 49 

and neural circuitry that produce coordinated walking is not feasible with current techniques. Hu-50 

man locomotion emerges from the interaction between large-scale neural networks and the nonlin-51 

ear physics of the musculoskeletal system, including muscle tendon dynamics, contact forces, and 52 

whole-body mechanics, making it challenging to disentangle the structural organization of control 53 

from the resulting movement. Directly measuring or mapping the activity of the vast neuronal popu-54 

lations, particularly during natural unconstrained movement, remains beyond current experimental 55

A wide range of biologically inspired locomotion control models have been proposed and evalu-61 

ated in neuromechanical simulations, most of which focus on generating stereotypical steady gait 62 

through low-level automatic control mechanisms commonly attributed to spinal circuitry. These 63 

mechanisms are typically framed in terms of spinal reflexes, central pattern generators (CPGs), 64 

and muscle synergies. Spinal reflex pathways transform proprioceptive and cutaneous sensory 65 

signals into rapid, phase-dependent modulation of motor output, and experimental studies in hu-66 

mans using methods such as H-reflex modulation, stretch perturbations, and cutaneous stimula-67 

tion provide direct evidence for their involvement in regulating gait and responding to perturbations 68 

(Dietz, 2002; Nielsen, 2003). CPGs refer to neural circuits capable of generating rhythmic motor 69 

patterns through networks of reciprocally interacting neurons, often modeled using coupled neural 70 

oscillators; although extensive experimental work in vertebrate animal preparations demonstrates 71 

that such circuits can produce locomotor rhythms without sensory feedback, evidence for their 72 

functional role in intact human walking remains indirect and debated (Grillner, 2006; Ijspeert, 73 

2008). Muscle synergies, in contrast, are not typically interpreted as mechanisms for generating 74 

neural drive, but rather as a strategy for coordinating and distributing motor commands across 75 

groups of muscles through low-dimensional activation modules inferred from electromyographic 76 

(EMG) recordings (Ivanenko et al., 2004). Circuit-level studies in animal models support the 77 

existence of modular spinal organization consistent with muscle synergies, but whether the low-78 

dimensional modules identified from human electromyographic data directly reflect such neural cir-79 

cuitry or instead partly emerge from biomechanical and task constraints remains debated (Tresch 80 

& Jarc, 2009). Reflecting these uncertainties, neuromechanical locomotion models often incor-81 

porate different combinations of reflex feedback, rhythm-generating circuits, and synergy-based 82 

coordination to represent spinal-level locomotor control. Representative implementations of these 83 

control principles in neuromechanical simulations are summarized in the subsection Existing neu-84 

romechanical locomotion control models below. 85

likeness refers to how closely simulated gait reproduces experimentally observed biomechanical 104 

and energetic characteristics, whereas versatility reflects the range of speed and slope conditions 105 

over which stable gait can be generated. This unified approach allows us to isolate how differences 106 

in implemented control architectures within each hypothesis influence gait realism and adaptabil-107

on an explicit CPG. Günther and Ruder (2003) proposed a walking control model rooted in the 121 

equilibrium point hypothesis (Feldman & Levin, 1995), in which muscle activation arises from de-122 

viations between actual and reference muscle lengths and velocities. A widely adopted model in 123 

this class is the sagittal-plane muscle-reflex model of Geyer and Herr (2010), which demonstrated 124 

human-like walking dynamics and muscle activation patterns with substantially improved agree-125 

ment with experimental observations relative to earlier models. In this model, reflex pathways were 126

CPG-reflex-based control models generate locomotion through an internal rhythmic drive, typi-146 

cally implemented using neural oscillators, together with sensory feedback pathways that modulate 147 

the locomotor pattern. To our knowledge, the earliest forward simulations of human locomotion 148 

control using biologically inspired control models were introduced by Taga (1995), who demon-149 

strated that stable walking patterns can emerge in a torque-driven multibody model through neural 150 

oscillators based on the formulation proposed by Matsuoka (1985). Building on this framework, 151 

Yamazaki, Hase, and colleagues developed what is, to our knowledge, the first neuromechanical 152 

model of human walking, integrating a motor control model with a musculoskeletal system in a for-153 

ward dynamics simulation (Yamazaki et al., 1996). Their subsequent studies further investigated 154 

the integration and relative contributions of proprioceptive reflexes and CPGs in generating loco-155 

motion (Ogihara & Yamazaki, 2001), and later extended this line of work to what is likely the first 156 

three-dimensional neuromechanical simulation of human walking (Hase & Yamazaki, 2002). Later 157 

studies explored variations of this CPG-reflex architecture by modifying oscillator formulations or 158

Muscle synergy-based control models generate muscle activation patterns through low-dimensional 164 

control signals that combine fixed synergy weights with time-varying activation pulses. Because 165 

muscle synergies primarily describe how a small number of activation modules are distributed 166 

across muscles rather than how these modules are generated, muscle synergy-based control ar-167 

chitectures typically require an additional mechanism to produce the temporal activation signals. 168 

In many implementations this rhythmic drive is generated by CPG-like mechanisms, while stability 169 

is maintained through simple feedback rules, often without explicitly modeling spinal reflex path-170 

ways. One of the earliest neuromechanical implementations of this concept was proposed by Jo 171 

and Massaquoi (2007), who constructed a walking controller in which the gait cycle was partitioned 172 

into discrete temporal windows associated with predefined muscle synergies. Aoi and colleagues 173 

developed a neuromechanical model in which temporal activation pulses with adjustable onset 174 

timing, duration, and amplitude drove predefined muscle synergies (Aoi et al., 2008). Subse-175 

quent studies within this framework incorporated additional control components, such as phase-176 

resetting mechanisms (Aoi et al., 2010; Tamura et al., 2020) and speed regulation modules (Aoi 177 

et al., 2019), to examine how synergy-based control interacts with sensory feedback and task 178 

constraints in generating stable and adaptable locomotion. 179 

CPG-reflex-synergy-based control models integrate rhythmic pattern generation, sensory feed-180 

back, and modular muscle coordination within a unified architecture. Such a hierarchical organi-181 

zation, in which central pattern generators provide rhythmic drive while sensory feedback and 182 

modular coordination shape muscle activation, is widely considered a plausible organization of 183 

spinal locomotor circuits in vertebrates (Danner et al., 2017). Recent neuromechanical simulations 184 

have explored implementations of this combined structure to integrate the complementary roles 185 

of CPG-based rhythm generation, reflex-mediated feedback control, and synergy-based muscle 186 

coordination within a single locomotion control framework (Di Russo et al., 2023). 187

## Methods 188 

In this section, we first describe the four locomotion control models examined in this study, 189 

each representing one of the control strategies introduced above. We then present the unified 190 

neuromechanical simulation framework used to evaluate these controllers, which includes a 2-D 191 

sagittal-plane neuromusculoskeletal model and a standardized optimization procedure designed 192 

to assess gait performance in terms of human-likeness and versatility. 193 

Model selection and controller descriptions 194 

We selected representative models from each of the four major control strategies: reflex-195 

based, CPG-reflex-based, muscle synergy-based, and CPG-reflex-synergy-based approaches. 196 

Selection was guided by physiological plausibility, demonstrated capability to generate human-197 

like and diverse locomotor behaviors, and how clearly each model represents the conceptual 198 

and historical foundation of its respective control architecture. For each model, we preserved 199 

the core control architecture while standardizing implementation details required for a consistent 200 

comparison within our simulation framework. Modifications to the control models were limited 201 

to those necessary to ensure compatibility with the shared musculoskeletal model and, when 202 

necessary, to maintain physiologically plausible gait behavior under these common conditions. 203 

The following subsections describe each selected model by briefly justifying its selection, sum-204 

marizing the key elements of its control formulation, and specifying any implementation modifi-205 

cations made within our unified framework. Readers are referred to the original publications for 206 

detailed descriptions of each model (reflex-based (Song & Geyer, 2015a), CPG-reflex-based (Ogi-207 

hara & Yamazaki, 2001), muscle synergy-based (Aoi et al., 2019), and CPG-reflex-synergy-based 208 

(Di Russo et al., 2023)), as well as to the open-source implementation provided with this study 209 

(https:// doi.or g/ 10.5281/ zenodo.20509551 ). 210 

Reflex-based model 211 

The model of Song and Geyer (2015a) was selected to represent the reflex-based control 212 

strategy because it drives locomotion entirely by sensorimotor feedback, without an explicit CPG, 213 

designed in a manner that closely reflects the proprioceptive, vestibular, and cutaneous pathways 214 

of the nervous system. Beyond its physiological plausibility, this model has demonstrated a broad 215 

range of locomotor behaviors, including three-dimensional walking and turning, slope ascent and 216

The controller is organized hierarchically into spinal and supraspinal layers. The spinal layer 220 

consists of ten modules divided between stance (M1–M5) and swing (M6–M10), each responsible 221 

for specific biomechanical functions such as compliant load support of the stance leg, effective 222 

swing toward target foot placement, and trunk balance. Muscle stimulation is generated through 223 

delayed feedback from muscle force, length, and velocity signals, representing afferent inputs from 224 

Golgi tendon organs and muscle spindles, along with additional sensory signals such as trunk 225 

orientation and leg load. These feedback pathways are parameterized through gains and offsets 226 

that shape muscle activation patterns. The supraspinal layer modulates foot placement based on 227 

hip position and velocity relative to the stance foot and coordinates gait phase transitions between 228 

stance and swing. 229 

We implemented the 2-D version of this model (Song & Geyer, 2017, 2018; Ton et al., 2024) 230 

that omits hip adduction and abduction degrees of freedom while retaining all other control com-231 

ponents, resulting in 64 optimization parameters. 232 

CPG-reflex-based model 233 

We selected the model of Ogihara and Yamazaki (2001) as representative of the CPG-reflex 234 

control strategy because it provides a clear and compact implementation of a rhythm generator 235 

coupled with sensory feedback within a neuromechanical simulation. This formulation directly ex-236 

tends the classical CPG-based locomotion framework introduced by Taga (1995), in which rhyth-237 

mic neural activity interacts with body dynamics to produce stable gait. We also considered the 238 

model of Hase and Yamazaki (2002), but it relies on an optimization procedure at each simula-239 

tion timestep to convert joint torques into muscle activations, which we considered physiologically 240 

implausible. 241 

The controller follows a CPG-reflex architecture in which a rhythmic drive provides a base-242 

line activation pattern, while sensory feedback modulates timing and amplitude at the motoneu-243 

ron level. Rhythm generation is implemented using a Matsuoka oscillator, consisting of mutu-244 

ally inhibiting neurons with adaptation that produce alternating flexor–extensor activity (Matsuoka, 245 

1985). Sensory feedback is incorporated through simplified neuron models representing muscle 246 

spindles and Golgi tendon organs, providing signals related to muscle length, velocity, and force. 247

Unified simulation framework and evaluation 313 

We evaluated the ability of each control model to generate human-like gait and its perfor-314 

mance across speeds and slopes using a unified neuromechanical simulation framework in the 315 

two-dimensional sagittal plane. In this framework, each controller drives a shared musculoskeletal 316 

model with nine muscles per leg, providing muscle stimulation inputs while interacting with iden-317 

tical biomechanical dynamics and simulation conditions. Gait behaviors were obtained through 318 

a standardized optimization procedure. The framework was implemented in MATLAB/Simulink 319 

(2024b) with the Simscape Multibody toolbox for rigid-body dynamics, and forward dynamics were 320 

computed using the ode15s solver. 321 

Musculoskeletal model 322 

We used the neuromechanical model of Song and Geyer (2018) with minor modifications. 323 

Specifically, to provide a more general and consistent framework across controllers, we adjusted 324 

the engagement threshold of the passive knee flexion torque preventing hyperextension to remove 325 

the earlier bias toward slight knee flexion during stance. In addition, we increased Achilles tendon 326

## Discussion 466 

This study shows that specific implementations of locomotion control architectures can mean-467 

ingfully shape both the human-likeness and versatility of neuromechanical simulations under con-468 

trolled biomechanical and computational conditions. By implementing four representative con-469 

trollers within a unified musculoskeletal model, optimization procedure, and evaluation framework, 470 

we isolated differences attributable to control organization rather than to differences in body me-471 

chanics or simulation protocol. The CPG-reflex-synergy model of Di Russo et al. (2023) achieved 472 

the strongest overall performance across the evaluated criteria, producing human-like gait pat-473 

terns and the widest range of walking speeds and slopes. The reflex-based model of Song and 474 

Geyer (2015a) showed similarly strong human-likeness and competitive versatility, whereas the 475 

muscle synergy-based model of Aoi et al. (2019) and the CPG-reflex-based model of Ogihara and 476 

Yamazaki (2001) showed less realistic gait patterns and more restricted adaptability. These results 477 

indicate that the organization of neural control, as instantiated in specific model formulations, is a 478 

23 

.CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 12, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.09.731213 doi: bioRxiv preprint major determinant of simulated locomotor behavior. 479 

The two best-performing models shared a common feature: their control signals were grounded 480 

in the mechanical state of the body and its interaction with the environment. In the reflex-based 481 

model, muscle activation is continuously modulated by sensory feedback related to muscle state, 482 

limb loading, and trunk orientation, while stance and swing reflex circuits are coordinated through 483 

gait events such as ground contact. In the CPG-reflex-synergy model, muscle activation is continu-484 

ously modulated through reflex pathways, in addition to the CPG-generated rhythm being synchro-485 

nized with body–environment interaction through phase resetting at heel-strike. Both models also 486 

incorporate interlimb coordination, through contralateral leg feedback in the reflex-based model 487 

and bilateral CPG coupling in the CPG-reflex-synergy model. In contrast, the CPG-reflex and 488 

muscle synergy models rely more heavily on internally generated timing, with less explicit sensory 489 

anchoring of the locomotor pattern and less direct coupling between limbs. These differences may 490 

explain why the reflex-based and CPG-reflex-synergy models produced stable gait across broader 491 

ranges of speeds and slopes: successful walking requires not only generating rhythmic activation 492 

patterns, but also continuously modulating those patterns according to body state and mechani-493 

cal events. This control requirement is captured in forward-dynamic simulations, where gait must 494 

emerge from transient initial conditions and remain stable despite step-to-step variability. In con-495 

trast, trajectory optimization approaches are well suited for identifying optimal periodic motions but 496 

do not typically test how a neural controller stabilizes gait from transient or imperfect states unless 497 

feedback, perturbations, or noise are explicitly modeled (De Groote & Falisse, 2021). 498 

Several additional observations emerged from the unified comparison. Notably, the two con-499 

trollers that produced gait less consistent with experimental observations were originally developed 500 

without some key neuromechanical features, such as compliant tendons and physiological trans-501 

mission delays (Table 1), highlighting the importance of neuromechanical modeling detail in eval-502 

uating locomotion control architectures. In addition, controller dimensionality varied substantially 503 

across models, with 41, 64, 99, and 240 parameters for the muscle synergy-based, reflex-based, 504 

CPG-reflex-based, and CPG-reflex-synergy-based controllers, respectively. While the greater di-505 

mensionality of the CPG-reflex-synergy-based controller may provide increased representational 506 

capacity, the reflex-based controller nevertheless achieved comparable overall performance de-507 

spite using substantially fewer parameters. 508 

The results of this study should be interpreted as comparisons of specific model implemen-509 

tations, not as definitive judgments on the underlying biological control hypotheses. Reflexes, 510

models in this study therefore does not imply that CPGs or synergies are unimportant for human 513 

locomotion. Rather, it indicates that the particular formulations tested here were less effective un-514

models with richer sensory feedback, synergy models with stronger event-based timing regula-516 

tion, or hybrid controllers with alternative spinal circuit representations, could produce different 517 

results. The present study should therefore be viewed as a controlled comparison of representa-518 

tive architectures and a tool for identifying useful design principles, rather than as a final ranking 519 

of biological control mechanisms. 520 

More broadly, the limited versatility observed in some controllers should also be interpreted 521 

in the context of the level of control these models were designed to represent. Most biologically 522 

inspired locomotion models, including those examined here, primarily focus on spinal-level mecha-523 

nisms responsible for generating and regulating steady-state locomotion through reflex pathways, 524 

rhythmic circuits, and modular muscle coordination. From this perspective, reduced adaptability 525 

across broader speed and slope conditions does not necessarily invalidate the underlying control 526 

hypothesis, as flexible locomotor behaviors in humans likely depend on additional supraspinal pro-527 

cesses involved in regulating task goals, balance, gait transitions, and environmental adaptation. 528 

In the present framework, such higher-level adaptation was approximated through re-optimization 529 

of controller parameters across conditions rather than through explicit hierarchical modulation dur-530 

ing locomotion itself. The present results therefore motivate future neuromechanical studies that 531 

model locomotion beyond nominal steady gait to investigate more holistic control architectures 532 

that couple spinal locomotor mechanisms with supraspinal systems capable of context-dependent 533 

modulation and task-level regulation. 534 

Limitations 535 

Our unified simulation framework has several limitations related to the simplified musculoskele-536 

tal model and optimization framework. The musculoskeletal model includes several simplifications 537 

that would affect the resulting behaviors of different control models: it is restricted to the sagittal 538 

plane, includes a reduced set of muscles, represents the upper body as a single rigid segment, 539 

and does not include a toe segment. Nevertheless, the selected framework is appropriate for the 540 

present comparison because many of the original neuromechanical control models were either 541

models by the original authors. The optimization framework also imposes assumptions regarding 543 

locomotor goals and optimality principles. In particular, muscle effort (computed as the summed 544 

integral of squared muscle activations) was used as the primary final-stage objective. Although this 545 

objective is widely used in musculoskeletal simulations and has been argued to produce human-546 

like gait patterns (Ackermann & Van den Bogert, 2010; Song & Geyer, 2018), alternative objectives 547 

such as metabolic energy expenditure (Koelewijn et al., 2019; Song & Geyer, 2018) or multi-548 

objective formulations (Veerkamp et al., 2021) may produce different locomotor behaviors and 549

those control formulations in generating human-like gait within the present framework beyond the 553

the obtained solutions may therefore not represent globally optimal locomotor behaviors. 557

because the musculoskeletal model and simulation environment were adapted from prior reflex-560

dynamics, optimization procedure, simulation protocol, and evaluation metrics to all controllers, 562 

and by limiting implementation changes to those required for compatibility or physiologically plau-563 

sible performance. We also modified several aspects of the original framework to reduce behavior-564

removing a passive knee torque threshold that previously prevented full knee extension and incor-566

was used to obtain suitable initial parameter sets for the final optimization stage across all con-570

local minima within practical computational constraints. Moreover, the implemented controllers 572

under alternative modeling assumptions and optimization strategies. 580 

Future Directions 581 

The public framework developed in this study provides a foundation for systematic and ex-582 

tensible comparison of neuromechanical locomotion control models. Future work can implement 583 

additional variants within each control class to determine which representations of reflex path-584 

ways, CPG dynamics, synergy structures, sensory feedback, and phase regulation are most im-585 

portant for generating human-like gait. The framework can also be extended to investigate other 586 

performance dimensions and applications, including robustness to external perturbations, gait 587 

transitions, pathological gait features, motor adaptation and learning, rehabilitation interventions, 588 

and interactions with assistive devices. Beyond controller comparisons, the framework may also 589 

be used to investigate how musculoskeletal morphology, muscle parameters, tendon compliance, 590 

objective functions, optimization algorithms, and physics solvers influence simulated locomotor 591 

behavior. Such studies would help distinguish conclusions that are specific to particular controller 592 

implementations from those that reflect broader interactions among neural control, body mechan-593 

ics, and task constraints. 594 

More broadly, to investigate locomotor behaviors beyond nominal steady gait, future neurome-595 

chanical studies may benefit from more holistic models that explicitly integrate spinal control prim-596 

itives with supraspinal modulation. Rather than treating changes in speed, slope, or task condition 597 

as separate optimization problems, such hierarchical models could include explicit mechanisms 598 

for task-level control, sensory state estimation, balance regulation, motor adaptation, and context-599 

dependent modulation of spinal circuits. Such models would provide a stronger computational 600 

bridge between spinal locomotor mechanisms, supraspinal regulation, and whole-body biome-601 

chanics. Because designing such higher-level control mechanisms manually may become increas-602 

ingly difficult as locomotor tasks and environments grow more complex, reinforcement learning 603 

and related data-driven approaches may provide useful tools for learning supraspinal modulation 604

Kinematic Fitting Results 628 

Figure B1 shows the simulated gait obtained from the kinematic fitting optimization used to 629 

initialize the nominal walking solution. Simulated kinematics, kinetics, and muscle activations 630 

are compared with experimental reference data for walking at 1.2 m s −1. Compared with the 631 

minimum-effort optimization results (Fig. 2), kinematic RMSE was substantially smaller for most 632 

control models. In particular, the reflex-based and CPG-reflex-synergy-based models reproduced 633 

more human-like early-stance knee flexion, and the reflex-based model no longer exhibited exces-634 

sive ankle plantarflexion during early swing. However, improvements in kinematic agreement were 635 

often accompanied by larger deviations in joint kinetics and muscle activation patterns, suggest-636 

ing that optimization based primarily on kinematic matching does not necessarily produce more 637 

physiologically realistic gait in neuromechanical simulations. 638
