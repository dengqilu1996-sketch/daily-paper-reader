# Design of a Low-Latency sEMG Real-Time Correction System Based on High CMRR and EMRMS Mathematical Modeling 

## Lo Hong U, Gao Zibo, and Loi Hoi Fong Col´ egio Diocesano de S˜ ao Jos´ e 5 a, Macau 

Abstract 

Surface electromyography (sEMG) is the most practical non-invasive interface for my-oelectric prostheses, exoskeletons, and rehabilitation systems, but power-line interference (PLI) contamination and excessive digital pipeline group delay still limit its clinical adop-tion. This paper proposes a co-designed analog–digital correction system combining a high-CMRR front-end with an exponentially-windowed RMS (EMRMS) envelope estimator and a recursive single-tone PLI canceller. We present a closed-form CMRR model capturing the electrode-skin imbalance, and provide a complete stability analysis of the LMS canceller. The EMRMS estimator reduces the computational overhead from O(L) to strictly O(1) in both time and space complexities . Featuring no data-dependent branching, the algo-rithm achieves deterministic algorithmic execution time (zero jitter under an RTOS environment) and is natively compatible with fixed-point arithmetic on microcontrollers lacking a hardware Floating-Point Unit (FPU). A reference implementation reaches an 8 .2 μsmedian per-sample latency, yielding an end-to-end delay of ∼ 30 ms — leaving a generous 

>90 ms budget for electromechanical actuation — while requiring an active CPU duty cycle of merely 1 .6%, enabling prolonged deep-sleep intervals. Validation on the public Ninapro DB2 dataset demonstrates a 13 .9 dB mean SNR improvement (averaged across 12 channels; single-channel comparison: 9 .7 dB, Table 3) and a 70 .0 μV envelope RMSE against a length-200 rectangular reference. Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests confirm statistical significance (p < 0.001) over static baselines, and Pearson correlation analysis ( ρ = 0 .993 ± 0.0002) confirms strict morphological fidelity. The full open-source codebase and benchmarks are publicly released. 

Keywords : sEMG, common-mode rejection ratio, exponentially-windowed RMS, real-time signal processing, myoelectric control, LMS adaptive filter, fixed-point arithmetic, embedded edge-computing

# 1 Introduction 

Surface electromyography (sEMG) records the summed electrical activity of the motor units underneath a skin electrode and is now the dominant input modality for myoelectric prostheses, exoskeletons, and human–machine interfaces [Atzori et al., 2014, Caporaso et al., 2025]. Two design axes matter simultaneously. First, the signal of interest is at most a few millivolts and lives between 20 and 450 Hz, while the body acts as an antenna for 50 /60 Hz mains pickup that can reach a few volts in common-mode amplitude; the analog front-end must therefore reject this common-mode signal by at least 100 dB [Wang et al., 2023, Kledrowetz et al., 2023]. Second, the digital pipeline that estimates the EMG envelope and feeds the controller has to do so with very low latency: Farrell and Weir [2007] and Smith et al. [2011] both report that prosthetic users perceive controller delays larger than ∼ 125 ms as sluggish, with completion-rate degradation visible already at 200 ms. Most existing systems address one of the two axes well but not both. 

Analog front-end. The driven-right-leg (DRL) feedback loop introduced by Winter and Web-ster [1983] remains the backbone of every modern biopotential amplifier. Recent designs com-bine an instrumentation amplifier (INA) with DRL feedback to reach a CMRR above 120 dB while keeping power consumption compatible with wearable use: Wang et al. [2023] report a 4-channel wrist-worn sEMG system with CMRR > 120 dB and a passband of 15–500 Hz; Kledrowetz et al. [2023] demonstrate a fully differential analog front-end in 28 nm FDSOI achiev-ing CMRR = 105 .5 dB at 1 V supply; Zhang et al. [2017] reach the same 120 dB target with discrete components; and Cerone et al. [2019] integrate these ideas into a modular high-density wearable. 

Noise suppression and PLI cancellation. Several reviews [Boyer et al., 2023, Caporaso et al., 2025] catalogue the contaminants of sEMG (motion artifact, ECG cross-talk, PLI, white Gaussian noise) and survey suppression techniques: high-pass filtering for motion artifacts, band-stop or adaptive cancellers for PLI, and wavelet denoising for broadband noise [Tabakhi et al., 2023]. Adaptive PLI cancellation without an external reference is itself a substantial research line: Abbaspour et al. [2011] use a recursive least-squares structure; Liu and Cheng [2015] demonstrate that PLI modulation can serve as a motion-artifact reference; Tomasini et al. [2016] implement a multiplier-free feed-forward comb filter for direct PLI cancellation. The classical convergence theory of the LMS family is covered by Haykin [2008], whose stability bound and misadjustment expressions we apply to the single-tone case in Section 2.4. 

Envelope estimation and deployment. The conventional rectangular-window RMS esti-mator is the default in commercial software [Phinyomark et al., 2012], but Ranaldi et al. [2018] propose an information-theoretic adaptive envelope and Nougarou et al. [2023] replace the de-noising stage with a feed-forward comb filter running on an Arduino. On the deployment side, Zanghieri et al. [2020] compress a temporal-CNN sEMG decoder onto a multicore IoT processor, Cˆ ot´ e-Allard et al. [2019] use transfer learning to cut training time by 89%, and Wang et al. [2020] prototype a low-complexity CNN on a Virtex-7 FPGA. The public Ninapro database [At-zori et al., 2014] has become the de facto benchmark; Li et al. [2023] report a CNN-ECA model achieving real-time gesture classification on it. Recent work by Yu et al. [2024] pushes online blind source separation to 12 .5 ms latency, and Belkhiria et al. [2025] place a linear-envelope detector on an FPGA to guarantee deterministic timing. Gao et al. [2018] report end-to-end delays of ∼ 300 ms in a complete exoskeleton system, well above the optimum. 

Identified gaps. The literature reveals two recurring gaps. First, CMRR figures reported by analog designers and envelope-extraction algorithms reported by signal-processing groups are almost never analysed in the same model: the analog stage is summarised by a single number (e.g. “CMRR > 120 dB”), while the digital stage assumes the residual noise it sees is already small. In practice, the residual depends on electrode-skin imbalance and DRL loop gain — parameters that drift during use. Second, the latency budget is rarely accounted for end-to-end. A 250 ms rectangular RMS window costs 125 ms of group delay before the controller even runs, 3

The pipeline achieves an 8 .2 μs median per-sample latency on a standard laptop — well below the 500 μs sample interval of a 2 kHz acquisition — and is validated on the Ninapro DB2 dataset (Section 3.4). 

# 2 Methods and Models 

To contextualise the mathematical models developed below, the complete real-time processing pipeline operates as a serialised dataflow. The continuous biopotential field is first conditioned by the high-CMRR analog front-end to suppress bulk common-mode noise. Upon analog-to-digital conversion at sampling frequency fs, the discrete-time signal sequentially traverses a digital 4th-order Butterworth band-pass filter, a static IIR notch filter, the adaptive single-tone LMS canceller for dynamic PLI tracking, and finally the O(1) EMRMS estimator to output a smooth, low-latency control envelope. 

## 2.1 High-CMRR mathematical model

## 3.2 Latency and envelope quality 

We feed 30 seconds of synthetic sEMG (Gaussian white noise modulated by a Gaussian burst, plus a 3 mV 50 Hz pickup and a 0 .1 mV slow drift) through the streaming pipeline at fs = 2 kHz. Median per-sample latency on a commodity 4-core laptop CPU is 8 .2 μs, with a 99th-percentile of 28 .0 μs — well below the 500 μs sample interval (Fig. 3). The total controller delay is dominated by the EMRMS time constant τ = 25 ms; the band-pass contributes ∼4 ms, the notch ∼1 ms in the EMG passband, the PLI corrector is delay-free, and wall-clock process-ing cost is negligible. The end-to-end delay is therefore ∼30 ms, comfortably below the 125 ms threshold of Farrell and Weir [2007] and the 150–200 ms optimum of Smith et al. [2011]. Since the 2 kHz acquisition rate defines a strict sampling interval of 500 μs, the measured median execution time of 8 .2 μs implies an active CPU duty cycle of 1 .6%. For the remaining 

∼491 μs of each period, the microcontroller can transition into a low-power deep-sleep mode, improving battery autonomy in wearable deployments. The 30 ms end-to-end algorithmic delay consumes only 24% of the 125 ms latency budget, leaving >90 ms for electromechanical actuation and downstream pattern-recognition models.

## 3.5 Step-size sensitivity and tracking under mains drift 

Step-size sweep. We sweep μ ∈ { 10 −4, 10 −3, 10 −2, 10 −1} on a 6 s record with the corrector matched to the actual mains frequency (Fig. 8a). The μ = 10 −4 run does not converge within 6 s, consistent with τLMS = 10 s; the μ = 10 −3 run settles at t ≈ 4 s to a residual of 48 μV (panel b). Larger step sizes converge rapidly but their steady-state residual is barely lower (48–45 μV) because misadjustment (17) becomes dominant: at μ = 10 −1, M ≈ 5% and EMG energy near 50 Hz starts leaking into ˆA, ˆB. The default μ = 10 −3 sits at the knee of the speed/accuracy curve. 

Tracking under mains drift. Figure 8(c) fixes the corrector at fPLI = 50 .0 Hz while the actual mains drifts linearly at 0 .1, 0 .2, and 0 .5 Hz/s. The residual rises monotonically as the single-tone model fails to track the moving target; steady-state residuals are 1 .1, 1 .7, and 2 .0 mV respectively (panel d). This is the expected failure mode of a fixed-frequency model and moti-vates the PLL extension in Section 4. In practice fPLI is initialised from the acquisition system’s line-power monitor at session start, and the worst-case cumulative frequency offset accumulated within a single session is well below 0 .05 Hz; at this offset the residual approaches the stationary 

∼50 μV floor of Fig. 8(a) rather than the drift-induced millivolt range of panel (d).

## 3.6 Limitations 

The proposed pipeline has three principal limitations. First, it is single-channel; spatial PLI cancellation using the common-mode component shared across channels is reserved for future work. Second, the single-tone LMS model assumes a fixed mains frequency: under drift rates above ∼0.1 Hz/s the residual rises to the millivolt range (Fig. 8c–d). Third, the pipeline assumes that broadband motion artefacts have been sufficiently attenuated by the analog high-pass stage. In dynamic scenarios involving violent electrode-skin shifts the EMRMS estimator may track the resulting low-frequency baseline wander; addressing this requires multi-modal sensor fusion (e.g. inertial measurement units), which is outside the scope of the current single-channel myoelectric model. Finally, all latency benchmarks were obtained on a general-purpose laptop OS; actual RTOS jitter behaviour must be characterised on the target embedded hardware. 14

# 4 Future Work 

Four extensions are planned. (i) Multi-channel PLI cancellation. Replacing the single-tone model with a spatial filter that uses the cross-channel common-mode component as a refer-ence [Cerone et al., 2019, Liu and Cheng, 2015] would naturally handle both PLI and electrode motion artefacts. (ii) Frequency-tracking PLL. Integrating a phase-locked loop to jointly up-date the frequency parameter ω[n] alongside the amplitude weights ( ˆA[n], ˆB[n]) would resolve the mains-drift limitation identified in Section 3.6. (iii) End-to-end classifier integration. The EMRMS output already constitutes a real-time feature stream; connecting it directly to a tem-poral CNN [Zanghieri et al., 2020, Li et al., 2023] or a lightweight attention layer would yield a unified deterministic control pipeline. (iv) Embedded hardware port. A port to an STM32F4 microcontroller is planned; standard MCU-to-laptop benchmark ratios suggest ≤40 μs per sam-ple at 168 MHz, leaving ample budget for an on-device classifier. We also plan to validate the CMRR model on bench measurements [Wang et al., 2023] and to compare EMRMS against the information-theoretic estimator of Ranaldi et al. [2018]. 

# 5 Conclusion 

We have presented a co-designed analog–digital sEMG correction system that addresses both the CMRR and latency axes within a single deterministic mathematical framework. The closed-form CMRR model predicts a 120 dB effective CMRR with a moderate 40 dB DRL gain under realistic dry-electrode conditions, matching state-of-the-art analog front-ends. The EMRMS recursion delivers a smooth control envelope at strictly O(1) time and space complexities with deterministic algorithmic execution time, making it well suited for safety-critical RTOS environments and FPU-less embedded platforms. The single-tone LMS canceller ( μ = 10 −3) provides up to ∼50 dB of additional suppression when the corrector frequency matches the actual mains. Validation on Ninapro DB2 yields a mean 13 .9 dB SNR improvement across 12 channels ( p < 0.001), strong morphological fidelity ( ρ = 0 .993) verified via Pearson correlation, and an end-to-end controller delay of ∼30 ms. At an active CPU duty cycle of 1 .6%, the pipeline offers a substantial latency margin for electromechanical actuation and improved battery autonomy for wearable devices. The reference implementation, unit tests, and reproducible benchmarks are released as open-source.
