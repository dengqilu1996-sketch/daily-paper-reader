# Abstract 12 

Total knee replacement restores mobility in patients with advanced osteoarthritis, yet 13 

many individuals still experience limited ability to perform high-flexion tasks such as 14 

squatting. Current preoperative planning relies on static imaging and cannot predict 15 

how different implant alignment choices will affect postoperative dynamic function. This 16 

study developed a predictive simulation framework that uses bi-level inverse optimal 17 

control to link preoperative implant alignment directly to expected postoperative squat 18 

kinematics. 19 

Subject-specific musculoskeletal models were constructed for six total knee replace-20 

ment patients using experimental squat data. Bi-level inverse optimal control was 21 

applied to identify both individualised and group-level cost functions. The individualised 22 

setting provided subject-specific accuracy, while the group-level setting derived a single 23 

group-level cost function as an initial step toward preoperative use without requiring 24 

postoperative motion data. 25 

The individualised setting reproduced experimental trajectories with low errors across 26 

all joints (mean apex difference 1.53 ◦, root-mean-square error 5.15 ◦, normalised root-27 

mean-square error 11.15%, Pearson correlation 0.96). The group-level setting yielded 28 

higher but acceptable errors (mean apex difference 5.70 ◦, root-mean-square error 6.75 ◦,29 

normalised root-mean-square error 17.53%, Pearson correlation 0.95) while preserving 30 

the general pattern and phasing of the motion. Squat depth emerged naturally from the 31 

optimisation rather than being prescribed. 32 

This framework may provide a basis for future quantitative tools to evaluate how im-33 

plant alignment choices influence postoperative squat performance, potentially improving 34 

functional outcomes in total knee replacement. 35 

These results suggest that the proposed IOC framework can reproduce key features 36 

of post-TKR squat kinematics, but further out-of-sample validation is required before 37 

it can be used for preoperative prediction or translated into tools aimed at improving 38 

functional outcomes in total knee replacement. 39

# Introduction 40 

Total knee replacement (TKR) is a well-established procedure for alleviating pain and restoring mobility in 41 

patients with advanced knee conditions such as osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis [1]. While early TKR 42 

focused primarily on pain relief and correction of deformities, modern surgical techniques increasingly emphasise 43 

the restoration of functional capacity, allowing patients to resume daily activities such as squatting [2 –4]. 44 

Despite these advances, 15–20% of TKR patients still report dissatisfaction due to limited range of motion 45 

and difficulties with dynamic tasks [5, 6]. 46 

Current preoperative planning relies largely on static imaging and templating to determine implant 47 

alignment. Surgeons commonly use radiographs or CT scans to position femoral and tibial components 48 

along mechanical or anatomical axes [7]. Although these approaches provide important information for static 49 

correction, they do not directly account for how different alignment choices may influence postoperative 50 

dynamic function during high-flexion activities. Squatting, in particular, requires substantial knee flexion 51 

and is an important indicator of functional recovery, yet current planning workflows have limited ability to 52 

estimate squat depth, joint kinematics, or compensatory movement patterns before surgery [8, 9]. 53 

Squatting consists of distinct phases, including descent, deep flexion, and ascent, each of which places 54 

high demands on knee stability, quadriceps strength, and range of motion. Post-TKR patients may struggle 55 

with these phases due to altered joint mechanics, reduced quadriceps strength, and pain avoidance, resulting 56 

in shallower squat depth and compensatory strategies [10, 11]. Biomechanical indicators such as peak knee 57 

flexion angle are therefore important markers of functional recovery. However, there remains a need for 58 

computational frameworks that can relate implant alignment and patient-specific biomechanics to dynamic 59 

functional outcomes. 60 

Optimisation-based motion prediction has emerged as a promising approach to address this problem. 61 

These methods formulate human movement as the minimisation of a cost function under biomechanical 62 

constraints, allowing motion to be generated without requiring large training datasets [12, 13]. In contrast, 63 

data-driven approaches have shown success for activities such as squatting and running, but often depend on 64 

extensive patient-specific data that may be unavailable or difficult to collect in the TKR population [14 – 16]. 65 

Physics-based optimisation therefore provides a complementary route for investigating how biomechanical 66 

model parameters and surgical alignment choices may affect postoperative movement. 67 

In a preliminary simulation study, we explored lower-limb joint kinematics during squatting using an 68 

earlier torque-driven skeletal model and optimal control formulation, which suggested that TKR component 69 

misalignment can influence squat performance [17]. Building on this foundation, the present study extends 70 

the modelling framework by incorporating both femoral and tibial component alignments, a 3-DOF knee 71 

joint with ligament dynamics, and comparison with experimental squat data from six TKR patients in the 72 

CAMS-Knee dataset [18, 19]. We further employ bi-level inverse optimal control to identify cost-function 73 

weights in both individualised and group-level settings. 74 

The aim of this study was to develop and preliminarily evaluate a bi-level inverse optimal control framework 75 

for reproducing post-TKR squat kinematics and exploring its potential use in future preoperative functional 76 

simulation. The individualised setting was used to assess the best achievable subject-specific reconstruction 77 

accuracy when postoperative motion data are available. In contrast, the group-level setting was used to 78 

examine whether a single population-level cost function could reproduce the general squat kinematic patterns 79 

across subjects, representing an initial step toward preoperative applicability without requiring subject-specific 80 

postoperative motion data. Through these two complementary settings, this study quantifies the trade-off 81 

between subject-specific reconstruction accuracy and population-level generalisability in post-TKR squat 82 

simulation. 83

by Catelli et al. [24], illustrated in Fig 1. This model encompasses 3 DOF in the pelvis (1 rotational and 2 102 

translational), 3 DOF at the hip (all rotational), 3 DOF for knee joint movement (all rotational), 2 DOF for 103 

ankle motion (both rotational), and 1 DOF for lumbar extension (rotational). 104

J1,com (Eq. (4) ) penalises the vertical position of the model’s centre of mass. Because the motion starts 148 

and ends in an upright posture, this term promotes lowering of the body during the intermediate portion of 149 

the motion and therefore allows squat depth to emerge from the optimisation. 150 

J2,balance (Eq. (5) ) penalises the anterior-posterior displacement between the whole-body centre of mass 151 

and a reference position associated with the foot. This term represents a balance-related objective intended 152 

to keep the centre of mass within the support region during squatting. 153 

J3,accel (Eq. (6) ) penalises joint accelerations across all N coordinates in the model. This term acts as a 154 

motion regularisation term and promotes smoother, more controlled trajectories. 155 

J4,effort (Eq. (7) ) penalises actuator excitations. The model employed K torque actuators, with actuator 156 

excitations bounded between −1 and 1, and ek denotes the excitation of the kth actuator. This term 157 

discourages unnecessarily large actuator inputs. 158 

Finally, J5,tension (Eq. (8) ) penalises normalised ligament tension during movement. In this expression, 159 

Tl(t) denotes the tension in the lth ligament at time t, P is the total number of ligaments, and Tmax (t) is the 160 

maximum ligament tension at the same time point. This term was included to discourage movement patterns 161 

associated with excessive ligament loading. 162 

## Bi-level optimisation 163 

Given the cost-function structure in Eqs. 4–8, the corresponding weights must be determined. We therefore 164 

employed a bi-level inverse optimal control (IOC) framework. Bi-level IOC has been widely used in human 165 

motion research to infer cost-function weights from observed movement [31–34]. 166 

In this study, bi-level IOC was used to identify the weight factors associated with post-TKR squat motion. 167 

At the inner level, an OCP (Eq. (3) ) was solved to generate a squat trajectory for a given set of weight factors 168 

w. At the outer level, the derivative-free optimisation algorithm PRIMA [32, 35, 36] adjusted these weights to 169 

minimise the discrepancy between simulated and observed squat kinematics. 170 

Two IOC settings were considered. In the individualised setting, the cost-function weights were optimised 171 

separately for each subject using that subject’s observed squat trajectory. This setting was used to quantify 172 

subject-specific reconstruction accuracy and to provide a benchmark for the best achievable agreement under 173 

the selected model and objective-function structure. Algorithm 1 outlines this individualised bi-level IOC 174 

procedure. 175

## Experimental data 198 

Experimental data were obtained from the CAMS-Knee database [18, 19] and used to estimate cost-function 199 

weights and compare observed joint kinematics with simulated trajectories. The dataset included six subjects 200 

with TKR (5 male, 1 female; mean age 75 ( ±5) years, weight 89 ( ±13) kg, height 172 ( ±4) cm), with data 201 

collected 64–87 months after surgery. 202 

Skin marker trajectories were recorded during squatting. A generic model was scaled and processed using 203 

the OpenSim scaling tool and inverse kinematics solver [20] to obtain lower-limb joint angle trajectories. 204 

The scaled MSK models were assigned component orientations matching those reported in the dataset [37] 205 

to facilitate comparison between simulated and experimental squat kinematics. Replacement component 206 

alignments are summarised in Table 2. Negative values indicate varus alignment and positive values indicate 207

# Results 217 

The bi-level inverse optimal control framework successfully recovered cost-function weights that reproduced 218 

post-total knee replacement (TKR) squat kinematics across all six subjects from the CAMS-Knee dataset. 219 

Agreement between simulated and experimental trajectories was quantified using four complementary metrics: 220 

apex joint-angle difference, defined as the difference in peak flexion between simulated and experimental 221 

trajectories; root-mean-square error (RMSE); normalised RMSE (nRMSE), scaled to each joint’s experimental 222 

range of motion; and Pearson correlation coefficient ( r) for the hip, knee, and ankle flexion/extension 223 

trajectories. Results are presented separately for the individualised subject-specific setting and the group-level 224 

population setting. All simulations satisfied the non-negativity and normalisation constraints on the weight 225 

factors and terminated within the prescribed limits of the upper-level optimisation. 226

# Discussion 272 

This study developed and preliminarily evaluated a bi-level inverse optimal control framework for simulating 273 

post-total knee replacement (TKR) squat kinematics. The framework recovered both individualised and 274 

group-level cost-function weights that allowed the model to reproduce key features of the experimental squat 275 

trajectories from six TKR patients in the CAMS-Knee dataset [18, 19]. In the individualised setting, subject-276 

specific optimisation produced low apex joint-angle differences, RMSE, and nRMSE values, demonstrating 277 

strong agreement with each subject’s observed motion. The group-level setting, in which a single set of 278 

weights was optimised across all subjects, yielded moderately higher errors but maintained high Pearson 279 

correlation coefficients, indicating that the general shape of the squat motion was preserved within the available 280 

cohort. These findings extend our preliminary simulation work [17] by incorporating both femoral and tibial 281 

component alignments, a 3-DOF knee joint with ligament dynamics, and comparison with experimental 282 

squat data from TKR patients. However, both settings showed limited ability to reproduce the temporal 283 

asymmetry in the descent-to-ascent transition timing observed in several experimental trajectories. 284 

The simulation results showed a level of agreement with experimental kinematics that was broadly 285 

comparable to related studies, although direct comparisons should be interpreted cautiously because of 286 

differences in study populations, movement tasks, and modelling approaches. For example, Lee et al. [14] 287 

used a Gaussian process regression model to predict joint angles during smith machine squats in healthy 288 

young subjects, reporting nRMSE values between 3.8% and 17% and Pearson correlation coefficients ( r)289 

ranging from 0.984 to 0.999 for the knee joint. In the present study, the individualised setting produced 290 

knee nRMSE values ranging from 3.07% to 15.19% and r values from 0.91 to 0.99. These values fall within 291 

a comparable range, particularly considering that the present study involved free squatting in older TKR 292 

patients rather than constrained smith machine squats in healthy participants. 293 

Other studies have reported lower errors for knee flexion-extension prediction in gait and running tasks. 294 

Renani et al. [38] and Wouda et al. [15] reported RMSE values of 3.3 ◦ and below 5 ◦, respectively, in healthy 295 

participants. Although these errors are lower than the mean knee RMSE of 5.71 ◦ observed in the individualised 296 

setting of the present study, squat motion involves a larger knee range of motion and greater functional 297 

demand than level gait or treadmill running. In addition, the TKR population is expected to show greater 298 

inter-subject variability because of differences in implant alignment, soft-tissue properties, strength, pain 299 

avoidance, and movement strategy. 300 

Tan et al. [16] investigated knee joint kinematics during sit-to-stand and stand-to-sit motions, which are 301 

mechanically related to the descent and ascent phases of squatting. They reported RMSE values between 302 

7.27 ◦ and 9.30 ◦, nRMSE values from 8.68% to 10.86%, and Pearson correlation coefficients greater than 303 

0.99. In comparison, the present study obtained mean knee RMSE and nRMSE values of 5.71 ◦ and 7.84%, 304 

respectively, in the individualised setting, and 7.17 ◦ and 10.08%, respectively, in the group-level setting. These 305 

comparisons suggest that the proposed simulation framework can reproduce post-TKR squat kinematics 306

The comparison between individualised and group-level cost functions highlights an important trade-off 316 

between subject-specific reconstruction accuracy and population-level generalisability. The individualised 317 

setting produced lower errors because the cost-function weights were optimised separately for each subject 318 

using that subject’s observed motion. This setting should therefore be interpreted as a subject-specific 319 

reconstruction benchmark that indicates the best achievable agreement under the current model structure 320 

and objective terms. In contrast, the group-level setting used a single set of weights for all subjects, making 321 

it more relevant to future preoperative applications where postoperative motion data would not be available 322 

for a new patient. However, this generality came at the cost of higher apex angle differences, RMSE, and 323 

nRMSE, particularly at the knee and ankle. 324 

The recovered weight factors also suggest that a single universal cost function may be insufficient to capture 325 

the full variability of post-TKR squat strategies. The centre-of-mass height term ( wcom ) and balance term 326 

(wbalance ) were consistently among the dominant terms, indicating that lowering the body while maintaining 327 

balance was central to reproducing the squat motion. However, the balance term showed considerable 328 

inter-subject variability, ranging from 0.0950 to 0.4158. This variability may reflect differences in body 329 

size, implant alignment, movement strategy, arm position, confidence during squatting, and individual 330 

prioritisation of stability. The effort-related terms also showed subject-specific variation across coordinates, 331 

further supporting the idea that different patients may use different biomechanical strategies to perform the 332 

same functional task. 333 

A key limitation of the present study is that the group-level cost function was fitted and evaluated within 334 

the same cohort of six subjects. Therefore, the group-level results should not be interpreted as independent 335 

out-of-sample prediction accuracy for unseen TKR patients. Rather, they demonstrate the feasibility of 336 

deriving a population-level cost function that can reproduce the general squat kinematic patterns observed 337 

within the available cohort. Future work should evaluate generalisability using leave-one-subject-out testing, 338 

external validation cohorts, or larger datasets when they become available. This distinction is important 339 

because true preoperative prediction would require the model to generalise to patients whose postoperative 340 

motion data were not used during weight identification. 341 

Several additional limitations should also be considered. First, the current OCP formulation did not fully 342 

capture the temporal asymmetry in the descent-to-ascent transition timing observed in the experimental 343 

squat trajectories of several subjects. This suggests that the current objective-function structure may capture 344 

overall squat depth and trajectory shape more effectively than phase-specific timing. Second, the model used 345 

uniform ligament parameters and maximum actuator torques across all subjects, which may not account for 346 

individual variations in soft-tissue properties, muscle strength, pain, or neuromuscular control. Third, the use 347 

of torque actuators simplifies the representation of human actuation and does not fully capture muscle-level 348 

coordination, activation dynamics, or metabolic effort. Finally, the derivative-free optimiser PRIMA is a 349 

local search method, so the recovered weights may represent local rather than global optima. 350 

Despite these limitations, the proposed framework provides a useful proof-of-concept for incorporating 351 

dynamic functional simulation into future TKR planning workflows. The group-level cost function explores 352 

whether a population-level objective formulation can reproduce post-TKR squat kinematics without using 353 

subject-specific postoperative motion data for each individual. Although the present study does not establish 354 

fully independent preoperative prediction, it represents a step toward dynamic, function-oriented simulation 355 

tools that may eventually complement static imaging-based planning. The individualised setting provides an 356 

upper-bound reconstruction benchmark, while the group-level setting provides an initial estimate of how well 357 

a shared cost function can reproduce squat kinematics across subjects. 358

# Conclusion 368 

This study demonstrates the feasibility of combining musculoskeletal simulation with bi-level inverse optimal 369 

control to reproduce post-total knee replacement squat kinematics. The individualised cost-function setting 370 

provided accurate subject-specific reconstruction and served as a benchmark for the best achievable agreement 371 

under the proposed model and objective-function structure. The group-level setting produced higher errors 372 

but preserved the overall squat motion pattern across subjects, suggesting that a shared population-level cost 373 

function may provide a useful starting point for future preoperative functional simulation. 374 

Importantly, the proposed framework generated the full stand-to-squat-to-stand cycle in a single optimisa-375 

tion process, allowing squat depth to emerge from the interaction between the model dynamics, constraints, 376 

and cost-function weights rather than being prescribed directly. Although further validation in larger and 377 

independent cohorts is required before clinical application, this proof-of-concept study represents a step 378 

toward dynamic, function-oriented simulation tools for total knee replacement planning. 379
