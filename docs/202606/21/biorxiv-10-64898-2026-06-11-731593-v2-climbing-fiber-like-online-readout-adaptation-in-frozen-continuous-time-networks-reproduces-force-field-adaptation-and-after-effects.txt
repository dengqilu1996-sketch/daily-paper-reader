# When Identifiability Is Not Enough: Terminal Controllability in Task-Compatible Active Calibration 

Anonymous Authors 

Abstract —Robots that operate with uncertain body, tool, or contact parameters must often refine their body models without abandoning the task at hand. This paper shows that active calibration is not solved by identifiability alone: an exploratory trajectory can yield accurate parameters while placing the robot in a state from which the downstream task is hard to complete. We study this failure mode in a minimal redundant planar manipulator with unknown link lengths and a two-phase calibration-to-task protocol. We formulate finite-step task-compatible probing as a higher-order generalization of first-order null-space information-gradient control, allowing the robot to escape first-order task-compatible degeneracy while limiting task drift. Across 50 seeds, finite-step probing eliminates calibration failures but still leaves substantial downstream task failure with-out terminal controllability management. These failures are not explained by parameter error (AUC = 0.511) or static singularity measures (AUC = 0.490), but short-horizon terminal rollout risk achieves perfect separation (AUC = 1.000), including with oracle parameters. Phase-2 experiments show that longer execution and null-space posture regularization together eliminate the remaining task failures; follow-up checks show that the failure appears in degenerate and near-degenerate starts, while non-degenerate starts succeed with the plain controller. The results support a three-layer view of active calibration: identifiability creation, task-compatible exploration, and post-calibration finite-horizon controllability. 

Index Terms —Active calibration, redundant manipulators, in-formation gain, null-space control, robot learning. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Robots operating outside fixed calibration setups face changing tools, payloads, contacts, and body parameters [1], [2]. A mounted tool can have an offset, a grasped object can shift, and contact conditions can change while the task is still active. In such settings, offline calibration is insufficient: the robot must refine its body model through action while preserv-ing the ongoing task. This work is motivated by developmental motor learning, where an agent learns its own body through action before its body model is reliable [3], but we study the problem as active calibration for redundant robots under task constraints. Active calibration and optimal experiment design are com-monly evaluated by uncertainty reduction or estimation ac-curacy [4]. That view is incomplete for robots that must act immediately after calibration. Exploratory motion can improve the body estimate while leaving the robot in a poor state for the downstream task. A task-compatible action must therefore do three things: create identifiability, preserve the task during exploration, and maintain finite-horizon controllability after calibration (Fig. 1). We isolate this failure mode in a deliberately minimal redundant manipulator. A 4-DOF planar arm must calibrate four unknown link lengths while holding a 2D end-effector position, then use the calibrated model to reach the task goal. The main initial configuration is fully extended and first-order degenerate for task-compatible information seeking: task-only control does not excite the body parameters, and action-space information-gain optimization fails. We compare task-only control, first-order null-space information gradients, finite-step task-compatible probing, and Phase-2 posture regularization over 50 random seeds. The purpose of this setting is not to model a complete robot application, but to separate param-eter identifiability from downstream task executability under controlled conditions. Our results show that finite-step probing restores identi-fiability but does not by itself solve the task. It achieves accurate parameter estimates with zero calibration failures, yet the plain downstream controller still fails in 44% of seeds. Rollout diagnostics show that these failures are not explained by parameter RMSE or static singularity measures; short-horizon terminal rollout risk separates successful and failed seeds, even when the rollout uses oracle parameters. A longer Phase-2 horizon is necessary, and fixed null-space posture regularization eliminates the remaining task failures for the degenerate start. This paper makes three contributions. First, we formulate finite-step task-compatible probing as a higher-order general-ization of first-order null-space information-gradient control. Second, we demonstrate that accurate calibration is insufficient for downstream task success in a redundant arm. Third, we diagnose the missing factor as terminal finite-horizon controllability in degenerate and near-degenerate starts and show through controlled experiments that it must be handled separately from identifiability. II. R ELATED WORK 

A. Robot Calibration and Experiment Design 

Robot calibration has a long history in kinematic model identification, including taxonomies of measurement, param-eterization, and observability issues [1]. Many calibration pipelines separate data collection from task execution: the robot visits informative configurations, estimates parameters, and then uses the calibrated model for control. Classical experiment design for manipulators selects measurement con-figurations or excitation trajectories that maximize an observ-ability or Fisher-information criterion [5]–[8]. Self-contained

Our work uses the same information-seeking foundation but asks a different question: whether an information-seeking trajectory that gives accurate parameters also leaves the robot in a state from which the downstream task can be completed within the available horizon. 

B. Redundancy and Task-Compatible Motion 

Redundant manipulators can use null-space motion to satisfy secondary objectives while preserving a primary operational-space task to first order [11], [12]. Task-priority and stack-of-tasks formulations extend this to multiple priori-tized objectives with singularity-robust and constraint-aware redundancy resolution [13]–[16]. This structure is directly attractive for calibration because it suggests that epistemic motion can be placed in the null space of the task Jacobian. However, first-order null-space methods inherit a local limita-tion: when the projected information gradient vanishes, they do not identify whether informative motion is truly absent or only unavailable at first order. Our finite-step probing formulation addresses this limitation by treating null-space information gradients as the local approximation of a constrained finite-displacement information problem. 

C. Epistemic Action and Developmental Motivation 

The work is also related to epistemic action, intrinsic motivation, and active-inference accounts of action as a trade-off between task-directed and information-seeking behavior [3], [17]. We use this perspective only as motivation and structure: the implemented objective is a Fisher-information proxy plus task and control regularization, not full expected free-energy minimization. This distinction matters because the empirical contribution is not a claim about a complete active-inference controller. Rather, it is a robotics result about where an epistemic calibration controller can fail: low parameter error does not guarantee finite-horizon task executability. III. P ROBLEM AND METHOD 

A. Two-Phase Active Calibration

B. First-Order Null-Space Information Gradient 

For a task Jacobian J(q, ˆθ) = ∂f /∂ q ∈ Rm×n, define the null-space projector 

N (q, ˆθ) = I − J(q, ˆθ)†J(q, ˆθ). (7) A first-order null-space information-gradient controller is 

u = utask + αN (q, ˆθ)∇q I(q; b), (8) where utask is the task-holding action and α is an information-seeking gain. This exploits redundancy while preserving the estimated task output to first order, following the same operational-space and null-space structure used in redundant manipulation [11], [12]. However, at first-order task-compatible degenerate configu-rations,

We use a planar 4-DOF arm with unknown link lengths:

Unless otherwise stated, trials start at q0 = [0 , 0, 0, 0] ⊤, a fully extended degenerate configuration. Phase 1 runs for 150 steps and holds the end effector near yhold = [1 .2, 0] ⊤ while calibrating. Phase 2 moves to ytask = [0 .1345 , 1.0815] ⊤, generated by target configuration [π/ 4, π/ 3, −π/ 6, π/ 6] ⊤ under θtrue . The total arm length is 1.2, so this target has radius approximately 1.09 in the same normalized planar workspace. We evaluate 50 random seeds. Each E-step performs a Gauss–Newton MAP update of ˆθ from the accumulated end-effector residuals and the Gaussian parameter prior; it is run every three Phase-1 steps with five Gauss–Newton iterations. The main metrics are parameter RMSE at the phase change, mean Phase-1 end-effector hold error (EEhold), final task error, failRMSE, and failTask. The failure thresholds are set relative to the workspace scale rather than chosen arbitrarily. Task failure is declared when the final task error exceeds 0.10, about 8% of the total reach (1.2) and about 7% of the commanded hold-to-task displacement (magnitude ≈ 1.52 ). RMSE failure is declared when the parameter RMSE exceeds 0.08, about 7% of the total arm length and less than half the initial parameter RMSE ( ≈ 0.18 for ˆθinit ); it therefore flags only trials in which calibration has not substantially improved on the prior. For binary failure rates, we report Wilson 95% confidence intervals (Wilson score intervals for binomial proportions) in the text where they are most relevant. The main conditions are:

B. Horizon and Posture Separate Two Phase-2 Effects 

We next fix Phase 1 to finite-step probing and vary only the Phase-2 horizon and controller. The plain controller at 50 steps fails in 44% of seeds. Extending the horizon to 200 steps reduces failure to 6%, while posture at 50 steps gives no benefit. Combining posture with a 200-step horizon reduces failure to 0%. Plain and posture controllers are identical at 50 and 80 steps. They diverge only after 100 steps, and posture reaches 0 failure by 150 steps. Thus posture does not primarily accelerate short-horizon convergence; it removes a slow-convergence tail over a longer horizon.

D. Negative Results Support the Decomposition 

Three ablations constrain the interpretation. Adding aposture-potential penalty or an early rollout-risk penalty during Phase 1 gives only marginal gains or regressions, showing that a risk quantity diagnostic at the phase transition is not automatically safe to optimize while the body belief is poor. Adaptive posture and manipulability-maximizing posture are also worse than fixed posture: at horizons 100/150/200 their failure rates are 0.22/0.04/0.04 and 0.20/0.06/0.06, whereas fixed posture gives 0.16/0.00/0.00. F. Commercial-Arm Case Study (KUKA iiwa) 

To check that the effect is not specific to planar kinematics, we repeat the protocol on a KUKA LBR iiwa 7-DOF kine-matic model with three unknown distal link-scale parameters and a 3D position task. Forward kinematics are taken from a vendored open-source URDF and validated against MuJoCo reference kinematics; the planar arm remains the controlled stress test, and the iiwa study is a limited commercial-arm check. The same signatures appear (Fig. 4): after probing, RMSE is low (median 0.019), yet the plain Phase-2 controller fails in all seeds at H = 20 and in 0.31 (0.14, 0.56) at 

H = 50 , recovering to 0.00 by H = 75 . At H = 50 , a non-probing Phase-1 condition has worse calibration (RMSE 0.106) but failTask 0.00, and across 30 seeds parameter RMSE (AUC 0.36) and static singularity (AUC 0.45) do not predict failures whereas rollout risk does (AUC 0.97, also with oracle parameters). VI. D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 

The experiments separate three requirements that are often conflated in active calibration: create identifiable observations, keep exploration task-compatible, and leave a finite-horizon executable terminal state. Finite-step probing satisfies the first two but fails the third in 44% of degenerate-start seeds at 

H = 50 , which is why low RMSE alone does not certify task success. Mechanistically, small Phase-1 joint displacement leaves the transition posture near a slow-convergence region; posture regularization or longer horizon mitigates this tail, but the iiwa case shows that horizon recovery can be the domi-nant effect. The practical implication is sequential: use task-compatible excitation while the model is uncertain, diagnose the transition state before task execution, and allocate horizon or terminal regularization when the diagnostic indicates risk. The current implementation assumes effective redundancy, either kinematic redundancy or a task that constrains only part of the end-effector motion. Fully constrained tasks would require task-feasible trajectory excitation rather than null-space probing. The study is also limited to simulated kinematic models and does not model dynamics, contact, compliance, actuator limits, latency, or safety constraints. Moreover, using terminal rollout risk as an online penalty remains a design implication rather than a validated controller. Even with these limitations, the results show that active calibration must be evaluated not only by parameter identifiability, but also by whether the calibrated transition state is executable within the downstream task horizon.
