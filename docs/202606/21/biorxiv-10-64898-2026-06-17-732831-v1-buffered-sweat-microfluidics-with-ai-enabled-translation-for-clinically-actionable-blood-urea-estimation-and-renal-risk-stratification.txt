Abstract 19 

Kidney-function assessment relies on blood urea as a clinically informative metabolic marker; 20 

however, its dependence on venipuncture and centralised laboratory testing limits high-21 

frequency monitoring and delays timely clinical intervention. Here, we report an integrated 22 

platform combining a wearable buffered microfluidic patch with a physiology-informed, data-23 

driven calibration framework for real-time, non-invasive estimation of blood urea from 24 

microlitre-scale sweat volumes (4.79 μL). By precisely regulating the release kinetics of 25 

internal buffer salts, the device stabilises the local reaction microenvironment, mitigating 26 

variability in sweat pH and flow to ensure reproducible measurement. The resulting signals 27 

are processed through an artificial intelligence (AI) -enabled analysis pipeline that integrates 28 

sweat urea with patient-specific physiological information to generate clinically interpretable 29 

outputs. In multicentre studies, sweat urea shows a strong association with blood urea across 30 

diverse cohorts, but with nonlinear and time-lagged relationships that limit direct use. The AI-31 

enabled calibration model compensates for these effects, enabling high-fidelity estimation of 32 

blood urea (r = 0.945 versus gold-standard measurements) at clinically relevant concordance 33 

levels. The platform further identifies kidney injury with 89.1% accuracy and stratifies disease 34 

severity with 83.2% accuracy. Notably, these results demonstrate that the integration of 35 

physicochemical stabilisation and AI-enabled data-driven translation establishes sweat as a 36 

clinically actionable surrogate for renal monitoring, supporting population-level estimation and 37 

highlighting the potential for personalised longitudinal assessment, and enabling a scalable, 38 

non-invasive strategy for high-frequency kidney disease management. 39

Introduction 40 

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affects approximately 850 million people worldwide, 41 

representing nearly 10% of the global population and rising as one of the leading causes of 42 

mortality 1-3 . A formidable challenge in clinical management is the stark contrast between the 43 

insidious progression of early-stage CKD and the abrupt deterioration seen in acute kidney 44 

injury (AKI). In early CKD, the kidney’s robust compensatory capacity maintains metabolic 45 

balance through hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons, rendering the disease largely 46 

asymptomatic and "silent" until late-stage failure 4. Conversely, in AKI or acute exacerbations, 47 

a rapid decline in nephron function triggers a catastrophic "waterfall effect," where the 48 

precipitous accumulation of nitrogenous wastes and electrolyte imbalances causes 49 

irreversible renal failure within hours 5-7 . This bi-phasic nature of renal disease, characterised 50 

by long-term silence followed by rapid collapse, demands high-frequency monitoring of key 51 

metabolic markers to enable early intervention and dynamic treatment titration 4-7 .52 

Renal function assessment currently relies on blood-based biomarkers, most notably 53 

blood urea and serum creatinine 8,9 . While central to clinical guidelines, these metrics are 54 

tethered to invasive venipuncture and centralised laboratory infrastructure, requiring 55 

sophisticated automated analysers and trained personnel. These requirements impose 56 

significant barriers: for high-risk populations, they preclude the frequent screening necessary 57 

to detect "silent" early-stage CKD; for hospitalised patients, the low sampling frequency and 58 

long sample-to-result turnaround times fail to capture the hyper-acute dynamics of AKI 6,9 .59 

These limitations highlight the need for alternative sensing strategies that are non-invasive 60 

and suitable for frequent monitoring, while achieving agreement with gold-standard blood 61 

measurements at levels typically required for clinical decision-making (typically around 90%, 62 

depending on application) 10 , ideally in simple and deployable formats that enable rapid and 63 

actionable feedback outside traditional clinical settings, analogous to the paradigm-shifting 64 

glucose test strip 11 .65 

Sweat urea, partitioned from blood via the sweat glands, has emerged as a promising 66 

non-invasive surrogate for blood urea monitoring (Fig.1a, upper left )12-16 . However, 67 

translating sweat measurements into clinically reliable indicators of renal function remains an 68 

unresolved challenge 13,16,17 . This limitation arises from both analytical and physiological 69 

instabilities inherent to sweat-based sensing. In current clinical practice, urea is most reliably 70 

quantified using enzyme-based assays, including urease-coupled colourimetric methods, 71

performance becomes highly sensitive to variations in pH and ionic composition, both of which 73 

exhibit substantial interindividual variability 12 . Similar environmental susceptibility is observed 74 

across alternative sensing modalities. Furthermore, the low secretion volumes (microlitre-75

measurement errors 17-20 . More crucially, in dynamic clinical states such as dialysis, the sweat– 77 

blood urea relationship is not a simple linear mapping but is confounded by complex nonlinear 78 

dynamics and temporal lags, necessitating a more sophisticated framework than traditional 79 

one-to-one correlation 21,22 .80 

Here, we report a buffered microfluidic platform coupled with physiology-informed, data-81 

driven mapping that overcomes these barriers to enable precise, non-invasive blood urea 82 

estimation and renal risk stratification ( Fig. 1 ). We developed a wearable microfluidic patch 83 

that requires only 4.79 μL of sample for real -time on-device urea quantification ( Fig. 1a ). To 84 

mitigate interindividual variability in sweat composition, we integrated a phosphate-buffered 85 

saline (PBS) kinetic release system that stabilises the reaction microenvironment, as 86 

supported by numerical simulations of pH distribution and reaction kinetics. To bridge the 87 

disparity between sweat and systemic measurements, we further developed a data-driven 88 

calibration model that integrates sweat urea with patient-specific physiological and clinical 89 

variables, learned from large-scale multicentre datasets ( Fig. 1b ). Implemented within a 90 

smartphone-based analysis pipeline, the system enables automated image acquisition, signal 91 

extraction and translation into clinically interpretable outputs. Appropriate microfluidic buffering 92 

fully utilizes the available colorimetric range and produces a smooth concentration-dependent 93 

response ( Fig. 1c ). By capturing physiological correlations, including temporal lag and 94 

nonlinear transport effects, the model enables quantitative mapping of sweat signals to high-95 

fidelity blood urea estimates (r = 0.945 versus gold-standard measurements, Fig. 1d ). Notably, 96 

the platform achieves clinically relevant performance in identifying kidney injury (89.1%) and 97 

stratifying disease severity (83.2% ) and has demonstrated its feasibility for near-patient testing 98 

scenarios in real-world clinical workflows. Together, these results establish buffered sweat 99 

microfluidics as a clinically actionable surrogate for blood urea and define a general framework 100 

for coupling physicochemical stabilisation with data-driven translation of biofluid signals, 101 

enabling non-invasive, high-frequency and personalised kidney disease management. 102

the reference branch, while the valve on the reaction side remains locked, thereby confining 152 

the reaction volume within the pad and minimising flushing effects ( Fig. 2d ).153

Physiology-adaptive buffering and integrated colorimetric readout for non-invasive 186 

urea quantification 187 

While the microfluidic system ensures reliable fluid transport and mechanical robustness, 188 

accurate biochemical sensing requires a controlled reaction microenvironment that can be 189 

disrupted by physiological variability in sweat composition. Urea quantification relies on 190 

urease-catalysed hydrolysis, which increases local pH and is transduced into a colorimetric 191 

signal by phenol red. However, substantial variations in sweat pH across individuals can alter 192 

the initial reaction conditions and introduce bias in urea readout. In addition, deviations in the 193 

initial pH can shift the system outside the effective operating window of phenol red (pH 194 

transition range: 6.8–8.2 ), resulting in signal saturation and loss of dynamic range, thereby 195 

limiting reliable urea quantification. 196 

To mitigate this variability and implement on-chip pH conditioning, we designed a 197 

structured PBS buffer pad upstream to regulate buffer release and pre-condition incoming 198 

sweat. A reduced transport model was used to relate geometric parameters of the groove 199 

structure, including pitch (p) and porosity (ϕ) , to an effective buffer delivery rate (k eq ), thereby 200 

mapping device structure to buffer flux ( Fig. 3a ). The optimised groove geometries were 201 

implemented in the device, as confirmed by microscopy images showing well-defined and 202 

spatially patterned microchannels ( Fig. 3b ), which regulate buffer –fluid interaction and hence 203 

buffer delivery into the flowing stream. 204 

By systematically varying p, ϕ and the incoming pH (4.5– 7.0), we identified an optimal 205 

operating regime in which buffer release stabilises the reaction conditions ( Fig. 3c ). In the 206 

reaction pad ( Fig. 3c,i ), the final pH decreases with increasing buffer delivery rate (k eq ), such 207 

that insufficient buffering results in elevated pH, whereas excessive buffering over-suppresses 208 

the enzymatic response. This behaviour defines a narrow window of k eq that maintains the 209

To quantify urea through the buffered sensing chemistry, we first characterised the 234 

colourimetric response of the assay ( Fig. 3d ). Urease -catalysed urea hydrolysis increases 235 

local pH, inducing a colour shift of phenol red from yellow to red ( Fig. 3d,i ). This change was 236 

quantified using the green (G) channel of the RGB signal, which exhibited an approximately 237 

linear decrease with increasing urea concentration under controlled conditions ( Fig. 3d,ii ), 238 

enabling direct calibration for urea quantification. 239 

To achieve reliable readout under practical imaging conditions, we implemented a 240 

smartphone-based analysis workflow ( Fig. 3e ). Fiducial markers were used to automatically 241 

locate the device and define a consistent region of interest, while image normalisation and 242 

colour correction were applied to reduce variations due to illumination and viewing geometry 24 .243 

In addition, two identical sensing units were incorporated to provide internal redundancy and 244 

enable signal averaging. Using this integrated acquisition and correction strategy, the 245 

colourimetric signal could be robustly converted into sweat urea concentration 25,26 . On-skin 246 

measurements confirmed reliable device identification and stable colour capture across 247 

repeated acquisitions ( Fig. 3f ), demonstrating consistent readout under wearable conditions. 248 

Having established the integrated microfluidic buffering and image-based quantification 249 

framework, we next evaluated the impact of buffering on quantitative accuracy by comparison 250 

to representative limiting conditions. To validate the necessity of controlled buffering, we 251 

compared the performance of our optimised microfluidic design with two limiting conditions 252 

using artificial sweat spiked with known urea concentrations ( Fig. 3g ). In the absence of 253 

buffering (“no buffer”, blue dots ), the measured signal rapidly saturates, leading to significant 254 

overestimation and loss of quantitative capability. This condition mimics direct, unregulated 255 

sweat sensing (e.g., patch or strip-based measurements), where variations in sample 256 

composition distort the biochemical response. In contrast, under excessive buffering (purple 257 

dots) , the signal becomes strongly suppressed and largely insensitive to urea concentration, 258 

as the reaction is over-neutralised. This reflects conventional bulk dilution approaches, where 259 

analytes are mixed into large volumes of buffer, compromising sensitivity. Only within the 260

Data-driven translation of sweat urea into a clinically actionable blood urea 265 

Having established a robust microfluidic buffering and colourimetric sensing framework 266 

for accurate quantification of sweat urea, we next asked whether these measurements could 267 

be translated into clinically meaningful estimates of systemic urea levels. A key challenge 268 

arises from the mismatch between sweat and blood urea concentrations.  Although sweat urea 269 

may be proportionally related to blood urea, the precise relationship remains unclear,  with 270 

reported ratios ranging from ~1 to 50 depending on sampling protocols, physiological state 271 

and disease conditions 13,15,27 . This variability, driven by transport limitations and delayed 272 

equilibration between compartments, has hindered direct clinical interpretation of sweat 273 

measurements. Thus, following precise and standardised quantification enabled by our device, 274 

a reliable framework is required to infer blood urea from sweat-derived signals. 275 

To address this, we performed a prospective clinical study and developed a data-driven 276 

calibration strategy that integrates sweat measurements with patient-specific information ( Fig. 277 

4). First, a training cohort from multicentre consisting of 129 patients and 475 paired 278 

measurements was used to construct the model. In the training phase, the calibration 279 

framework integrates heterogeneous data streams into a unified feature representation ( Fig. 280 

4a ). Primary measurements (P), including sweat and blood urea, are combined with temporal 281 

information (T) and auxiliary clinical variables (A), including demographic parameters (age, 282 

sex and body mass index), renal -related indicators (renal replacement therapy status and 283 

chronic kidney disease history), and clinical severity indices (for example, APACHE II, SOFA 284 

and GCS scores). In addition, historic measurements are used to generate extended features 285 

(E) via linear prediction, enabling the model to capture temporal trends and delayed 286 

physiological responses. These components are concatenated into a feature matrix (X) for 287 

each patient (n p), where k denotes the number of samples per patient. Importance sampling 288 

and cross-validation are then applied to ensure robust model training. 289 

Within this framework, two models were constructed to reflect distinct clinical scenarios. 290 

Base model  is trained using only primary measurements (sweat and blood urea), 291 

corresponding to settings in which limited patient information is available and prediction must 292

estimated blood urea from sweat urea concentration measured by buffered microfluidics could 342 

support clinically meaningful decision-making, in particular the identification and stratification 343

To assess whether AI-estimated blood urea could support clinically relevant stratification, 347 

we examined how well it distinguished different stages of kidney injury across possible 348 

decision thresholds. The resulting receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve yielded an 349 

area under the curve (AUC) of 0.953 for distinguishing AKI from non -AKI states (95% CI, 350 

0.940– 0.967; Fig. 5a), where an AUC of 1 indicates perfect separation and 0.5 indicates 351 

chance-level classification. Among patients with AKI, the AI-estimated blood urea also 352 

distinguished severe from non-severe disease with an AUC of 0.901 (95% CI, 0.873– 0.929). 353 

Thus, despite the physiological differences between sweat and blood urea concentrations, the 354 

calibrated signal retained sufficient information to stratify both the presence and severity of 355 

AKI. To assess practical clinical performance, we examined classification outcomes at the 356 

optimal thresholds. For AKI prediction, the  AI-estimated blood urea  correctly identified 84.6% 357 

of measurements associated with AKI (true-positive rate) while accurately excluding 94.5% of 358 

non-AKI measurements (true-negative rate; Fig. 5b ). For severe AKI, the model retained 359

Conclusion 414 

In this work, we establish an integrated microfluidic–AI framework for non-invasive 415 

assessment of renal function through sweat urea sensing and clinical translation. By 416 

combining a low-volume microfluidic device with controlled buffering and colorimetric readout, 417 

we achieve accurate and reproducible quantification of sweat urea using only microlitre-scale 418 

samples, while minimizing the impact of interindividual variability in sweat composition. The 419 

incorporation of structured fluidic buffering and optical standardisation enables stable assay 420 

conditions across heterogeneous physiological environments, providing high-fidelity 421 

measurements under real-world constraints. Building upon this measurement foundation, we 422 

develop a data-driven calibration strategy that translates sweat urea into clinically actionable 423 

estimates of blood urea by integrating patient-specific physiological context. This approach 424 

enables not only accurate estimation of systemic urea levels, but also robust renal risk 425 

stratification, including the detection and severity classification of AKI. Importantly, the 426 

framework captures nonlinear transport limitations and temporal lag between sweat and blood 427 

compartments, allowing physiologically informed tracking of renal dynamics across both stable 428 

and rapidly evolving conditions. Together, this work demonstrates that coupling 429 

physicochemical stabilisation with data-driven modelling can transform sweat-derived signals 430 

into reliable indicators of organ function, providing a scalable and patient-friendly alternative 431 

to conventional blood-based testing. 432 

The integration of microfluidic sensing and artificial intelligence presented here highlights 433 

a broader opportunity for transforming accessible biofluids into clinically actionable information. 434

systemic metabolism could further enhance diagnostic resolution and enable multidimensional 436 

assessment of disease states. The incorporation of longitudinal data streams and adaptive 437 

modelling strategies may also improve prediction accuracy across diverse patient populations 438

real-time data processing have the potential to evolve into continuous health monitoring 441

1. Microfluidic device design and moulding 452 

A negative mould for the microfluidic channel layer was first fabricated by stereolithography-453 

based 3D printing using a photosensitive resin (Formlabs Form 3, resin type V3, in-plane 454 

resolution 25μm). After printing, the mould surface was activated by oxygen plasma treatment 455 

(150 W power, 5 minutes duration) to promote subsequent silanisation. Immediately after 456 

plasma activation, the mould was vapour-phase silanised using 300 μL of a trichloro (1H, 1H, 457 

2H, 2H-perfluorooctyl) silane reagent in a vacuum desiccator environment for 2 hours at 60°C 458 

temperature, thereby forming an anti-adhesive monolayer on the mould surface. 459 

Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS Sylgard 184) prepolymer and curing agent were mixed at a mass 460 

ratio of 15:1, degassed under vacuum (100mbar) for 3 mins, and cast onto the treated mould. 461 

The PDMS was then thermally cured at 60 °C for 6 h. After curing, the PDMS replica was 462 

carefully peeled off and rinse with IPA for 10 mins. 463

11. Collection of paired sweat-blood measurements 714 

Paired sweat-blood measurements were collected prospectively during routine ICU blood 715 

testing. For each predefined sampling event, sweat was collected from intact forearm skin 716 

using the wearable microfluidic patch immediately before a planned venous blood draw. After 717 

patch filling and colour development, the device was imaged using the smartphone readout 718 

system, and venous blood was drawn without delay. Blood urea was measured by the central 719 

laboratory at each participating hospital using routine automated clinical chemistry analysers. 720 

A paired record was defined as one valid sweat-urea measurement and the immediately 721 

subsequent blood-urea measurement from the same sampling event. Measurements with 722 

incomplete patch filling, failed image registration, visible leakage, image saturation or 723 

excessive disagreement between duplicate sensing units were excluded. Repeated paired 724 

measurements from the same patient were permitted during ICU admission. For each paired 725 

record, demographic characteristics, laboratory results, treatment information, renal 726 

replacement therapy status and kidney injury status were extracted from the hospital 727 

information system and linked to the corresponding sweat–blood measurement. 728 

12. Statistical analysis 729 

Continuous variables were reported as median with interquartile range or mean ± s.d., as 730 

appropriate. Categorical variables were reported as counts and percentages. Between-group 731 

comparisons were performed using the Mann– Whitney U test, Student’s t -test, χ² test or 732 

Fisher’s exact test, as appropriate. Correlations were assessed using Pearson’s correlation 733 

coefficient. ROC curves were used to evaluate diagnostic performance, and AUCs with 95% 734 

confidence intervals were reported. A two-sided P value <0.05 was considered statistically 735 

significant. 736
