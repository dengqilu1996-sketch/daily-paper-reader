Abstract 18 

19 

Habits are automatic actions shaped by prior experience, enabling efficient and fast responses 20 

in stable environments. While habitual gaze rapidly directs the eyes toward valuable objects, 21 

the mechanisms underlying habitual manual choice for reaching and grasping remain unclear. 22 

Here we show that macaque monkeys develop habitual manual choices toward previously 23 

rewarded objects through multi-day object–value learning, driven by learned value and spatial 24 

preferences. This reaching habit persisted without reward and showed shorter latencies than in 25 

value-deliberative tasks, consistent with automatic control. Regression analyses further 26 

revealed that manual choice was guided by learned object values, whereas visual salience had 27 

no effect, unlike gaze habits. Instead, intrinsic spatial preferences continued to bias reaching 28 

behavior even after long-term value learning. These findings demonstrate that habitual manual 29 

choice arises from the integration of spatial preferences and long-term value memory, defining 30 

a distinct mechanism of automatic behavior beyond habitual gaze. 31

Introduction 33 

34 

Through repeated experience, actions become automatic, forming habits that enable fast and 35 

efficient behavior in stable environments 1–7 . For example, when a thirsty individual repeatedly 36 

reaches for a nearby glass of water, the action gradually becomes automatic—executed rapidly 37 

and without deliberation. This automatic choice allows faster and more efficient selection of 38 

valuable objects than competitors in stable contexts. 39 

40 

Previous studies have mainly focused on habits formed through extensive repetition, where 41 

repeated movements render an action automatic 8–12 . Yet repetition alone may not fully explain 42 

why the thirsty individual so readily reaches for the glass. In this example, the motivational 43 

value of water – not mere repetition of the reaching movement – drives the action. This 44 

distinction highlights an alternative pathway for habit formation: value associations may 45 

trigger automatic behaviors independently of motor repetition 13–15 .46 

47 

Value-based habits have been documented in the primate oculomotor system 7,16–23 . Once visual 48 

objects are associated with reward, macaques and humans show persistent gaze biases toward 49 

high-valued objects, even when value is no longer relevant and feedback is withheld 7,16,19,21,24 .50 

This behavior, which persists without reinforcement and occurs with shorter latencies than 51 

goal-directed saccades, has been taken as a hallmark of automatic control in visual habit 19,23 .52 

Functionally, such a system provides a fast, low-cost way to orient toward valuable objects 53 

without deliberation, thereby enhancing efficiency and survival 3,7,25,26 . Experimentally, visual 54 

habit is typically studied by training primates across multiple days on object–reward 55 

associations and then testing them in reward-neutral sessions. Animals continue to direct gaze 56 

toward previously high-valued objects, demonstrating stable, automatic biases 16,19,21,23 . These 57 

findings have been central to identifying basal ganglia mechanisms underlying visual habits. 58 

59 

However, in natural conditions, valuable objects cannot be obtained by gaze alone; they must 60 

be physically reached for and grasped. This raises a critical question: if visual habit can emerge 61 

from value-based learning, might manual choice behavior also become habitual through similar 62 

mechanisms? Despite this, little is known about whether the value-based mechanisms that drive 63 

habitual gaze extend to manual choice. In everyday life, acquiring reward depends not on 64 

looking but on reaching and grasping, making the hand the effector most directly tied to 65

obtaining value. Yet prior studies of hand habits emphasized motor repetition, leaving 66 

unanswered whether long-term value learning alone can induce automatic hand actions 8–10 .67 

68 

At the circuit level, value-based visual habits are thought to arise from long-term value signals 69 

encoded in the striatum and conveyed through the substantia nigra pars reticulata (SNr) to 70 

control saccades. Anatomical evidence indicates that the same striatal value signals also project 71 

through the globus pallidus internal segment (GPi) to hand motor pathways 19,27–29 , providing 72 

a plausible route by which value memories could shape habitual manual choice. However, eye 73 

and hand effectors differ in their functional demands, raising the possibility that value-based 74 

habits may exhibit effector-specific characteristics 30 . Since both SNr and GPi receive striatal 75 

value signals, we hypothesized that long-term learning would induce habitual manual choice, 76 

with effector-specific divergence emerging at the output level. 77 

78 

To address this, we tested whether long-term object-value learning can induce habitual manual 79 

choice behavior in primates and whether the underlying mechanisms differ from those driving 80 

visual habits. We developed a multi-phase behavioral paradigm in which monkeys learned 81 

visual object–reward associations through manual choices across multiple days and were 82 

subsequently tested in a value-neutral manual choice task without reward or feedback. This 83 

allowed us to assess whether previously learned value alone could drive persistent, automatic 84 

hand movements, and whether such behavior reflected long-term value memory by retesting 85 

after a one-week retention period. As a hallmark of habit is automaticity – fast, efficient 86 

responses that bypass deliberative evaluation 3,4,19,21 – we further compared response latencies 87 

in the habitual task with those in a value-deliberative reversal learning task. Finally, to identify 88 

the factors shaping habitual manual choices, we performed regression analyses with object 89 

value, spatial preference, object preference, and visual salience as predictors. 90

Results 92 

> 93

Formation of long-term value memory for visual objects via manual choice 94 

> 95

To investigate whether habitual manual choice behavior developed through stable object-value 96 

associations, we implemented a structured task schedule consisting of Pre-learning, Learning, 97 

and Retention phases (Fig. 1A). During the Pre-learning phase, we established a behavioral 98 

baseline reflecting the monkeys’ innate manual choice preferences using a habitual choice task 99 

conducted prior to value learning. In the Learning phase, monkeys learned the reward values 100 

associated with visual fractal objects, forming the basis of value-based habitual choice behavior. 101 

On the first day of this phase (Day 0), monkeys performed an object-value learning task in 102 

which particular visual objects were paired with specific reward outcomes (Fig. 1A). On 103 

subsequent days (Day 1-9), the habitual choice task was conducted before the object-value 104 

learning task to assess the development of manual choice behavior based on long-term value 105 

memory, independent of any immediate reward feedback. This daily sequence was repeated for 106 

nine consecutive days. One day after the final value learning session (Day 10), we tested 107 

whether the monkeys showed habitual choice behavior. To examine the persistence of the 108 

learned habitual behavior, monkeys underwent a one-week retention period without exposure 109 

to the trained objects, after which they were again tested with the habitual choice task. 110 

> 111

In the object-value learning task (Fig. 1B), monkeys performed a two-choice discrimination 112 

paradigm using fractal images associated with different reward values. Each learning set 113 

consisted of four fractals: two high-valued and two low-valued (Fig. 1C). In each trial, a pair 114 

of fractal objects was presented simultaneously at pseudorandomly assigned locations among 115 

4 possible screen positions, and monkeys were required to select one by touching it. Choosing 116 

a high-valued object resulted in a water reward, whereas selecting a low-valued object yielded 117 

no reward. This consistent reward structure enabled the monkeys to acquire stable value 118 

representations of individual visual objects. 119 

> 120

Monkeys rapidly acquired object-value associations, with the proportion of high-valued object 121 

choices increasing sharply on the first day of learning and quickly reaching a plateau (Figs. 1D 122 

and S1A). A three-way ANOVA with value, day, and bin as factors revealed a significant main 123 

effect of value (F(1, 560) = 169,328.54, p < 0.001), as well as significant interactions of value 124

Furthermore, we obtained evidence of choice behavior based on long-term value memory by 130 

examining the monkeys’ first choices in the initial trial of each learning session, as this was the 131 

first time they encountered the object each day. On the first day of learning, monkeys did not 132 

exhibit a significant preference between high- and low-valued objects in the initial trial (Day 0 133 

in Fig. 1E). However, from the second day onward, a significant value bias was observed, with 134 

monkeys increasingly selecting high-valued objects for the first choice (Figs. 1E and S1B). A 135 

post hoc Bonferroni pairwise comparison confirmed a significant increase in high-valued 136 

object selection between Day 0 and 1 (p=2.694 × 10⁻ 3), while no significant difference was 137 

found between Day 1 and 2 (p=0.683), suggesting that the value bias rapidly stabilized after 138 

the second day. This pattern indicates that object-value associations were consolidated across 139 

sessions and retained over time. These findings confirm that the monkeys acquired stable value 140 

representations of fractal objects through the learning task, forming the basis for examining 141 

whether these value memories subsequently guide habitual manual choice behavior under more 142 

naturalistic condition. 143

Long-term value memory influences habitual manual choice behavior 146 

> 147

To examine habitual manual choice behavior guided by long-term value memory, we used a 148 

habitual choice task in which the four previously learned fractals were presented at 149 

pseudorandomly selected positions among 4 of 8 possible touchscreen locations (Fig. 2A and 150 

Movie S1). Monkeys erased each object by touching it, with trial progress indicated by a central 151 

gauge bar that filled incrementally with each object removal. Once all four objects were erased, 152 

the monkey obtained a liquid reward by touching the fully filled gauge bar (Figs. 2A and C). 153 

Importantly, no reward was provided during the object choices themselves, and neither object 154 

identity nor the sequence of selections influenced value reinforcement, thereby eliminating 155 

direct feedback for individual choices. Thus, the optimal strategy was to select all four objects 156 

as quickly and automatically as possible. Because habitual behaviors persist in the absence of 157

We first examined the first choice of each trial to assess whether monkeys exhibited a bias 161

findings indicate that monkeys formed habitual preferences for previously learned high-valued 173 

objects, independent of immediate reward feedback. 174

To evaluate the persistence of this habitual choice behavior, monkeys were re-tested after a 176 

one-week retention period without additional learning and object exposure. The proportion of 177 

first choice toward high-valued objects remained comparable to that observed during the 178 

Learning phase, even one week after the last learning (Fig. 2D, right panel) (one-way ANOVA, 179

sustained over the one-week period. 182 

> 183

To rule out performance fluctuations or task familiarity as alternative explanations, we included 184 

a control object set that had not been associated with rewards. This control set was tested on 185 

the same days as the learning object sets (Day 1-10). Monkeys did not exhibit any significant 186 

first-choice bias to specific objects across sessions, confirming that the observed habitual 187 

choice behavior was specific to the learned value associations (Figs. 2E and S2C-D). 188

Response latency indicates automaticity of habitual manual choice behavior 207 

> 208

Automatic, habitual behavior is generally characterized by fast actions executed through simple 209 

sensory-to-action circuits, in contrast to more deliberative behaviors that rely on cognitive 210 

flexibility and involve more complex brain circuits 3,4,7,27,29 . Thus, it is important not only to 211 

examine whether actions based on previously learned values persist in the absence of reward 212 

feedback, but also to assess how quickly these learned actions are executed. 213 

> 214

To differentiate the speed of habitual responses from deliberative goal-directed actions, 215 

monkeys were tested in a value-reversal version of the object-value learning task, which 216 

required flexible, short-term value-based decision-making (Reversal value-based manual 217 

choice task) (Fig. 3A). In each trial of the reversal task, the two fractal objects were presented 218 

simultaneously at 2 of 4 possible positions, and the monkeys selected one by touching the 219 

display. Choosing the high-valued object resulted in a liquid reward, whereas selecting the low-220 

valued object yielded no reward. Within a single session, object-value associations were 221 

reversed twice, requiring cognitive flexibility to dynamically update object-value association 222 

(Fig. 3A). We introduced this reversal component to establish a behavioral context that 223

Progression to the next reversal block required the monkeys to select the high-valued object in 227

acquisition of flexibly changing object-value association. 230

We defined response latency as the time from object presentation to object selection and 232 

compared latencies across three behavioral tasks: the object-value learning task, the reversal 233 

value-based manual choice task, and the habitual choice task (Figs. 1B, 2A, and 3A). Because 234 

the object-value learning and reversal tasks required deliberative discrimination of associated 235 

value, we hypothesized that monkeys would exhibit longer response latencies in these tasks 236 

compared to the habitual task, which does not require explicit value evaluation 21,31 . Consistent 237 

with this prediction, monkeys exhibited significantly shorter response latencies during the 238 

habitual choice task (741.15 ± 3.52 ms) compared to both the object-value learning task (749.57 239 

± 3.27 ms) and the reversal task (760.11 ± 4.84 ms), indicating a more automated behavioral 240 

response (one-way ANOVA, F(2, 44,478) = 6075.57, p < 0.001) (Figs. 3C and S3B-C). 241 

> 242

across tasks (Fig. 3D). Since the central dot touch is an instructed, non-value–dependent action, 244 

it serves as a motor baseline for each task, against which object-touch latencies can be 245 

interpreted. As expected, in the object–value learning and reversal tasks, central dot latencies 246 

were shorter than object-touch latencies (one-way ANOVA, p < 0.001 for Central dot vs. 247 

Learning; p < 0.001 for Central dot vs. Reversal) (Fig. 3D). In contrast, in the habitual choice 248 

task, object-touch latencies were even shorter than central dot touch latency (one-way ANOVA, 249 

p < 0.001 for Central dot vs. Habitual choice) (Fig. 3D), indicating that object-directed actions 250 

in the habitual choice task were executed with highly automatic timing. 251

Value and spatial preference shape value-based habitual manual choice behavior 268 

> 269

What are the key factors that drive habitual manual choice movements? Do such actions depend 270 

on the visual salience of the target, the motivational value of the object, or internal biases such 271 

as spatial or object preference? While habitual visual behavior is often guided by visual salience 272 

and object-based value associations 7, it remains unclear whether these same drivers apply to 273 

hand-based habits. Given that gaze and hand systems serve distinct computational roles—gaze 274 

being object-centered and visually guided, and hand movements being spatially constrained 275 

and biomechanically shaped 30 — we tested whether the behavioral drivers of value-based 276 

habitual manual choices differ from those of habitual gaze behavior. 277 

> 278

To identify the key contributors to manual choice behavior, we performed trial-level regression 279 

analyses using visual salience, innate object preference, innate spatial preference, and value as 280 

predictors (Fig. 4A). Visual salience was computed using a graph-based visual saliency (GBVS) 281 

algorithm 32 , with each object assigned a ranked saliency score per trial. Innate spatial 282 

preference was assessed through a pre-learning movement test, where objects were presented 283 

in all possible position configurations (4 objects ⅹ 8 positions, 1680 configurations). The 284 

selection order for each position was used to compute preference scores for the eight spatial 285 

locations (Fig. 4B). Notably, spatial preferences differed between subjects: monkey B exhibited 286 

a leftward bias, while monkey M showed a rightward bias (Fig. 4B), suggesting that spatial 287 

preference is individually variable and innate. Similarly, object preference was calculated from 288 

pre-learning tests based on the selection order of each object (higher object preference score 289

Discussion 332 

333 

In this study, we demonstrated that habitual hand choice behavior in primates emerges from 334 

the combined influence of learned object values and stable spatial preferences, while visual 335 

salience exerts no measurable effect. These habits persisted even when feedback was withheld 336 

and were executed with reduced response latency, thereby meeting two canonical criteria of 337 

habitual control: independence from immediate reinforcement and automaticity of execution. 338 

By identifying value and spatial preference as the principal drivers of manual choice, our 339 

findings reveal that the determinants of manual choice habits differ from those of oculomotor 340 

habits, where both value and visual salience influence gaze. This dissociation suggests that 341 

although long-term value memories form a common substrate across effectors, their behavioral 342 

expression is shaped by effector-specific constraints—visual salience in gaze, spatial 343 

preference in hand. In this way, our study extends the framework of value-based habits from 344 

the oculomotor to the manual domain, providing a behavioral foundation for linking long-term 345 

object value memories to effector-specific expressions of habit. 346

Functional significance of value-based habitual manual choice 349 

350 

Most prior studies of hand habits have emphasized motor repetition as the key driver of 351 

automaticity (e.g. sequential finger movement) 34–36 , showing that actions repeated over time 352 

can be expressed without conscious control 1,2,6,11 . Our findings demonstrate that long-term 353 

object value, distinct from motor repetition-guided habits, can also establish stable manual 354 

choice habits. 355 

356 

Two behavioral signatures confirmed this. First, monkeys’ choices did not depend on reward 357 

contingencies but instead reflected the previously learned values of fractal objects. Both 358 

monkeys preferentially selected previously high-valued objects on the first touch of a trial, 359 

despite the absence of feedback, and this bias persisted after one-week retention period (Figs. 360 

2D-G). These results indicate that, unlike simple motor habits formed through repetition, such 361 

movements can become habitual through sensory–motor associations driven by long-term 362 

value memory. 363

Second, choices were automatic. Responses in the habitual task were faster than in value-365 

deliberative tasks, and object-selection latencies in the habitual task were shorter even than 366 

simple central dot-touch latencies, indicating streamlined sensorimotor execution (Figs. 3C-D). 367 

Moreover, in value-deliberative tasks, choice latencies differed between high- and low-valued 368 

options in both this and previous studies, whereas in the habitual task no such latency difference 369 

was observed (Figs. 3E-G), consistent with execution that bypasses deliberative evaluation 37,38 .370 

These properties, well established for gaze habits 20,23,29 , are now demonstrated for manual 371 

choice, showing that long-term value memory can drive rapid, stimulus-guided hand actions 372 

without immediate reinforcement. 373 

> 374

The adaptive advantages of such value-driven hand habit are clear in natural settings. Foraging 375 

and tool use require fast, repeatable reaches toward objects with established utility or 376 

nutritional value. Automating these selections reduces cognitive load, stabilizes performance, 377 

and conserves energetic resources 12 . In our task, this efficiency was reflected not only in first-378 

choice biases but also in fewer steps to clear high-valued objects (Fig. 2F), indicating 379 

prioritized sequencing once the habit formed. Thus, value-based habitual control is not 380 

restricted to gaze; it generalizes to manual choice actions, where it likely supports economical 381 

interaction with valuable objects in familiar environments 1,2,6 .382

> 384

Behavioral drivers of habitual manual choice versus habitual gaze 385 

> 386

Regression analyses indicate a dissociation in the behavioral mechanisms underlying manual 387 

choice and gaze habits. In the oculomotor domain, previous studies have shown that both 388 

learned object value and visual salience strongly bias gaze behavior, even when feedback is 389 

absent 7,39 . In contrast, in our manual choice task, visual salience exerted no significant 390 

influence at any phase. Instead, two factors proved decisive: spatial preference, which was a 391 

consistent predictor from Pre-learning through Retention, and learned object value, which 392 

emerged as a predictor only after value learning. Innate object preference measured before 393 

learning lost significance once value associations were established. Thus, the shared 394 

component across effectors is a value signal that emerges after training and supports habit 395 

expression without immediate reward; the dissociations are the absence of salience effects and 396 

the dominant role of space in the hand domain. 397

These results map onto the distinct computational demands of the two effector systems. For 399 

gaze, selection operates in an object-centered frame where value and visual salience jointly 400 

accelerate saccades toward previously rewarded objects 7,40 . For the hand, in contrast, 401 

movement planning requires integration of spatial geometry and biomechanical costs such as 402 

reach distance, posture, and energetic efficiency 30 . Consistent with this, our latency analyses 403 

showed that positions with stronger spatial preference were selected faster, indicating that 404 

motor costs and spatial ease of execution directly modulate the automaticity of habitual manual 405 

choice actions. Under natural conditions where a valuable object consistently appears in the 406 

same position (e.g. a ripe apple growing on the same branch of a tree each year), spatial 407 

preference combined with value-guided choice may allow animal to reach the object more 408 

quickly and with less energy. 409 

> 410

By showing that value-based habits extend from gaze to manual choice movements, our study 411 

demonstrates that habits are not unitary but shaped by effector-specific constraints. Gaze habits 412 

integrate value with visual salience to optimize rapid orienting, while hand habits combine 413 

value with spatial preference to support efficient interaction with reachable objects. Linking 414 

these behavioral dissociations to neural circuit mechanisms will be essential for understanding 415 

how habits are implemented across effectors and how they support adaptive behavior in natural 416 

contexts. 417 

> 418

Together, these findings suggest a simple principle: value is the shared driver of habit 419 

expression across effectors, but the complementary factor is effector-specific – visual salience 420 

for gaze and spatial constraints for manual choice. This principle yields testable predictions 421 

such as that manipulations that change visual salience should bias selectively habitual gaze 422 

without affecting habitual manual choice. Testing such dissociations will be essential for 423 

linking behavioral differences to their underlying circuit implementations. 424

Plausible basal ganglia circuit mechanisms underlying habitual manual choice 427 

> 428

Our results raise important questions about how value information is stored and expressed in 429 

neural circuits to guide reaching behavior. In the oculomotor system, value-based visual habits 430

have been linked to long-term representations in the striatum, which influence activity in the 431 

SNr and downstream superior colliculus (SC) to guide saccades even in the absence of reward 432 

feedback 14,16,21,29,41 . Likewise, habitual manual choices are likely mediated by striatal value 433 

signals, as the striatum not only processes value information for reaching behavior in the 434 

reversal value task but may also serve as the locus for long-term value memory 37,38 . However, 435 

unlike the visual habit circuit, object value signal for manual choice may be transmitted through 436 

the GPi to thalamo-cortical and motor pathways 28 . This circuit architecture suggests a 437 

mechanism by which long-term value memory can guide reaching behavior without the need 438 

for current reinforcement. 439 

440 

The reduced response latencies observed during habitual manual choice suggest that, once 441 

value associations are consolidated, their expression is routed through relatively direct 442 

sensorimotor circuit. Unlike deliberative goal-directed actions, which rely on prefrontal and 443 

associative networks in the rostral basal ganglia circuits, visual habit is mediated by caudal 444 

parts of thalamus and basal ganglia 29 . Anatomical studies have shown that these caudal 445 

thalamus and basal ganglia structures receive inputs from visual areas, including the 446 

inferotemporal cortex, and send outputs to motor areas such as the SNr 4,29,42–44 . Moreover, 447 

long-term value memory processed within these caudal structures enable fast, automatic gaze 448 

shifts toward valuable objects 27 , supporting the view that caudal thalamic and basal ganglia 449 

mechanisms may also underlie habitual manual choice behavior. 450 

451 

While such sensorimotor circuits that encode long-term value memory for visual habit provide 452 

a plausible mechanism for habitual manual choice behavior, neural response data and causal 453 

manipulations are required to establish this. The primate behavioral paradigm developed here 454 

provides a strong foundation for such studies, enabling test of whether striatal value signals are 455 

transmitted through GPi–cortical pathways to drive habitual manual choices, and how these 456 

pathways interact with SNr-mediated circuits for visual habit. 457

Materials and methods 459

General procedures 461 

Two adult monkeys ( Macaca fascicularis; 2.2 kg female monkey B, 8 years old; 3.3 kg female 462 

monkey M, 17 years old) were used for the experiments. Animal care and experimental 463 

procedures were approved by the Korea Research Institute of Bioscience and Biotechnology 464 

(KRIBB) Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (Approval No. KRIBB-AEC-22132), 465 

and complied with national and institutional guidelines for the care and use of laboratory 466 

animals. This study was designed and reported in accordance with the ARRIVE guidelines for 467 

reporting animal research. 468 

Behavioral tasks 469 

The behavioral tasks were controlled by a MATLAB-based real-time experimentation data 470 

acquisition system (NIMH MonkeyLogic, Laboratory of Neuropsychology, National Institute 471 

of Mental Health, National Institutes of Health [LN/NIMH/NIH]). The monkey was seated in 472 

a primate cage, facing a fronto-parallel screen in a sound-attenuated and electrically shielded 473 

room. Visual stimuli were presented on an infrared touch monitor (IV-190IR, Iviewkorea, 474 

Korea). A frontal opening in the cage allowed the monkey to reach out its arm and touch the 475 

monitor. For all behavioral tasks, monkeys were trained to reach out their arm and touch the 476 

monitor to select the visual stimulus (Movie S1). 477 

478 

Visual stimuli 479 

We used fractal object images created using Fractal Geometry (https://github.com/ProfKimHF) 480 

(Figs. 1C and 3A). The mean luminance was equalized across the images using the SHINE 481 

toolbox written with MATLAB (www.mapageweb.umontreal.ca/gosselif/shine). The size of 482 

each image was approximately 20˚ⅹ20˚ in size. 483 

484 

Object-value learning task procedure 485 

Monkeys were trained to associate visual fractal objects with either high or low value in order 486 

to establish long-term value memory of fractal objects. Each trial began with the appearance 487 

of a white central dot (Fig. 1B). The monkey was required to touch the central dot within 5 488 

seconds to initiate the trial. Following a 1 second blank delay, two fractal objects—one high-489 

valued and one low-valued—were presented simultaneously at diagonally opposite positions 490 

on the touchscreen. The positions were pseudorandomly selected from four possible locations 491

It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.20.733501 doi: bioRxiv preprint

(right-top, right-bottom, left-top, left-bottom). Monkeys were required to choose one of the 492 

objects within 2 seconds by touching it by hand. Selection of a high-valued object resulted in 493 

immediate delivery of a liquid reward, whereas selection of a low-valued object produced no 494 

reward. 495 

> 496

A fixed set of four fractal stimuli (two high-valued, two low-valued) was used per session, and 497 

the same set was trained for 10 consecutive days to promote long-term learning. Each session 498 

consisted of 48 trials, with 4 sessions per day for each set. Therefore, each fractal stimulus was 499 

exposed 96 times each day, with a total of 960 associative learning trials for each stimulus 500 

across the Learning phase. Across the experiment, each monkey was exposed to four sets of 501 

four fractals, yielding 16 unique learned stimuli in total (Fig. 1C). 502 

> 503

Habitual choice task procedure 504 

To assess habitual responses toward previously learned objects, monkeys performed a free 505 

sequential manual choice task using fractal stimuli previously paired with either reward or no 506 

reward. Each trial began with the presentation of a central white dot, which the monkey was 507 

required to touch within 5 seconds to initiate the trial (Figs. 2A and C). A 1 second blank delay 508 

followed, after which four fractal objects from a single set were presented simultaneously at 509 

pseudorandomly chosen positions drawn from an array of eight possible screen locations 510 

spaced at 45° intervals around the center. A vertical gauge bar with four empty segments was 511 

displayed concurrently. Monkeys erased the objects by touching them sequentially in any order. 512 

Each touch caused the selected object to disappear and filled one segment of the gauge bar, 513 

providing visual feedback of task progress. Every object had to be selected within 2 seconds, 514 

and after all objects were erased, the monkey was required to touch the fully filled gauge bar 515 

within 2 seconds to receive a liquid reward. Importantly, reward delivery depended only on 516 

completion of the trial and was not contingent on the identity or order of object selection, 517 

thereby eliminating direct reinforcement of individual choices. 518 

> 519

The task was first administered in a baseline phase, before value learning, to measure 520 

spontaneous object selection preferences. Each object set was tested across five consecutive 521 

days, with 336 trials per session, allowing full coverage of all 1,680 possible object–location 522 

configurations. After completion of value learning, monkeys were tested in the same task over 523 

ten consecutive days, with 36 trials per session. To isolate habitual behavior from immediate 524 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 25, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.20.733501 doi: bioRxiv preprint

reward feedback, the habitual choice task was always conducted prior to the daily object–value 525 

learning task. Each monkey completed the task with four learned object sets (16 fractals total) 526 

and one control set of novel fractals that had not been paired with reward. 527 

528 

To assess memory retention, monkeys were tested one week after the final training session 529 

using the same object–value learning task. During this retention test, no exposure to the trained 530 

fractals occurred in the intervening week, and performance was measured to evaluate the 531 

stability of the learned object–value associations. 532 

533 

Reversal manual choice task procedure 534 

This task was designed to assess flexible manual choice behavior driven by short-term, flexible 535 

value memory. The overall trial structure was identical to the object–value learning task, except 536 

that object–value contingencies were reversed across blocks within each session (Fig. 3A). 537 

538 

Each session consisted of three consecutive blocks: an initial Acquisition block followed by 539 

two reversals. At the start of the Acquisition block, two fractal objects were introduced, with 540 

one designated as high-valued and the other as low-valued. Monkeys advanced to the next 541 

block once they selected the high-valued object on 24 consecutive trials. In each subsequent 542 

block, the reward contingencies for the two objects were reversed. Sessions ended when the 543 

criterion was met in the final reversal block. 544 

545 

Each monkey performed the task with four distinct object pairs (eight fractals in total) (Fig. 546 

S5). Each pair was tested once per day for ten consecutive days, with the initial value 547 

assignment reversed on each subsequent day to enforce continuous updating of object–value 548 

associations. 549

Behavioral analysis 553 

To quantify habitual behavior in the habitual choice task, we analyzed the monkeys’ first object 554 

choices. For each session, trials were divided into two halves, and within each half we 555 

calculated the proportion of trials in which a high-valued object was selected first. This analysis 556 

was performed separately for each learning set. For the control set, we computed the proportion 557

To quantify selection order in the habitual choice task, we calculated the mean step at which 560 

high-valued objects were chosen within each trial. For learning sets, the mean step was 561

564 

Response latencies for all behavioral tasks (object-value learning, reversal, and habitual manual 565 

choice task) were defined as the interval between stimulus onset and the first touch. Specifically, 566 

in the object–value learning and reversal tasks, response latency was measured as the time from 567 

fractal presentation to selection. In the habitual choice task, response latency was defined as 568 

the time from fractal presentation to the first fractal touch. 569

Statistical comparisons for first-choice proportions, mean selection steps, and response 571 

latencies were conducted using one-way ANOVA. Post hoc pairwise comparisons were 572 

performed with Bonferroni correction, and statistical significance was set at p < 0.05. 573

To identify factors influencing the order of object selection after learning, we performed ordinal 576 

logistic regression using the Proportional Odds Logistic Regression ( polr ) function from the 577 

MASS package in R. Separate models were fit for each day and each subject, across four 578 

learned object sets and one control set, by the following model: 579 

logit Pr (Y ≤ j|.) = 1! − (3"45 + 3#78 + 3$58 + 3%49: ), j = 1,2,3.580

The dependent variable was the ordinal rank of touches within a trial (1st–4th), treated as an 582 

ordered categorical outcome. Four predictors were included: visual salience (VS), object 583 

preference (OP), spatial preference (SP), and object value (Val) (Fig. 4A). 584 

585 

Visual salience scores were computed using the Graph-Based Visual Saliency (GBVS) 586 

algorithm 32 . For each trial, salience values were calculated for the four displayed fractals, 587 

ranked from 1 (least salient) to 4 (most salient), and entered as an ordinal predictor. 588

completed trials covering all possible object–location configurations (4 objects × 8 positions, 591 

1680 trials). Selection order across these trials was used to assign preference scores to each 592 

location. For regression, the four positions presented on a given trial were ranked (1–4) 593 

according to this preference profile and entered as the predictor for spatial preference. 594 

> 595

Object preference was calculated in the same way as spatial preference but based on fractal 596 

identity rather than location. These scores were ranked and entered as a separate predictor. 597

Object value was entered as a binary categorical factor, coded 1 for high-valued and 0 for low-599 

valued objects. 600 

> 601

To ensure stability of coefficient estimates, 25 trials were randomly sampled without 602

averaged across iterations, and runs with incomplete data or insufficient rows were excluded. 604 

Statistical comparisons were conducted using repeated-measures ANOVA, and post hoc 605 

pairwise comparisons were performed with Bonferroni correction, with statistical significance 606 

set at p < 0.05. 607

Animals and sample size justification 609 

Two adult monkeys were used in this study. This sample size is consistent with established 610 

practices in non-human primate cognitive neuroscience, where highly trained animals perform 611 

a large number of repeated trials, enabling robust within-subject statistical analyses. 612 

In accordance with the ARRIVE guidelines and the principle of reduction, the number of 613 

animals was kept to the minimum necessary to achieve the scientific objectives while ensuring 614 

reproducibility. Statistical analyses were performed across repeated trials and sessions within 615 

each animal, rather than treating individual animals as the primary unit of analysis. 616
