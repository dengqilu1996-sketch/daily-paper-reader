> Multiple learning signals can shape motor output, including reward and punishment (via value-

26 

> based reinforcement learning) and sensorimotor error (via motor adaptation). However, it is

27 

> unclear if action values, learned via reinforcement learning, interact with error-based motor

28 

> learning. Here, we asked if the learned value of competing motor goals influences how the

29 

> motor system learns from sensorimotor errors linked to those goals. We designed a paradigm

30 

> that required participants (N = 85) to learn about the value associated with different movement

31 

> targets prior to undergoing implicit visuomotor adaptation at or nearby those same targets. We

32 

> observed two classes of related effects: repulsion and suppression. When adaptation brought

33 

> the limb toward targets associated with low-value, learning was attenuated compared to when

34 

> adaptation  brought  the  limb  away  from  targets  associated  with  low-value,  indicating  a

35 

> “repelling” effect of low-value actions. Moreover, adaptation was suppressed in all directions

36 

> when  low-value  targets  were  themselves  the  goal  of  movement.  These  findings  were

37 

> asymmetric with respect to valence: we did not observe comparable attraction effects when

38 

> adaptation brought the limb toward targets associated with high-value, nor did we see overall

39 

> enhancement of adaptation when targets associated with high-value acted as movement goals.

40 

> Additional analyses and experiments demonstrated that these effects did not reflect generic

41 

> biases and were driven by relative rather than absolute goal values. Repulsion and suppression

42 

> effects were tied to movement directions rather than the targets themselves. These results

43 

> point to a novel interaction between reinforcement learning and motor memory — a kind of

44 

> “instrumental-motor transfer.”

45 

46 

> Significance Statement

47 

> In order to ensure our motor behavior yields our desired outcomes, we must not only learn

48 

> which actions lead to which outcomes (via reinforcement learning) but also how to precisely

49 

> execute those actions to achieve the desired outcome (by calibrating our actions via implicit

50 

> motor adaptation. While it is clear that reinforcement learning and motor adaptation operate at

51 

> different levels of an action selection hierarchy, it is unclear if they interact. Knowing if and how

52 

> these processes interact is critical for understanding the fundamental algorithms and various

53 

> neural circuits underlying real-world motor behavior. Here, we show a novel interaction between

54 

> the two processes where implicit adaptation is suppressed when adaptation would bring the

55 

> hand toward a previously punished movement goal.

57 

> We move about the world in a complex landscape of movement goals, each with a different

58 

> learned or intrinsic value. For example, when wandering around the kitchen, moving your hand

59 

> towards a tasty snack is a much more valuable goal than moving it onto a hot stovetop. This

60 

> “affordance” landscape for action (Pezzulo and Cisek, 2016) is in part shaped by reinforcement

61 

> learning

(Sutton and Barto, 1998) , where we learn the value of different actions via the rewards 62 

> or  punishments  those  actions  tend  to  produce,  and  adjust  action  selection  accordingly

63 (Trommershäuser et al., 2008) .64 

65 

> At a lower level, our motor system must learn how to physically accomplish our movement

66 

> goals by generating motor commands that produce desired sensory consequences. Performing

67 

> movements accurately requires unconsciously calibrating motor commands to reduce errors, a

68 

> process known as motor adaptation (Shadmehr et al., 2010; Krakauer et al., 2019). While it is

69 

> clear that these two forms of learning — reinforcement learning and motor adaptation — are

70 

> distinct and operate at different levels of an action selection hierarchy, it is unclear if they

71 

> interact.

72 

73 

> Some evidence in the domain of visuomotor reach adaptation points to an interaction between

74 

> value-based learning signals (rewards and punishments) and motor adaptation. Galea et al.,

75 

> (2015) found that when people learned to counteract visuomotor perturbations to hit a target,

76 

> the addition of reward and punishment feedback significantly modulated motor learning. This

77 

> study and several more recent experiments (Kim et al., 2019; Khatib et al., 2023; Forano and

78 

> Franklin, 2024) are taken as evidence that the implicit motor adaptation system is sensitive not

79 

> only to sensory prediction errors (e.g., an unexpected sensory outcome of a movement to a

80 

> target) but also to motivational signals (e.g., earning $1 when hitting the target).

81 

82 

> However, other results have complicated the interpretations of these findings. First, recent work

83 

> has suggested that true implicit motor adaptation may not be sensitive to reward feedback

84 

> (Butcher and Taylor, 2018; Chen et al., 2025); reward may instead act on other learning

85 

> processes that operate in parallel to motor adaptation, such as deliberate aiming strategies

86 

> (Taylor and Ivry, 2013; McDougle et al., 2017; Holland et al., 2018; Krakauer et al., 2019;

87 

> Schween et al., 2020; Tsay et al., 2024; Hillman et al., 2025). Moreover, purported effects of so-

88 

> called intrinsic reward (successfully contacting a goal target) on adaptation are susceptible to

89 

> subtle methodological changes and may be better explained through alternative processes (Al-

90 

> Fawakhiri et al., 2023). In our view, it is still unclear whether value-based learning modulates

91 

> implicit motor adaptation, or if it affects motor output in other ways, perhaps by reinforcing

92 

> deliberate strategies or explicit motor memories, or by otherwise biasing the selection of

93 

> rewarded or successful actions

(Thorndike, 1911; Diedrichsen et al., 2010; Huang et al., 2011; 94 Holland et al., 2018; Sidarta et al., 2018; McDougle and Taylor, 2019; Hillman et al., 2024; 95 McDougle and Hillman, 2026) .96

98 

> feedback onto a motor adaptation task – which produces idiosyncratic effects – we had people

99 

> independently learn the value of competing movement goals prior to adaptation. That is, we

100 

> asked if the relative values of movement goals, learned via reinforcement learning, affect

101 

> subsequent adaptation to sensorimotor errors experienced at or around those movement goals.

102 

> To do this, we deployed a movement-based reinforcement learning task

(McDougle et al., 2016, 103 2019) interleaved with an implicit motor adaptation task  (Morehead et al., 2017) , and measured 104 

> the effect of the relative value of competing movement goals on adaptation. We hypothesized a

105 

> novel  interaction  between  reinforcement  learning  and  adaptation,  expecting  to  see  a

106 

> suppression of adaptation when it moved the effector toward lower-valued goals  and  a

107 

> facilitation of adaptation when it moved the effector toward higher-valued goals. Such results

108 

> would point to a computational link between the value landscape of our immediate environment

109 

> and the motor system’s automatic processes of sensorimotor learning.

> General Task Procedure

143 

> Participants were instructed to make fast but accurate slicing motions using a trackpad (or

144 

> mouse, if online) toward one of two targets presented on the screen. Two targets (16mm or

145 

> 60px diameter; Exps. 1-2: pink and purple; Exp. 3: green and gray) were presented 80mm (in-lab)

146 

> or 300px (online) from the central starting position (6mm or 24px diameter), centered in the

147 

> middle of the screen. Initially, the goal target was indicated by the color of the central starting

148 

> location, presented on the screen for 500ms prior to the appearance of the two targets. After

149 

> learning the reward association to each of the targets (see below), participants were instructed

150 

> to always go to either the more rewarded (Exps. 1-2) or less rewarded (Exp. 3) target; on these

154 

> Trials could provide one of three types of feedback. On veridical trials, participants controlled a

155 

> light blue cursor (4mm or 16px diameter) and veridical feedback was provided throughout the

156 

> entire reach and at endpoint. The cursor would freeze when the reach amplitude exceeded the

157 

> target radius (80mm or 300px). On “clamp” trials, the cursor was also displayed throughout the

158 

> entire reach and terminated at the target radius. However, the trajectory was constrained along

159 

> a fixed trajectory, 5.5˚ offset from the center of the goal target, such that the cursor would

160 

> straddle the edge of the target on every trial (Kim et al., 2019). Finally, on no-feedback trials, no

161 

> feedback was provided during movement and the trial ended after the participant’s reach

162 

> amplitude exceeded the target’s radial distance.

163 

164 

> If a trial was rewarded (see below), numerical points were provided only if the displayed cursor

165 

> (veridical or clamp) landed within the target (i.e., points were provided on all clamp trials). Point

166 

> feedback was provided at the same angular position as the cursor feedback, at a radial

167 

> amplitude that exceeded the target distance by 20% so that the text would be visible beyond the

168 

> target. If the cursor missed the target or was not displayed (as in no-feedback trials), no points

169 

> were displayed.

170 

171 

> Trials would begin with the presentation of the central starting location in the middle of the

172 

> screen for 500ms. Initially, the color of the starting location indicated which target was the

173 

> “goal” target. When the starting cue disappeared, it was replaced by the cursor, and the two

174 

> targets appeared. The pair of targets was presented either at [125˚ & 145˚] or [305˚ & 325˚],

175 

> separated by 20˚. Target locations alternated pseudorandomly such that targets could not

176 

> appear in the same quadrant for more than 4 trials in a row. Data from both quadrants was

177 

> rotated to a common reference frame and averaged for all analyses.

178 

179 

> Upon the appearance of the targets, participants were instructed to make a quick but accurate

180 

> swiping motion through the middle of the goal target. If the displayed cursor landed on the

181 

> target and the trial was rewarded, point feedback was provided along with endpoint feedback.

182 

> Otherwise, only endpoint feedback was provided. If the participant’s reach was greater than 30˚

183 

> away from the goal target, the reach duration exceeded 300 ms, the reaction time (time from

184 

> target appearance to reach initiation) exceeded 1000ms, or if the reach trajectory was not

185 

> straight (median absolute angular deviation greater than 10˚), endpoint feedback was replaced

186 

> (Exps. 2 & 3) or followed by (Exp. 1) a warning screen for 1000ms telling participants what their

The central starting location was

194 

> displayed on the screen for 500ms, and a fixed 800 ms intertrial interval was enforced. The

195 

> remainder of the 3.6 seconds was divided between reaction time, reach duration, and endpoint

196 

> feedback. For instance, if a participant had a 500ms reaction time, 100ms reach duration,

199 

200 

> The experiment began with a 96-trial baseline block where the goal target color and location

201 

> randomly varied ( Fig. 1A ). This phase was designed to help participants gain familiarity with the

202 

> center-out reaching task, and with moving to the target whose color matched the central

203 

> starting cue. Randomly interleaved throughout the block were 48 trials where both targets were

204 

> presented and 48 trials where only one target was presented (12 repetitions at each of the 4

205 

> possible target locations, 6 repetitions for each color). This was done to measure and control

206 

> for any baseline biases in reach direction caused by the presence of an alternative target

207 

> (Tipper et al., 1997). Target configurations and quadrants alternated pseudorandomly such that

208 

> no quadrant or configuration would appear more than 4 trials in a row. In total, participants

209 

> reached equally to each color and target location, reaching to the target whose color matched

210 

> the color of the central starting location presented at the start of the trial. Participants were

211 

> instructed to focus on reaching quickly but accurately through the middle of the goal target.

212 

213 

> After the baseline phase, participants were informed that the cursor would now be “clamped” to

214 

> a single direction: it would follow a fixed trajectory terminating slightly off-center on the edge of

215 

> the target, no matter where they actually moved their hand. Participants were instructed to

216 

> ignore the cursor and focus on bringing their actual reach to the center of the target, even

217 

> though they would not receive veridical feedback. This fixed error-clamp task has been shown

218 

> to reliably induce implicit motor adaptation without inducing explicit re-aiming strategies

219 

> (Morehead et al., 2017; Avraham et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022). We reasoned that by having the

220 

> cursor still visibly contact the target, participants would not explicitly interpret the movement as

221 

> a “miss”, though we still expected to see robust adaptation to the imposed subtle perturbation,

222 

> consistent with previous work (Kim et al., 2019; Al-Fawakhiri et al., 2023).

223 

224 

> After baseline, participants then began Control Phase 1 ( Fig. 1A ). This consisted of two 48-trial

225 

> blocks of clamped feedback at one of the two targets, with each clamp block followed by a 48-

226 

> trial washout block that was designed to de-adapt movements back to baseline. During

227 

> washout blocks, the first 24 trials were no-feedback, followed by 24 trials of veridical feedback

228 

> to fully wash out implicit adaptation (Avraham et al., 2021). The two clamp blocks alternated the

229 

> sign (+/-) of the clamped cursor rotation such that adaptation would proceed either toward the

230 

> alternative target (TOWARD) or away from it (AWAY). Whether participants started with a

231 

> TOWARD or AWAY block was counterbalanced. Since Control Phase 1 always went to one

232 

> target, we counterbalanced the color and location of that target. We note that all adaptation in

233 

> the  Control  Phase  clamp  blocks  occurs  without  any  learning  about  the  “values”  of  the

234 

> competing goals, allowing us to compare later adaptation behavior to the Control Phase data.

> After completing the four blocks of Control Phase 1, participants next completed the 48-trial

237 

> Reward Association Phase. Here the configuration of the targets swapped, such that if the red

238 

> target was placed clockwise relative to the green target in Control Phase 1, it would now be

239 

> counterclockwise (and vice versa). The Reward Association Phase used a forced-choice design,

240 

> where subjects were instructed to reach to a specific target on each trial. When the veridical

241 

> cursor feedback landed in the instructed target, participants would receive points. One of the

242 

> two targets was designated the “rewarded target”, which provided rewards (+10 points) on 80%

243 

> of trials and took away points (-5) on 20% of trials. The rewarded target was always the

244 

> opposite color as the target  participants reached to in Control Phase 1, to  ensure that

245 

> participants were paying attention to the task contingencies rather than adopting a simple

246 

> strategy of recalling the color of the target they most frequently reached to. The other target, the

247 

> “punished target”, subtracted points (-5) on 80% of trials and provided points (+10) on 20% of

248 

> trials. We emphasize that participants were instructed to always reach to the target that

249 

> matched the color of the central starting location, even if this involved going to the punished

250 

> target and likely losing points. They were further instructed to track which target provided more

251 

> points than the other, and were informed that they would be later asked to identify the more

252 

> favorable target at the end of the block. For the first 12 trials, participants went to one color

253 

> target. They then went to the other target for the next 12 trials. After these first 24 trials, the

254 

> target configuration switched again. Participants continued reaching to the same color target,

255 

> now in a different location, for 12 trials. Finally, for the last 12 trials, participants went to the

256 

> other color target. This ensured that participants received equal amounts of reward and

257 

> punishment history at each reach location and that the association was only to the color of the

258 

> target, and we counterbalanced whether participants started at the rewarded or punished target.

259 

> To ensure that at least 80% of trials were rewarded at the rewarded target and punished at the

260 

> punished target, we kept track of the percentage of reversed outcomes (e.g. punishment at the

261 

> rewarded  target)  and,  if  this  exceeded  20%  within  the  block,  the  random  outcome  was

262 

> overwritten. At the end of the Reward Association block, participants were asked to explicitly

263 

> identify the rewarded target. All participants correctly identified the rewarded target.

> To visualize adaptation learning curves, trials were binned in cycles of two trials, reflecting the

337 

> number of potential target pair locations. All learning curves were “baseline-corrected” such that

338 

> the average of the first two cycles was subtracted from all cycles in each block. This was

339 

> especially important, as competing target values could simply induce a fixed movement bias

340 

> instead of a modulation of adaptation. With this baseline correcting, learning within a block

341

342

> Two methods were employed to quantify differences in adaptation between TOWARD versus

345 

> AWAY blocks. First, we averaged the last two cycles within each block to get a measure of how

346 

> much  total  (baseline-corrected)  adaptation  occurred  within  each  block/phase  for  each

347 

> participant. We then employed paired t-tests (or two-way ANOVAs) to quantify differences

348 

> between  adaptation  in  TOWARD  versus  AWAY  blocks.  We  also  performed  exploratory

349 

> nonparametric cluster permutation tests to visualize specific learning epochs where hand

350 

> angles significantly differed between TOWARD and AWAY blocks (Maris and Oostenveld, 2007;

351 

> Avraham and Ivry, 2025). To do this, we first performed a single paired one-tailed t-test on each

352 

> cycle of the learning curves between TOWARD and AWAY blocks. If the initial paired t-test of the

353 

> last two cycles showed significantly greater adaptation AWAY vs TOWARD in a given phase, this

354 

> was used as the prediction in the one-tailed t-test of each cycle of that phase. If the initial

355 

> comparison  happened  to  be  null, we  assumed  adaptation  AWAY  would  be  greater  than

356 

> TOWARD. A “cluster” was then defined as any string of adjacent significant cycles (p<0.05). For

357 

> each cluster, we summed the t-statistic for each cycle to obtain a “t-sum statistic” for the

358 

> cluster. We then computed a null distribution of t-sum statistics by randomly permuting the

359 

> AWAY and TOWARD labels for each cycle 10,000 times. For each permutation, we identified

547 

> Recent studies of human motor learning point to an interaction between value-based learning

548 

> and error-based implicit motor adaptation (Galea et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2019; Forano and

549 

> Franklin, 2024). However, the interpretation of these results has been complicated by various

550 

> alternative  explanations,  including  the  use  of  explicit  strategies  and  task  error  signals

551 

> associated with non-adaptation-based forms of motor learning (Butcher and Taylor, 2018; Al-

552 

> Fawakhiri et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025). Here, we take a novel approach by first training

553 

> participants on  the relative values of two competing action goals in the environment, and then

554 

> asking if this learned association shapes implicit adaptation in the vicinity of these goals.

555 

> Across three experiments (N = 85), we demonstrate that implicit adaptation is suppressed

556 

> around low-value movement goals ( Figs. 2-3 ). These findings point to a close interaction

557 

> between value-based learning and implicit motor adaptation.

558 

559 

> More  specifically,  in  Test  Phase  1 of  Experiment  1,  we  showed  that  participants  adapt

560 

> significantly less when adaptation brings the effector toward a low-value, punished movement

561 

> goal, in a manner that cannot be explained by baseline motor biases ( Fig. 2 ). In Test Phase 2,

562 

> where  the  previously  punished  movement  direction  now  acted  as  the  movement  goal,

563 

> adaptation was suppressed in both directions. We extended these findings in Experiment 2 by

564 

> showing that “reversing” the learned reward association to particular reach directions shifts the

565 

> movement directions where adaptation was suppressed ( Fig. 3 ). That is, after learning a

566 

> “swapped”  reward  association  (in  terms  of  reach  direction,  but  not  goal  identity/color),

567 

> participants exhibited suppressed adaptation toward the  new  location of the punished target.

568 

> While Experiments 1 and 2 showed that adaptation was suppressed around previously  punished

569 

> reach goals, Experiment 3 showed suppression of adaptation toward a neutral reach location

570 

> that was merely  relatively  devalued compared to an alternative reach goal. This suggests that

571 

> the suppression of adaptation to low-valued action goals is not determined by the absolute

572 

> reward value of the goal but rather by its relative value compared to alternative, competing

573 

> goals.

574 

575 

> To our surprise, Experiment 3 failed to show significant reinforcement-attraction effects toward

576 

> previously rewarded reach directions. Numerically, adaptation in Test Phase 1 of Experiment 3

577 

> and Test Phase 2 of Experiment 1 pointed to a weak attraction effect but these effects were not

578 

> significant. Speculatively, this null finding may mirror asymmetric “loss aversion” effects seen in

579 

> many economic decision-making tasks (Sokol-Hessner and Rutledge, 2019); that is, the relative

580 

> saliency of the low-value target in our design may have been much higher than the high-value

581 

> target, making a potential high-value goal attractor effect difficult to detect. Prior work has

582 

> shown that loss aversion modulates the degree to which action values and incentives influence

583 

> motor behavior, an effect that is sensitive to contextual framing (Chib et al., 2012, 2014; Dunne

584 

> et al., 2019). Further, as will be discussed below, our effects suggest that information about the

585 

> value of a particular reach direction is shared across other similar reach directions. This “value-

586 

> generalization” may occur over a wider range of reach directions when an action is punished

587 

> than when that action is rewarded, mirroring results regarding the generalization of appetitive

> Our findings point to an interaction between value-based reinforcement learning and error-

612 

> based implicit motor adaptation. How might this interaction play out computationally? We

613 

> propose that the relative values of competing reach goals creates a kind of adaptation capacity

614 

> gradient ( Fig. 4A ), which can lead to repulsion ( Fig. 4B ) or local suppression effects ( Fig. 4C )

615 

> depending on the current movement demands. This framework echoes recent work that has

616 

> highlighted the utility of “policy-gradient” models to explain motor behavior in a variety of

617 

> adaptation and de-novo motor skill learning contexts (Haith, 2025; Greenstreet et al., 2026). In

618 

> these models, the action space is parameterized across the relevant task dimensions; in our

619 

> case, this would involve parameterizing movements according to the associated reach angle

620 

> (though  more  complex  tasks  may  require  higher  dimensional  representations).  This

621 

> parameterized, continuous action space provides the basis for learning a value landscape

622 

> across movement goals ( Fig. 4A ). Importantly, learned values for any particular action should

623 

> “spread” to adjacent actions in this space, in line with observed generalization effects of motor

624 

> adaptation (Greenstreet et al., 2026).

> motor performance (Galaro et al., 2019). Here, we highlight that punishment may similarly

648 

> locally suppress adaptation to motor errors. This proposed interaction of the basal ganglia and

649 

> cerebellum may serve to shape motor learning in a manner that ultimately avoids potentially

650 

> maladaptive actions. Moreover, the cerebellum itself has been shown to encode reward and

651 

> punishment feedback among other reinforcement learning signals like prediction error (Wagner

652 

> et al., 2017; Heffley and Hull, 2019; Kostadinov et al., 2019; Trach et al., 2026); thus, intra-

653 

> cerebellar processes may also underlie interactions between the value domain and motor

654 

> learning.  Further  investigations,  perhaps  using  neuroimaging,  could  help  elucidate  the

655 

> underlying  neural  mechanisms  of  the  suppression  of  adaptation  we  observed  here.  In

656 

> conclusion, our study provides novel behavioral evidence that implicit motor adaptation is

657 

> sensitive to value-based information about action goals in the environment. These findings

658 

> highlight  a potential  “Instrumental-Motor  Transfer”  between  reinforcement  learning

659 

> computations and motor memories.
