Abstract :22 

In a previous study  by our group  (Nold et al., 2025) , we  investigated  exercise -induced pain 23 

modulation after high -intensity com pared to low -intensity exercise  in a heterogeneous sample 24 

of diverse fitness levels . Exploratory  analyses suggested an interaction of  sex, fit ness level, and 25 

drug treatment , indicating that m ales showed increasing hypoalgesia after  high - compared to 26 

low -intensity  exercise with increasing fitness levels , which was diminished  when naloxone was 27 

administered . In contrast,  these  effects were not evident in females. These exploratory findings 28 

warranted further investigation to d etermin e if and to what extent  exercise -induced pain 29 

modulation  depend s on fitness level and /or  sex.  In this current study , we investigated an all -30 

female sample  (N = 21)  of high fitness levels using a similar paradigm as in the previous study ,31 

comparing heat and pressure  pain ratings after high -intensity and low -intensity exercise.  Our 32 

data  show an interaction of exercise intensity and stimulus intensity  in  heat pain, with greater 33 

pain relief following  high -intensity exercise,  especially at the highest stimulus intensity. 34 

Despite results for pressure pain not reaching significance, a similar trend was evident.  These 35 

results suggest that  females  at a high  fitness level  also  show  exercise -induced pain modulation 36 

for  high -intensity compared to low -intensity exercise . Furthermore, we pooled the data of the 37 

current and previous study , providing  evidence for fitness level but not sex  to moderate 38 

exercise -induced pain modulation  in heat pain. These  findings suggest that  exercise -induced 39 

pain modulation  depend s on fitness level but not on sex. 40

Introduction 43 

In a previous study  (Nold et al., 2025) , we investigated the effect of high -intensity  (HI) and 44 

low -intensity (LI) aerobic exercise on pain perception in a heterogeneous sample of both sexes 45 

and diverse fitness levels  (N = 39, 21 females) , where e xploratory analyses revealed a three -46 

way interaction of sex, fit ness level, and drug treatment . Males showed  greater  hypoalgesia 47 

after HI compared to LI exercise with increasing fitness levels . This effect  was  reduced  when 48 

the μ -opioid antagonist  naloxone was administered. These effects were not evident in females. 49 

Taken together, these  results  point to  the  nuanced  interplay of individual factors  such as fitness 50 

level and sex  underlying exercise -induced hypoalgesia. 51 

Repeated  aerobic exercise has been shown to be associated with the activation of endogenous 52 

neuromodulatory systems  implicated in pain modulation  (for review see  Gajic et al., 2026) ,53 

such as  the  endogenous opioid  system  (Koltyn et al., 2014; Saanijoki et al., 2022)  and 54 

endocannabinoid  system  (Matei et al., 2023) . Correspondingly , p revious research  in humans 55 

(Geva and Defrin, 2013; Schmitt et al., 2020; Sluka et al., 2018)  suggests  that fitness level  play s56 

a crucial role  in  exercise -induced pain modulation , where  subjects  with  higher  fitness  levels 57 

show ed  increased  hypoalgesia following exercise  compared to subjects with lower fitness levels 58 

(Schmitt et al., 2020) . Correspondingly , higher  self -reported fitness levels  were  shown to be 59 

associated with  reduced  pain ratings  (Geva and Defrin, 2013) , reduced  temporal summation 60 

(Naugle and Riley, 2014) , and  greater opioid release  in the  orbitofrontal  cortex and insula 61 

(Saanijoki et al., 2022) . Crucially,  most  of these studies  (Saanijoki et al., 2022; Schmitt et al., 62 

2020)  have  been conducted in  healthy all -male  samples , limiting the  translation  to  females, 63 

where  the  association  of  physical  fitness and pain  perception  after exercise  remains  less clear 64 

(de Bruijn et al., 2011) .65 

Despite  the majority of chronic pain patients  being  female  (Fillingim et al., 2009; Mogil, 2012; 66 

Rice et al., 2019) , evidence  on  potential sex differences in exercise -induced pain modulation  is 67 

surprisingly limited  (Koltyn et al., 2014) . Of those studies that have considered potentia l sex 68 

differences in exercise -induced  pain modulation,  some  (Brellenthin et al., 2017; Koltyn et al., 69 

2014; Niwa et al., 2022; Smith, 2004)  could show that there are no  sex  differences , whereas 70 

others  (Koltyn, 2000; Sternberg et al., 2001; Vaegter et al., 2014)  suggest  that hypoalgesia 71 

following exercise is more  pronounced  in females,  potentially due to  sex differences in the 72 

descending antinociceptive circuit s (Fullerton et al., 2018; Mogil, 2012) , but overall results 73 

remain inconclusive  (Rice et al., 2019) .74

This  current  behavioural  study aims to build on our previous findings  (Nold et al., 2025)  by 75 

investigating exercise -induced pain modulation in an independent all -female sample ( N = 21) 76 

with a  high  fitness level using the same paradigm as in the previous study , without a 77 

pharmacological intervention.  Importantly, fitness levels  (quantified by the weight -corrected 78 

functional threshold power (FTP) ) in the previous study  (Nold et al., 2025)  differed 79 

significantly  (P =  0.003)  between females ( M = 1.58,  SD  = 0.44 ) and  males  (M = 2.03,  SD  =80 

0.40 ), t hus,  warranting  further investigation  into  whether exercise -induced hypoalgesia after  HI 81 

compared  to LI  aerobic  exercise  can also be observed  in females  with a  higher fitness .82 

Disentangling if and to what extent exercise -induced hypoalgesia depend s on fitness level and 83 

sex is a crucial step to  determine  exercise -based  interventions for (chronic) pain  populations. 84

Discussion 308 

In this  study , we investigated  exercise -induced pain modulation between high -intensity  (HI) 309 

and  low -intensity  (LI)  exercise in a sample of females  (N = 21)  of  relatively  higher fitness 310 

level s. In a previous study by our group  (Nold et al., 2025) , exploratory analyses suggested  a311 

three -way interaction  of  fitness levels, sex , and  drug treatment , where males showed 312 

hypoalgesia after HI compared to LI exercise with increasing fitness levels , which was 313 

diminished when naloxone was administered. These effects were not evident in females.  In  this 314 

current study , we  aimed to explore whether exercise -induced pain modulation is also evident in 315 

females of higher fitness levels and whether this is moderated by fitness level or sex. We  could 316 

show that , for heat pain , females  from the current study  showed  pain relief after HI compared 317 

to LI exercise , especially for  highly  painful stimuli . A similar trend was  observed for  pressure 318 

pain.  To place these findings in the context of our earlier observations, we pooled data from the 319 

current and previous studies and examined the influence of fitness and sex on exercise -induced 320 

pain modulation . Across the combined sample, both frequentist and Bayesian analyses 321 

indicated that fitness was associated with  exercise -induced hypoalgesia , whereas there was little 322 

evidence for sex -dependent effects. Together, these findings provide support for a role of fitness 323 

level  in explaining variability in  exercise -induced pain modulation , while offering little 324 

evidence that  exercise -induced pain modulation  differ s systematically between males and 325 

females. 326

A multitude  of findings  on  exercise -induced hypoalgesia  have been derived from  earlier studies 375

pain perception  (Gajic et al., 2026; Song et al., 2022) .393

Concerning the mechanisms of sex potentially influencing  exercise -induced pain modulation ,394 

the  literature remains scarce and rather inconclusive. Since exercise has been linked to the 395 

release of endogenous opioids  (Haier et al., 1981b; Janal et al., 1984)  and, more recen tly, 396 

endocannabinoids  (Crombie et al., 2018; Siebers et al., 2022, 2021) , sex differences in these 397 

neuromodulatory  systems might also contribute to the different expressions of  exercise -induced 398 

pain modulation  between the sexes.  In terms of endogenous opioid signalling, previous studies 399 

indicate that men show  larger mu -opioid receptor  (MOR)  activation in pain -related regions th an 400 

women during sustained pain  (Zubieta et al., 2002) . Furthermore, sex differences in opioid 401 

analgesia remain controversial  (Fullerton et al., 2018) , pointing towards sex differences in the 402 

descending antinociceptive circuit. Specifically in the PAG, male rats showed higher MOR 403 

expression and bin ding compared with female rats  (Loyd et al., 2008) . Interestingly, increasing 404 

evidence in rodents also points towards sex differences in the expression and function of the 405 

endocanna binoid system in pain  (Blanton et al., 2021) , such as lower CB1 receptor density  in 406 

the prefrontal cortex and amygdala  (Castelli et al., 2014)  or lower 2 -AG concentration in the 407 

PAG  (Levine et al., 2021)  of female than male rodents. These  regions  are critical parts of the 408 

DPMS  (Bingel and Tracey, 2008; Borras et al., 2004; Eippert et al., 2009; Petrovic et al., 2002) 409 

and  are  also involved in mediating  exercise -induced pain modulation  (Geisler et al., 2019; Nold 410 

et al., 2025; Scheef et al., 2012) .411 

Of those studies tha t have included  both sexes in their sample s (Naugle et al., 2014; Niwa et 412 

al., 2022) , a dose -response  relationship  of aerobic  exercise  intensity and the ana lgesic response 413 

could be shown in the overall sample , where  high -intensity  (70%  heart rate reserve (HRR) )414 

aerobic exercise produced larger  hypoalgesic  effect s compared to moderate -intensity  (50% 415 

HRR) exercise  (Naugle et al., 2014; Niwa et al., 2022)  and  low -intensity (30% HRR)  exercise 416 

(Niwa et al., 2022) . Importantly, despite including  both sexes , these studies did not  include  sex 417 

as a factor in their  analyse s,  potentially  confounding the observed  effects  (Tesarz et al., 2012) .418 

Of those studies that  have explicitly  accounted for  sex as a moderating variable in  exercise -419 

induced  hypoalgesia , some studies  could  show  a hypoalgesic  response  from exercise  in both 420 

sexes  (Koltyn et al., 2014, 2001) , whereas other s (Gajsar et al.,  2017; Lemley et al., 2016)  found 421 

a stronger hypoalgesic response in females.  When  administering  an  aerobic  exercise  protocol ,422 

one study found the  hypo algesic response  following exercise to  be  more prono unce d in females 423 

compared to males  (Vaegter et al., 2014) .424

Limitations 448 

On the experimental day,  heat stimuli with a target  intensity  of  VAS 30  were  rated below the 449 

pain threshold. T he anticipated intensities of VAS 50 and VAS 70 were rated  above the pain 450 

thre shold , suggesting  that  they were perceived as painful.  However , the lower pain ratings on 451 

the experimental day as opposed to the calibration day are likely due to habituation effects 452 

between days  (Ellerbrock et al., 2015; May et al., 2012) . Furthermore, despite assessing 453 

exercise -related expectations  and identifying participants to expect decreased whole -body pain 454 

following exercise,  we did  not experimentally  manipulate those expectations , precluding causal 455 

conclusions regarding their  relative  contribution to the observed effects. Although expectations 456 

can modulate pain perception  (Colloca et al., 2018; Jones et al., 2017) , p ositive exercise -related 457 

expectations alone do not appear to augment exercise -induced hypoalgesia  (Vaegter et al., 458 

2020) , suggesting that exercise -related expectations do not account for our findings . Moreover ,459

Conclusion 471 

In this study , we could show that  participants with  relatively  higher fitness level s (as measure d472 

by weight -corrected FTP)  experience  greater  pain relief following high -intensity compared to 473 

low -intensity exercise,  especially  at high pain intensities.  Together with our previous findings, 474 

these results suggest that fitness level may play a more  central  role than sex in explaining 475 

variability in exercise -induced pain modulation. 476

Bayesian model comparison 600 

Bayesian  model comparison was conducted  using  the  Bayes Factor ( BF 10 ) estimated via bridge 601 

sampl ing  for all  frequentist  linear model  analy ses. The  Bayes  Factor quantifies the  relative 602 

evidence  provided  by the data for one model over another by comparing their marginal 603 

likelihoods.  Specifically , BF 10  expresses the evidence for the more complex model relative  to604 

a simpler model. Generally,  BF 10  > 1 indicates support for the more complex model o ver  the 605 

simpler model and vice versa for  BF 10  < 1. According to the conventional evidence thresholds 606 

(Lovric, 2025) , a 1 < BF 10  < 3  indicates  anecdotal/weak evidence, 3 <  BF 10  < 10 moderate 607 

evidence , and  BF 10  > 10 strong evidence in favour of the complex model.  BF 10  close to 1 608 

indicates insensitive or inconclusive evidence for the complex over the simpler model. We 609 

computed the Bayes factor for the main effects model vs  the  null model,  the  interaction model 610 

vs. main effect model, and  the  full model vs. null model. 611
