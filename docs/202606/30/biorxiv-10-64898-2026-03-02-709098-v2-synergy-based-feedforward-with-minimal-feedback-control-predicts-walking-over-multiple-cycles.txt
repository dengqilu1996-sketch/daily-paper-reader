Abstract 12 

Neural feedback is important for the control of movement, and multiple neurological disorders (e.g., 13 stroke, cerebral palsy, Parkinson’s disease, incomplete spinal cord injury) are characterized by altered 14 neural feedback. Researchers have created numerous computational neuromusculoskeletal models 15 controlled by simulated neural feedback mechanisms, but these models rarely represent actual human 16 subjects and thus have not found practical clinical application. As a step toward designing patient-17 specific treatments for individuals with neurological disorders, this study used the 18 Neuromusculoskeletal Modeling Pipeline to develop and evaluate a novel synergy-based feedforward 19 (FF)+feedback (FB) control model using a personalized three-dimensional neuromusculoskeletal 20 walking model of an actual human subject post-stroke. Experimental walking data collected from the 21 subject were used to create the subject’s personalized walking model. Then for 5 calibration walking 22 cycles, personalized synergy-based FF+FB control models were created. First, the personalized 23 model was used to estimate lower body muscle activations consistent with the subject’s 24 electromyographic, joint motion, and joint moment data. Second, five synergy activations per leg 25 with associated synergy vectors were calculated that closely reconstructed the subject’s muscle 26 activations and joint moments simultaneously. Third, nominal FF synergy activation controls were 27 calculated by averaging the synergy activations for each leg. Fourth, the nominal FF synergy controls 28 were scaled by 0, 25, 50, 75, 100, and 125%, and the gap in reproducing the subject’s muscle 29 activations was filled by fitting FB synergy activation controls as a function of joint positions, 30 velocities, and moments as surrogates for muscle lengths, muscle velocities, and tendon forces. Next 31 for 3 testing walking cycles, the six synergy-based FF+FB models were used to control the subject’s 32 personalized walking model in predictive simulations. The 100% FF model (which still had minimal 33 FB) reproduced the testing walking cycles the most closely, and only the 75%, 100%, and 125% FF 34 models predicted near-periodic walking motions using initial conditions consistent with experimental 35 values. 1 Introduction 40 

Neural feedback is an essential component of the neuromuscular control of movement. Neural 41 feedback helps individuals adapt their walking to new situations or perturbations (Severini and Zych, 42 2022), and reflexes help prevent dangerous slips and falls (McCrum et al., 2017). Disruptions to 43 these mechanisms can produce muscle spasticity, a harmful velocity-dependent feedback mechanism 44 that contributes to movement impairment and health hazards (Jansen et al., 2014; Trompetto et al., 45 2014; Ang et al., 2018; Falisse et al., 2018). Dysfunctional neural feedback is present in conditions 46 such as cerebral palsy (Falisse et al., 2018), spinal cord injuries (Trompetto et al., 2014; Ang et al., 47 2018), stroke (Jansen et al., 2014; Ang et al., 2018), and Parkinson’s disease (Kim et al., 2009). 48 However, a thorough understanding of how dysfunctional feedback mechanisms work remains 49 elusive, hindering the development of more effective treatments for these conditions. 50 Researchers have explored the use of computational neuromusculoskeletal models to develop a better 51 understanding of human reflex contributions to walking and movement impairments (Geyer and 52 Herr, 2010; Song and Geyer, 2013; Jansen et al., 2014; Falisse et al., 2018; Haeufle et al., 2018; Di 53 Russo et al., 2021; Lassmann et al., 2023; Su and Gutierrez-Farewik, 2023; Koseki et al., 2024). 54 Reflex mechanisms are typically modeled at the level of individual muscles and have included stretch 55 reflexes and Golgi tendon reflexes (Geyer and Herr, 2010; Jansen et al., 2014; Trompetto et al., 2014; 56 Ang et al., 2018; Falisse et al., 2018; Haeufle et al., 2018). While complicated neural networks in the 57 brain control movement, simple walking models can be successfully controlled by feedback 58 mechanisms alone (Geyer and Herr, 2010). However, these models can be made more robust by 59 combining feedforward and feedback control (Haeufle et al., 2018). 60 While most studies have assumed neural feedback mechanisms control individual muscles, several 61 studies have explored feedback control of muscle synergies. In a computational study, 62 neuromusculoskeletal models walked and ran at different speeds using similar control structures 63 activating muscle synergies rather than individual muscles (Aoi et al., 2019). In an experimental 64 study of subjects attempting to stand upright while being perturbed, muscle synergy controls could be 65 fitted using feedback quantities related to a target motion, such as center of mass location (Safavynia 66 and Ting, 2013), supporting the use of muscle synergies in human movement simulations. Additional 67 studies have reported that neural feedback for the control of movement in general and walking in 68 particular may in part come through muscle synergies (Ting and McKay, 2007; Chvatal and Ting, 69 2013). It is notable, however, that most past simulation studies have predicted general movement 70 characteristics or limited experimental data rather than the response of a specific subject under new 71 conditions. Furthermore, no previous study has addressed the issue of how much feedback control is 72 needed to simulate walking performed by an actual human subject. 73 As a step toward addressing this question, this study describes the development and evaluation of a 74 novel process for creating personalized synergy-based feedforward (FF)+feedback (FB) neural 75 control models to predict three-dimensional (3D) walking for an individual post-stroke. The study is 76 made possible by a personalized neuromusculoskeletal walking model created from experimental 77 walking data collected from the subject. Personalized FF+FB neural control models are implemented 78 using muscle synergies. Models with different ratios of FF to FB control, combined using an 79 approach from a previously published study (Haeufle et al., 2018), are calibrated using 5 cycles of 80

2 Materials and Methods 87 

2.1 Experimental Data 88 

For this modeling study, we used published experimental walking data collected with written 89 informed consent from a male stroke survivor (age 79 years, LE Fugl-Meyer Motor Assessment 90 32/34 pts, right-sided hemiparesis, height 1.7 m, mass 80.5 kg) (Meyer et al., 2016). The walking data 91 included marker-based motion capture data collected using a modified Cleveland Clinic full-body 92 marker set with additional foot markers (Reinbolt et al., 2005) (Vicon Corp., Oxford, UK), ground 93 reaction force and moment data collected using a split-belt instrumented treadmill (Bertec Corp., 94 Columbus, OH, USA), and electromyographic (EMG) data collected from 16 muscles per leg 95 (Motion Lab Systems, Baton Rouge, LA, USA). Data collected during a standing static pose were 96 used for model scaling purposes (see below), while data collected during 30 seconds of treadmill 97 walking at a self-selected speed of 0.5 m/s were used for the feedback control study. 98 EMG data for each leg were collected using a combination of surface and fine wire electrodes. 99 Surface electrodes were used for gluteus maximus and medius, semimembranosus, biceps femoris 100 long head, rectus femoris, vastus medialis and lateralis, medial gastrocnemius, tibialis anterior, 101 peroneus longus, and soleus. Fine wire electrodes were used for adductor longus, iliopsoas, tibialis 102 posterior, extensor digitorum longus, and flexor digitorum longus. Raw EMG data were converted to 103 muscle excitation envelopes using standard EMG processing methods (Lloyd and Besier, 2003). 104

2.2.1 Joint and Ground Contact Model Personalization 124 

A generic full-body OpenSim (Seth et al., 2018) musculoskeletal model (Hammond et al., 2025) 125 derived from a previously published OpenSim model (Rajagopal et al., 2016) was used as a baseline. 126 Forearm pronation-supination and knee adduction degrees of freedom (DOFs) were locked, leaving 127 the generic model with 31 DOFs. These DOFs included a 6 DOF ground-to-pelvis joint, two 3 DOF 128 hip joints, two 1 DOF knee joints, two 1 DOF ankle joints, two 1 DOF subtalar joints, two 1 DOF toe 129 joints linking the hindfoot and toes, a 3 DOF back joint, two 3 DOF shoulder joints, and two 1 DOF 130 elbow joints. The model included 86 Hill-type muscle-tendon actuators with rigid tendons (43 per 131 leg). The model was scaled in OpenSim using the static pose data with bodies and joints scaled 132 symmetrically. 133 The skeletal geometry and joint parameters of the scaled model were personalized to the subject’s 134 experimental data using the Joint Model Personalization tool within the NMSM Pipeline (Hammond 135 et al., 2025). The tool optimized joint axis orientations, isotropic body scale factors, and marker 136 locations to reduce inverse kinematics (IK) marker distance errors while seeking to reproduce the 137 motion of a portion of the 30 second walking trial. The tool was allowed to change joint axis 138 orientation parameters in the knee, ankle, and subtalar joints, scaling of the pelvis, femur, and tibia 139 bodies, and the locations of markers attached to the femur and tibia bodies. The cost function 140 penalized IK marker distance errors. The calibrated model was then used with OpenSim’s Inverse 141 Kinematics tool to solve for joint angles that reproduced the experimental marker motion data as 142 closely as possible. The IK results were filtered using a fourth-order zero phase lag Butterworth filter 143 with a cutoff frequency of 7/tf Hz, where tf is the average period of a gait cycle from the 30 second 144 recording (Hug, 2011). 145 After the skeletal structure was personalized and IK joint angles were calculated, the Ground Contact 146 Model Personalization (GCP) tool within the NMSM Pipeline (Hammond et al., 2025) was used to 147 generate a subject-specific elastic foundation foot-ground contact model that was the same for both 148 feet. Prior to running the tool (and for future steps), the experimental ground reaction forces and 149 moments were filtered using a second-order zero phase lag Butterworth filter with a cutoff frequency 150 of 7/tf Hz. The GCP tool automatically placed 37 viscoelastic+friction contact elements across the 151 hindfoot and toes segments of each foot and calibrated each contact element’s stiffness coefficient, a 152 common nonlinear damping coefficient, a common dynamic friction coefficient, and a common 153 spring resting length defining the height at which the contact elements penetrated the ground. To 154 calibrate these parameters, the optimization cost function penalized errors in reproducing all three 155 components of the experimental ground reaction forces and moments while making minimal 156 adjustments to the experimental foot kinematics with respect to ground. To prevent unrealistic 157 discontinuities in contact element stiffness values, the cost function also minimized the deviation of 158 each contact element stiffness value away from neighboring values using a Gaussian-weighted 159 average, where stiffness values from nearer elements had a higher weight. In addition, to make the 160 ground reaction data more consistent with the marker motion data and the dynamics of the skeletal 161 model, the cost function made small adjustments to the location of each force plate in the lab 162 coordinate system (Williams et al., 2025). The adjusted ground reaction data were used when 163 calculating joint moments with OpenSim’s Inverse Dynamics (ID) tool. 164

2.2.2 Torque-Driven Tracking Optimization 165 

Joint angle, ID, and ground reaction data were used in the Tracking Optimization (TO) tool within 166 the NMSM Pipeline (Hammond et al., 2025) to generate dynamically consistent torque-controlled 167 walking simulations of individual gait cycles to be used in place of experimental data. From the full 168 30 seconds of walking data, 8 gait cycles with clean marker motion, ground reaction, and EMG data 169 were identified. Each gait cycle started with right foot heelstrike, and IK, ID, and ground reaction 170 data for these 8 cycles were split into separate data files, each splined to 101 time points. The data for 171 each gait cycle were used to provide an initial guess and tracked quantities for direct collocation 172 optimal control motion prediction problems solved using the TO tool. The values of coordinate 173 positions, velocities, and accelerations were allowed to change, resulting in different ground reactions 174 and joint loads, and torque controllers were used to control the motion of the hip, knee, ankle, and 175 subtalar joints. The arms and torso did not have associated controllers, but their coordinate positions, 176 velocities, and accelerations were included as design variables. The cost function minimized tracking 177 errors in joint angles, joint velocities, joint accelerations, ground reaction forces and moments, ID 178 joint loads, and heel marker positions and velocities. Constraints were used to maintain joint 179 coordinate and joint speed periodicity and initial and final values of ground reaction forces and 180 moments. Additional constraints enforced that torque controls calculated during the optimization stay 181 within 0.1 Nm of their respective ID joint loads and that residual ID loads calculated for the pelvis, 182 corresponding to the 6 DOF ground-to-pelvis joint, were less than 1 N or 0.1 Nm in magnitude. 183 Dynamically consistent TO results for each of the 8 gait cycles were used in future steps. 184 

2.2.3 Muscle-tendon and Neural Control Model Personalization 185 

The Muscle Tendon Personalization (MTP) tool within the NMSM Pipeline (Hammond et al., 2025) 186 was used to calibrate muscle-tendon model properties. Based on the TO results for each of the 8 gait 187 cycles, time-varying muscle length, velocity, and moment arm data were calculated for each muscle. 188 These data were filtered with using the same protocol as IK joint angles. EMG data were high-pass 189 filtered at 40 Hz, demeaned, rectified, low-pass filtered with a cut-off frequency of 3.5/tf Hz (Meyer 190 et al., 2016), normalized to a maximum value of 1, split into separate data files for each cycle, and 191 splined to 101 time points plus a cycle-dependent number of padding frames. The padding frames 192 added 200 ms of data before and after the one-cycle time range used by ID to accommodate EMG 193 electromechanical delay. The MTP tool calibrated muscle-tendon parameters and solved for time-194 varying activations for each muscle using EMG data. Muscle-specific values for electromechanical 195 delays, activation time constants, activation nonlinearity constants, EMG scale factors, optimal 196 muscle fiber lengths, and tendon slack lengths were calculated. In addition, synergy extrapolation 197 (Ao et al., 2022) was used to estimate the excitations of hip rotator muscles, tensor fasciae latae, and 198 sartorius, which muscles that did not have experimental EMG data. The cost function penalized: 1) 199 errors in hip flexion, hip adduction, knee, ankle, and subtalar joint moments produced by muscles 200 relative to ID loads from the torque-driven TO simulations; 2) extrapolated muscle excitations; 3) 201 changes to measured excitations made by the synergy extrapolation process; 4) deviations from error 202 centers in activation time constants, optimal muscle fiber lengths, tendon slack lengths, EMG scale 203 factors, and normalized muscle fiber lengths; 5) activation nonlinearity constants; 6) passive muscle 204 forces; 7) muscle excitations; 8) deviations within defined muscle groups in normalized fiber length, 205 and 8) electromechanical delays. The optimization was performed for all 8 gait cycles simultaneously 206 to ensure all cycles used consistent muscle-tendon properties. 207 Next, the Neural Control Model Personalization (NCP) tool within the NMSM Pipeline (Hammond 208 et al., 2025) was used to impose a synergy structure on the MTP muscle activations while ensuring 209 consistency with ID joint moments. 4 4 Discussion 302 

This study developed a subject-specific neuromusculoskeletal model with a synergy-based FF+FB 303 lower body control model capable of reproducing walking motions measured from an actual human 304 subject. To our knowledge, this study is the first to perform predictive simulations of walking using a 305 combined FF+FB control model applied to a personalized 3D neuromusculoskeletal model and to 306 evaluate the predictions against actual walking data collected from the subject being modeled. As the 307 simplest possible starting point, the study used FB models with joint-level (rather than muscle-level) 308 inputs and synergy-level (rather than muscle-level) outputs. For the three testing gait cycles, a rather 309 significant level of nominal synergy FF control (at least 75%) was needed for combined synergy-310 based FF+FB control to succeed in predicting near-periodic walking motions that remained close to 311 experimental initial conditions. While lower levels of nominal FF control were unsuccessful at 312 generating such walking motions, they were able to predict near-periodic walking motions that 313 deviated substantially from the subject’s experimental motion, suggesting a potentially important role 314 of feedforward control for real-life walking. Our results indicate that while fully feedback-controlled 315 simple models may walk in predictive simulations (Geyer and Herr, 2010), researchers should be 316 cautious about applying such models to actual human subjects or when seeking to replicate actual 3D 317 experimental walking data. 318 This study faced multiple technical challenges that needed to be overcome before synergy-based 319 FF+FB control models could be constructed from actual experimental walking data. First, an 320 appropriate number of synergies to use for the neural control structure needed to be determined. We 321 use of 6 synergies per leg produced a slightly higher total VAF than the 5-synergy solution, a neural 322 feedback model developed using 6 synergies was not as successful as the one developed using 5 323 synergies. On closer inspection of the 6-synergy solution, we found that the synergy vector weights 324 were poorly distributed, with two synergy vectors representing highly similar coordination patterns. 325 This redundancy may be a poor match for a neural feedback model that needs to generalize to predict 326 motions not included in fitting data. Second, the optimization used to fit feedback gains required a 327 regularization term that penalized high individual feedback gains. While the solution for feedback 328 gains was theoretically unique, it was practically non-unique, resulting in high feedback gains when 329 the regularization term was omitted. Adding a small amount of regularization had little effect on 330 fitting accuracy while simultaneously allowing predictive simulations with combined FF+FB to 331 became successful. Third, development of successful neural feedback models required fitting data 332 from multiple gait cycles. As an initial test of our neural feedback models, we fitted a FB model to a 333 single calibration gait cycle using the same nominal FF synergy activations calculated from the five 334 calibration gait cycles. When we then performed a predictive simulation for that single calibration 335 gait cycle, we expected the simulation to work better than any of our predictions for testing cycles, 336 since the FB model produced a near-perfect fit to the synergy activations produced by the NCP tool. 337 However, this model did not converge to a perfect walking solution, generating worse predictions 338 than the 100% FF results for testing cycles. The FB model struggled to make the small changes in 339 control needed to achieve dynamic consistency, since the synergy activations used for FB model 340 fitting did not come from a dynamically consistent simulation but rather from the NCP tool. In contrast, to 392 simplify our neural FB models, we used joint-level quantities (i.e., joint angles, joint velocities, joint 393 moments) as surrogate inputs for muscle-level quantities in our sensory FB models, with our sensory 394 FB signals contributing to synergy controls. We chose this simplified approach for two reasons. First, 395 FB models at the muscle-level require calibration of a much larger number of FB gains. Since we 396 calibrated our FB models using data from only five walking cycles performed at a single speed, we 397 would have created an overfitting problem by modeling FB at the level of individual muscles. 398 Second, combined FF+FB control models have not been previously investigated using a 3D 399 personalized neuromusculoskeletal walking model that closely reproduced experimental walking data 400 collected from the subject being modeled. Thus, for a first investigation using real human subject 401 data, we chose to start with the simplest representation of neural FB – FB from joint-level quantities 402 to synergy-level controls. Now that the present study has demonstrated combined FF+FB control can 403 work for this simple FB case using experimental walking data and a personalized 3D 404 neuromusculoskeletal walking model, a future study should take the next step by exploring muscle-405 level FB models using a realistic walking model and real subject experimental walking data.Although 406 this study presents significant advances in modeling neural feedback for a real subject, this work has 407 several important limitations that future research should address. First, a subset of joint angles is not 408 controlled by the feedback model, most notably hip internal rotation and toes rotation. Thus, muscles 409 do not provide complete control of all DOFs in the legs. We attempted to control hip internal rotation 410 with our synergy-based FF+FB control models, but these simulations produced unrealistic joint 411 angles – an issue that was largely absent when this coordinate was removed. A previous Muscle-412 tendon Model Personalization effort using the same experimental dataset starting from the same 413 generic full body OpenSim model also had difficulty matching the hip internal-external rotation 414 moments, likely due to inaccurate moment arms or peak isometric strength values for hip internal-415 external rotation muscles(Meyer et al., 2016). Second, no muscle-specific feedback was modeled, 416 even though muscle-specific feedback is important for feedback mechanisms such as the stretch 417 reflex and Golgi tendon reflex (Geyer and Herr, 2010; Jansen et al., 2014; Trompetto et al., 2014; 418 Ang et al., 2018; Falisse et al., 2018; Haeufle et al., 2018). Accurately modeling muscle-specific 419 feedback becomes more complex in detailed 3D models as relationships between agonist-antagonist 420 muscle pairs are not always well-defined and can vary depending on the state of the model or the 421 coordinate of interest. Identifying how to include these interactions in a feedback model with detailed 422 3D geometry is significant and important work. Using muscle-specific feedback inputs and outputs 423 would also greatly increase the number of feedback gains that must be fitted, creating a more 424 complex optimization problem that could easily suffer from overfitting. Feedback from and to 425 individual muscles could give our model finer control and improve its predictive ability, which 426 would be worthwhile area for future study. Fifth, our muscle models are coupled to rigid tendons and 427 do not account for short-range stiffness. Rigid tendons are likely sufficient for walking at slow speeds 428 (Millard et al., 2013; De Groote et al., 2016), but short-range stiffness is important for even slight 429 movements (De Groote et al., 2017).
