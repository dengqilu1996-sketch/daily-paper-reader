Abstract 10 

1.  Video -tracking programs have now become an essential tool for researchers measuring animal 11 

behavior across biological fields. The panel of available programs is growing rapidly, providing 12 

researchers with numerous specific tools that will match their precise needs. However, their 13 

proliferation may complicate post -tracking data processing, and some programs do not even 14 

provide tools for correcting tracking errors or analysing tracking data. In the case of commercial 15 

software, the loss of access to a program due to budget limitations or researchers' mobility from 16 

one institution to another could prevent them from accessing and visualizing their tracking data. 17 

2.  There is therefore a growing need for an accessible and flexible tool to handle post -tracking 18 

processes such as the correction and analysis of tracking data obtained across different video -19 

tracking programs. 20 

3.  We present here the latest update of the video tracking and analysis program AnimalTA. With this 21 

new release, we propose to solve the above -mentioned problems by providing the scientific 22 

community with a program that will allow for data importation from other video -tracking programs. 23 

Like in its previous versions, AnimalTA remains a free, open -source, and highly user -friendly 24 

program, ensuring that it will always be accessible without restriction. Now, with this new 25 

importation option, users who performed their tracking with other programs can benefit from 26 

AnimalTA’s complete toolset of data visualization, correction, and analysis. 27 

4.  Finally, this article gives an overview of the other main improvements associated with this new 28 

release. The program is now faster in both video importation and  tracking, proposes  an amplified 29 

toolset for data  visualization  and correction, and features new options for data analysis. 30

# Introduction 31 

The quantitative study of animal behaviour is becoming increasingly reliant on automated tracking 32 

technologies that can extract high -resolution movement data from large, complex datasets (Dell et al., 33 

2014). Over the past decade, video -based tracking has become a key methodology in behavioural ecology, 34 

ethology, and applied animal science. It enables researchers to capture behavioural phenotypes with a level 35 

of precision and efficiency that manual observation cannot match. This methodological shift has been 36 

accompanied by a growing demand for transparency, reproducibility, and long -term data accessibility  —37 

values that are now central to open science frameworks in the biological sciences (Bell et al., 2009; Wilson 38 

et al., 2023). In this context, software tools used for collecting, processing, and analysing behavioural data 39 

are foundational components of scientific infrastructure, not merely technical conveniences. 40 

In recent years, the landscape of animal tracking software has diversified considerably, reflecting the range 41 

of research questions and experimental systems investigated by behavioural scientists. General -purpose 42 

tracking tools, such as AnimalTA, ToxTrack and idTracker, offer robust solutions for tracking multiple 43 

individuals under controlled conditions (Chiara & Kim, 2023; Rodriguez et al., 2018; Pérez -Escudero et 44 

al., 2014; Torrents et al., 2026). Meanwhile, machine -learning -based pose estimation platforms, such as 45 

DeepLabCut (Nath et al., 2019) and SLEAP (Pereira et al., 2022), have transformed our ability to track 46 

fine -scale movements of body parts across a wide range of species and contexts. More specialised tools, 47 

such as Loopy ( http://loopb.io , Loopbio Gmbh, Vienna, Austria), address specific experimental designs or 48 

taxa. Despite this diversity, a persistent challenge remains: the output formats, data structures, and 49 

downstream analytical workflows associated with these tools are rarely interoperable, limiting the adaptive 50 

and flexible use of tracking, correction, and analysis functions of different programs within a given 51 

research. Although some programs provide integrated trajectory correction modules, to our knowledge, no 52 

tools currently allow manual correction of trajectories independently of their original tracking pipelines, 53 

making it difficult to obtain fully verified, functional datasets.  Researchers working across platforms or 54 

seeking to correct and reanalyse existing datasets often encounter significant barriers to data integration 55 

and reuse. 56 

Open -source, freely available software plays a critical role in overcoming these barriers, particularly for 57 

researchers working outside well -resourced institutional environments. A recurring practical challenge in 58 

this area concerns early -career researchers and students who conduct data collection within a host 59 

laboratory, but then lose access to proprietary or institutionally licensed tracking software when they change 60 

position or affiliation. This means that previously obtained raw tracking data cannot be curated, corrected ,61 

or reanalysed without incurring the cost of accessing the original software environment, posing a significant 62

ensuring that analytical capacity is not contingent on institutional affiliation or grant availability. 65 

AnimalTA was developed in response to these needs, providing a free, user -friendly, open -source platform 66 

for tracking and analysing the movement and behaviour of individual or grouped animals (Chiara & Kim, 67 

2023). AnimalTA can track the movement of one or multiple individuals across one or multiple arenas, 68 

visualize and correct the resulting trajectories, and analyze these trajectories to extract parameters such as 69 

total distance traveled, average speed, path meander, explored area, time spent in a selected area, and 70 

average inter -individual distance over time and across time sequences. Since its initial publication, the 71 

software has been widely adopted within the animal science community, with over 7,140 downloads and 72 

56 citations reflecting a genuine demand for accessible, well -performing analytical tools. The original 73 

version of AnimalTA was primarily designed to work with data generated by its own tracking engine. 74 

However, user feedback and evolving community practices have highlighted the need to extend its 75 

compatibility to data produced by other widely used tracking platforms and enhance the interactive data 76 

curation and error correction tools available. 77 

Here, we present a substantial update to AnimalTA that directly addresses these needs. The updated 78 

software can now import and process tracking outputs from both general -purpose and machine -learning -79 

based tracking tools, such as ToxTrack, DeepLabCut, SLEAP, and Loopy. It has also been designed to be 80 

extensible, so that it can accommodate additional formats as the needs of the community evolve. New 81 

interactive functionalities have been implemented for tracking data correction and curation, providing real -82 

time visual feedback that facilitates the rapid identification and repair of tracking errors. These features are 83 

particularly valuable in teaching and training contexts. These developments position AnimalTA as a 84 

powerful, shared analytical environment that bridges tracking platforms, reduces pipeline fragmentation, 85 

and supports the reproducibility and accessibility of behavioural research, regardless of the upstream tools 86 

used. 87

## 1.  General presentation 90 

AnimalTA is an open -source, Python -developed video -tracking program released for the first time in 2023 91 

(Chiara & Kim, 2023). It uses various libraries, including tkinter for the Graphical User Interface (GUI), 92 

OpenCV for image processing, ffmpeg, and decord for video conversion and reading. AnimalTA uses a 93

## 2.  Loading trajectory data from other video -tracking programs 100 

With this update, AnimalTA now supports researchers who obtain tracking data using tools other than 101 

AnimalTA but seek a faster and more efficient way to visualize, correct, and analyze their data. It is also 102 

useful for those who no longer have access to the initial tools, which were accessible thanks to an 103 

institutional licence. The updated program includes a new “Import Data” panel, which allows users to 104 

combine previously imported videos with individual and group trajectory data from external sources. 105 

Prior to importing trajectory data, users must first upload their videos into the program using the “Add 106 

Video” option. AnimalTA then automatically converts the videos, allowing any format to be used without 107 

requiring external conversion tools. This process is now handled by the ffmpeg package (replacing OpenCV 108 

used in earlier versions), providing faster and more robust conversion with reduced sensitivity to corrupted 109 

frames. In addition, a multiprocessing system enables multiple videos to be converted simultaneously, 110 

significantly reducing overall processing time. 111 

Once the videos are imported into AnimalTA, users can define a scale for each video and set “arenas” (i.e., 112 

regions of interest) within which animals are expected to appear and move. These parameters will later be 113 

used by the program during data analysis. Finally, trajectory data from external sources can be imported 114 

via the “Import Data” panel. 115 

To facilitate data import, the current version of AnimalTA already supports data formats from several 116 

widely used video -tracking programs, including SLEAP (Pereira et al., 2022), DeepLabCut (Nath et al., 117 

2019), and ToxTrac (Rodriguez et al., 2018). For coordinate data from these programs, users simply need 118 

to specify the file location of the tracking data to be automatically imported into the program. Data from 119 

other sources can also be imported, as long as they are stored as a single “.csv” file. In such cases, users 120 

must describe the structure of the coordinate data file through an adapted and user -friendly interface (see 121 

Figure 1). 122

## 3.  Improved tracking performances 133 

In addition, AnimalTA tracking performances have been improved with the modernization of the tracking 134 

module, which now relies on a multiprocessing system (multiprocessing package, see Figure 2A for details). 135 

This update significantly enhances tracking speed, particularly for high -resolution videos or 136 

computationally intensive image processing tasks (Figure 2B –F). 137

Figure 2. A)  Workflow of the tracking process in AnimalTA. The tracking pipeline is divided into two main stages: 139 

image processing and contour processing. The image processing stage is the most computationally intensive part of 140 

the program. It includes extracting frames from the video, applying preprocessing steps (e.g., adjustments), and 141 

performing segmentation. Because each frame (or chunk of frames) can be processed independently, this stage can be 142 

executed in parallel and in any order. The output of this step is a set of contours (OpenCV objects), which represent 143 

the external boundaries of detected blobs (i.e., potential targets). The contour processing stage is responsible for 144 

assigning identities to these detected blobs and tracking them over time. Although this step is less computationally 145 

demanding, it must be performed sequentially and in the correct frame order, since identifying a target at frame i 146 

depends on its position at frame i –1. To optimize performance, the program uses multiprocessing. Multiple processes 147 

are dedicated to image processing, each handling different chunks of frames and pushing their results into a shared 148 

contours queue, where the frames’ data are not ordered. A separate process is dedicated to contour processing. This 149 

contour -processing process continuously monitors the queue. To reorder the frames, when specific frame data 150 

becomes available in the queue, they are retrieved and checked to determine whether they belong to the next missing 151 

frame data. If it matches, the data are processed immediately; otherwise, they are temporarily stored in an ordered 152 

buffer until the missing intermediate data arrives. Once the expected data are available, the process resumes sequential 153 

execution, emptying the buffer as much as possible before returning to the queue. This design allows the program to 154 

maximize parallel efficiency during heavy computations while preserving strict temporal order, which is required for 155 

accurate tracking.  B)  Video used to compare the efficiency of old and new versions of AnimalTA, which shows 6 156 

female cockroaches ( Blatta lateralis ).  C to F)  Time required by the old version (light pink) and new version (purple) 157 

of AnimalTA to track the video (shown in panel B) using a computer with the following specificities: 4 cores, 8 logical 158

## 4.  Visualization and correction of trajectories 168 

While the video tracking programs are becoming increasingly powerful and efficient, they are not yet 169 

perfect, and tracking inaccuracies remain common (Figure 3). In the new version of AnimalTA, special 170 

care has been taken to make data correction as fast and intuitive as possible. 171

AnimalTA allows for a rapid and efficient visualization of tracking data by superimposing tracked 178 

coordinates onto the original video (see Figure 3). Users can play the video at variable speeds, navigate 179 

directly to specific frames or timestamps, and zoom in or out as needed. The new version also introduces a 180 

“Lock View” option, which allows users to zoom in while the display automatically follows the target’s 181

region of the video. It also introduces new options for easier navigation within the coordinate data frame. 191 

These new features enable researchers to flexibly adapt their use of AnimalTA to specific experimental 192 

designs and the objectives of behavioural tests. 193

## 5.  Extension of the analysis toolset 195 

With this update, the program introduces new analysis methods, enhancing AnimalTA’s versatility in data 196 

processing and result visualization. For example, it now allows the creation of combined heatmaps by 197 

superimposing trajectories from different arenas, as well as heatmaps based on segmented images extracted 198 

from the video. These features produce clear, informative, and visually appealing outputs (Figure 4). 199

# Conclusion 222 

Since its first launch in 2023, AnimalTA has rapidly gained an increasing number of users, most of whom 223 

are animal scientists who frequently and repeatedly use this free tool to track and analyse animal movement 224 

and behaviour. Thanks to their enthusiastic support and feedback, we have been able to make an important 225 

update to AnimalTA, which greatly enhances the tool's utility for many more users. Many existing general -226 

purpose tracking tools and machine -learning -based tools have their own strengths in tracking ability, 227 

enabling researchers to adaptively choose the most efficient tool for their research topic, species, and 228 

experimental design. Now, with the updated AnimalTA, which allows importing and processing tracking 229 

outputs from other tracking tools, their users can also benefit from the most distinctive post -tracking 230 

functions of AnimalTA, such as rapid correction of tracking errors, versatile data analysis, and effective 231 

result visualisation. 232
