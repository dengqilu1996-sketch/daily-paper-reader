# Abstract 7

Background: Personalized computational neuromusculoskeletal models have great 8

potential for optimizing the design of clinical treatments for movement impairments. While 9

many software tools address specific parts of the model personalization and treatment 10 

optimization processes, they typically require significant programming experience to use 11 

and do not cover the full breadth of these two processes. Furthermore, published 12 

neuromusculoskeletal modeling studies typically do not provide all the methodological 13 

details needed for others to reproduce the work. Consequently, researchers seeking to 14 

develop skills in the model personalization and treatment optimization processes face a 15 

steep learning curve due to the lack of detailed training materials that demonstrate both 16 

processes for real-life clinical problems using real-life subject movement data. 17 

Methods: This article presents detailed training tutorials for the model personalization 18 

and treatment optimization processes using two real-life clinical problems and the 19 

Neuromusculoskeletal Modeling (NMSM) Pipeline. The first clinical problem involves the 20 

design of personalized gait modifications and high tibial osteotomy surgery for an 21

# Introduction 47 

Neuromusculoskeletal (NMS) modeling has great potential for informing the clinical 48 

treatment design process. For NMS models to be used effectively to design treatments 49 

for movement impairments, they need to be personalized to each individual, where the 50 

aspects of an individual’s model that require personalization will depend on the clinical 51 

problem being addressed (1). For some clinical problems, personalized skeletal geometry 52 

developed from the individual’s imaging data may be necessary (2–11). Several software 53 

tools exist that can personalize skeletal geometry either with the use of imaging data (e.g., 54 

NMS Builder (12), The MAP Client (13–15), OpenSim Creator (16), Bone Deformation 55 

Tool (17), STAPLE (18), the workflow presented in (19)) and without the use of imaging 56 

data (e.g., The MAP Client (13), Torsion Tool (20)). For other clinical problems, 57 

personalized joint structure, muscle-tendon, neural control, and foot-ground contact 58 

model components calibrated using the individual’s movement, force, and 59 

electromyographic (EMG) data may be necessary (3,21–28). Several tools exist that can 60 

personalize some, but not all, of these model components (e.g., AddBiomechanics (29), 61 

CEINMS (30), CEINMS-RT (31), SimCP (32)). Consequently, creation of a personalized 62 

neuromusculoskeletal model possessing these personalized model components is either 63 

not possible or challenging due to the need to use a combination of tools. 64

Once individual-specific NMS models are available, they can be used to predict 65 

computationally how the individual will function following a planned clinical intervention 66 

(3,21,23,33). This computational process, termed “predictive simulation,” is itself 67 

challenging, historically requiring specific skills in computational simulation and numerical 68 

methods to perform. To date, most predictive simulations of gait using NMS models have 69 

been performed using in-house software (21,34–42), but such tools are difficult for other 70 

researchers to use for their own research purposes. Consequently, several research 71 

groups have recently developed software tools that make the predictive simulation 72 

process easier to learn, facilitating replication of research performed by others. OpenSim 73 

Moco (43–45) has made predictive simulation much more accessible within the OpenSim 74 

simulation environment, while other software tools facilitate generation of predictive 75 

simulations either partially or completely outside of the OpenSim environment (e.g., 76 

BioMAC-Sim-Toolbox (46), SCONE (47), OpenSim-MATLAB Optimal Control (48), 77 

MyoSuite (49), GaitDynamics (50)). Each of these tools can be used to generate complex 78 

simulations that predict the functional outcome of a planned treatment, but none of them 79 

were designed to interface directly with model personalization software. For this reason, 80 

nearly all published predictive simulations of human movement have utilized scaled 81 

generic models that predict generic movements rather than personalized models that 82 

predict individual subject movements. 83 

For model personalization and predictive simulation alike, training new researchers to 84 

use these valuable software tools poses a significant challenge. If a new researcher wants 85 

to generate predictive simulations of an individual’s post-treatment movement function 86 

using a personalized NMS model of the individual (henceforth called “treatment 87

To learn how to apply these tools to real-life clinical problems, researchers need to 97 

study published research articles. Unfortunately, reproducing published research is often 98 

challenging. On the one hand, there has been significant community effort to share 99 

models and data through web-based repositories such as SimTK (57) or 100 

AddBiomechanics (58). On the other hand, published articles do not include all 101 

implementation details needed for readers to go from initial models and data to final 102 

models and results, thereby preventing other researchers from reproducing and 103 

expanding upon the work. This state of affairs is understandable given the challenges of 104 

recording all steps and implementation details so that other researchers can reproduce 105 

them. This situation has resulted in a “reproducibility crisis” that significantly hinders 106 

progress of the field (59). If new researchers want to learn how to use existing software 107 

tools to create personalized predictive simulations that can be used for clinical treatment 108 

design, they must be resourceful, tenacious, and technically adept to assemble the 109 

necessary knowledge. 110

A major reason for this lack of “real-life” training materials for model personalization 111 

and treatment optimization is that existing software tools do not cover the entire process, 112 

so at best, tool developers can create tutorials that cover only isolated areas. To create 113 

real-life training materials that teach users the entire model personalization and treatment 114 

optimization process, we need a toolset that covers the full spectrum of these two 115 

processes. This problem has been addressed by the recently published 116 

Neuromusculoskeletal Modeling (NMSM) Pipeline (60). The NMSM Pipeline is a117 

MATLAB-based open-source software package with integrated Model Personalization 118 

and Treatment Optimization toolsets for OpenSim (44,45). All tools within each toolset 119 

were designed to be used sequentially, with Model Personalization tools naturally 120 

interfacing with Treatment Optimization tools with minimal effort from the user. The inputs 121 

to the NMSM Pipeline are a scaled generic OpenSim model with or without personalized 122 

skeletal geometry (as appropriate) and individual subject movement data. Users can then 123 

personalize relevant joint structure, muscle-tendon, neural control, and foot-ground 124 

contact (as appropriate) model components for an individual subject and then use the 125 

subject’s personalized model in the treatment optimization process. Users can perform 126 

the entire model personalization and treatment optimization process without writing a 127 

single line of code. Instead, they create all necessary tasks using Extensible Markup 128 

Language (XML) settings files, created either in an OpenSim graphical user interface or 129 

by manual editing of XML files, which significantly lowers the barrier to entry for these 130 

high-end NMS modeling and simulation capabilities. This low barrier to entry can 131 

significantly increase the number of potential users who can explore computational 132 

treatment design using personalized NMS models. 133

Although the NMSM Pipeline makes treatment design with predictive simulations 134 

easier than it was previously, the tools still require significant training to learn. Basic 135 

tutorials for the NMSM Pipeline have already been made available (61), but like other 136 

musculoskeletal modeling software packages, these tutorials do not capture the nuances 137 

and details that should be understood to use the tools effectively for addressing real-life 138 

clinical problems. There is still a need for detailed tutorials that work through the entire 139 

model personalization to treatment optimization process for real-life clinical problems. 140 

Because the NMSM Pipeline makes model personalization and treatment optimization 141 

accessible and connected by a single workflow, it is now possible to design such in-depth 142 

tutorials. These tutorials can focus not only on what users are doing and why they are 143 

doing it, but also on how the final results are obtained. 144 

This paper presents in-depth training tutorials for the NMSM Pipeline that guide users 145 

through real-life research to design treatments for two clinical conditions that impair 146 

walking function. These tutorials are the first to take users through the entire 147 

computational treatment design process, starting with model personalization and ending 148 

with treatment optimization using the personalized model. The first tutorial explores 149 

personalized joint and foot-ground contact modeling for a single subject with bilateral 150 

medial knee osteoarthritis (OA). The tutorial guides users through the model 151 

personalization and treatment optimization processes without muscles or neural control 152 

to design either a walking modification or high tibial osteotomy (HTO) surgery to reduce 153 

the peak adduction moment in both knees. The second tutorial explores personalized 154 

muscle-tendon and neural control modeling for a single subject with stroke. The tutorial 155 

guides users through the model personalization and treatment optimization processes 156

a. Clinical Problems 194 

The first tutorial involves designing a walking modification or HTO surgical plan to treat 195 

an individual with bilateral medial knee OA. The peak knee adduction moment (KAM) 196 

during stance phase has been proposed as a surrogate measure for medial knee contact 197 

force, with a value less than 2.5% BW x HT corresponding to the best long-term outcome 198 

for individuals with medial knee OA (64–66). For this subject, the target value for the peak 199 

KAM is 30.3 Nm. The peak KAM is derived entirely from external forces and skeletal 200

that do not calculate internal knee contact forces. 202 

Two potential treatments for medial knee OA that can reduce the peak KAM are high 203 

tibial osteotomy (HTO) surgery and medial thrust gait (MTG) modifications (64,66). For 204 

the first treatment, an optimal HTO surgery is designed. This surgery involves cutting the 205 

subject’s proximal tibia and angling the distal end outward to turn a varus (bow-legged) 206 

knee into a valgus (knock-kneed) knee. The surgery is approximated in the OpenSim 207 

model by changing the knee adduction angle without altering the knee functional axis. 208 

The effect of the surgery on the resulting knee adduction moment during walking is 209 

estimated by performing a predictive simulation with the altered OpenSim model. The 210 

cost function minimizes changes relative to a baseline in the lower body joint moments 211 

controlling the motion. For the second treatment, optimal MTG modifications similar to 212 

(21) are designed. These gait modifications involve alterations to pelvic obliquity and 213 

lower body joint angles such that the knee moves closer to the midline of the body. No 214 

changes to the subject’s OpenSim model are required. The effect of the gait modifications 215 

on the resulting knee adduction moment during walking is estimated by performing a 216 

predictive simulation with the original OpenSim model. The cost function minimizes the 217 

knee adduction moment and changes relative to a baseline in the lower body joint 218 

moments controlling the motion, while path constraints maintain the same foot motion 219 

with respect to ground. For both potential treatments, all unlocked coordinates in the 220 

OpenSim model are treated as states that are changeable by the predictive simulations, 221 

and the treatments are designed for both knees since the subject has bilateral knee OA. 222

The second tutorial involves designing a synergy-based FES prescription to treat a 223 

person with right-sided hemiparesis caused by a stroke. The subject is high functioning 224 

but has an asymmetry in his propulsive and braking impulses between his legs, which 225 

leads to an inefficient gait pattern. Synergy-based FES is a novel treatment for stroke that 226 

electrically stimulates muscles based on the subject’s neural control characteristics (67– 227 

69). Muscle synergies can be calculated that decompose a subject’s muscle EMG data 228 

into a lower dimensional set of time-varying synergy activations (representing activation 229 

timing) and time-invariant synergy vector weights (representing activation coordination) 230 

(70–73). By comparing a subject’s synergy activations and vectors with those obtained 231 

from healthy subjects, researchers have identified a subject’s “impaired” synergies and 232 

then stimulated the subject’s paretic leg muscles with patterns consistent with 233 

corresponding healthy subject synergies (67,68). 234 

For the subject in this tutorial, the calculated muscle synergies shared synergy vectors 235 

between the two legs while also making the subject’s personalized model walk the same 236 

way the subject did. Calculating bilateral muscle synergies in this unique way allowed 237 

direct comparison between the subject’s right and left leg synergy activations. This 238 

comparison revealed asymmetries in the subject’s synergy activation amplitudes but not 239 

shapes, allowing corresponding paretic/non-paretic pairs of synergy activations to be 240 

placed into one of three categories based on relative amplitudes: healthy (paretic ≈ non-241 

paretic), impaired-compensatory (paretic/impaired < non-paretic/compensatory), and 242 

compensatory (paretic > non-paretic). We hypothesize that correcting the asymmetry in 243 

synergy activations will also correct the asymmetry in propulsive and braking impulses. 244

b. Tool Descriptions 249 

The primary software tools used in the tutorials are the NMSM Pipeline’s Model 250 

Personalization and Treatment Optimization toolsets (60). The NMSM Pipeline is 251 

MATLAB-based software that uses OpenSim models through OpenSim’s MATLAB 252 

application programming interface (API). The tutorials use software that is either open-253 

source, widely available in an academic setting, or proprietary but inexpensive. MATLAB’s 254 

optimization toolbox, parallel computing toolbox, statistics and machine learning toolbox, 255 

curve fitting toolbox, symbolic math toolbox, and signal processing toolbox are required 256 

to use the NMSM Pipeline. The NMSM Pipeline’s Treatment Optimization toolset requires 257 

the proprietary GPOPS-II direct collocation optimal control solver (74). GPOPS-II 258 

currently costs $165 per year for a single user academic license and $825 per year for a 259 

department-wide academic license. The tutorials and results presented in this manuscript 260 

were obtained using the NMSM Pipeline Version 1.4.0 and OpenSim Version 4.5. 261 

The NMSM Pipeline’s Model Personalization toolset consists of four tools: Joint Model 262 

Personalization, Ground Contact Model Personalization, Muscle-tendon Model 263 

Personalization, and Neural Control Model Personalization. All Model Personalization 264 

tools personalize the model by adjusting relevant parameters to track experimental data. 265 

Joint Model Personalization finds subject-specific joint parameter values (joint 266 

positions/orientations in body segments, body scale factors, marker locations on body 267 

segments) that allow the personalized model to reproduce experimental marker motions 268

Control Model Personalization fits “functional” synergies that are able to reproduce the 286 

desired joint moments, which cannot be done by matching only muscle activations 287 

(33,88,89). 288

# Discussion 687 

This paper presented a pair of neuromusculoskeletal modeling tutorials using the NMSM 688 

Pipeline and OpenSim that provide extensive details on how to approach the 689 

computational treatment design process. Extra attention is given to “small” details that 690 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 30, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.02.709099 doi: bioRxiv preprint

significantly influence optimization convergence and results quality, yet these details are 691 

rarely presented in journal publications. Currently, most researchers can learn how to use 692 

tools such as OpenSim through a set of tutorials that explore the main tool functionality 693 

but lack explanation of the finer details that are critical for research tutorials. While 694 

extensive details are useful, including them makes tutorial development much harder and 695 

time consuming. Development of the in-depth tutorials presented here took three 696 

individuals a year and a half, which is not realistic for every software tutorial. These 697 

tutorials are based on real research first, and then the instructions were written after 698 

results were obtained. This process required working through the simulation pipeline 699 

numerous times to verify that the instructions work as intended and to test different 700 

scenarios that could impact optimization convergence. The result is a pair of 701 

comprehensive tutorial instructions for how to approach computational treatment design 702 

and how to use the NMSM Pipeline for this purpose. 703 

The knee OA tutorial walks users through successfully reproducing the validated 704 

results in (21) or predicting the functional outcome of an HTO surgery. Reproducing the 705 

results of (21) required a specific problem formulation in which foot position and 706 

orientation were constrained. The original paper did not use a ground contact model but 707 

rather prescribed the motion of the feet with respect to ground and applied slightly variable 708 

ground reactions to them. The HTO solution is not validated, but the optimal correction of 709 

6 degrees falls within the typical range of correction for HTO surgery (90). 710 

The two knee OA treatment predictions have limitations that may affect their prediction 711 

accuracy. First, the HTO surgery was simulated by simply changing the knee adduction 712 

angle. However, the actual surgery adjusts the angle of the tibia below the knee joint. This 713

limitation could be addressed by adding a specific HTO joint to the tibia that anatomically 714 

represents the surgery. Next, the surgery simulation did not use muscles, which could 715 

result in the predictive simulation producing a motion that the subject’s neural control 716 

cannot achieve. While the predicted medial thrust gait motion has been experimentally 717 

validated for a single subject (21), there is no guarantee that other subjects will be able 718 

to achieve it. For both treatment problems, it is possible that including personalized 719 

muscle-tendon models and a personalized neural control model could yield more 720 

accurate predictions. Nevertheless, both the HTO and MTG simulations as performed in 721 

the tutorials produced physiologically realistic results. 722 

The stroke tutorial successfully walks users through designing a synergy-based FES 723 

intervention to correct gait propulsive force asymmetry. The treatment design in the 724 

tutorial relies on two key assumptions that are not common in the literature. First, we 725 

assumed that synergy vectors are nearly identical between the two legs. This assumption 726 

relies on the hypothesis that synergy activations are sourced from the brain while synergy 727 

vectors reside in the spinal cord (91,92). Previous studies have reported that when 728 

muscle synergies are calculated from EMG data for walking, synergy vectors are 729 

“remarkably similar” between paretic legs of stroke survivors, non-paretic legs of stroke 730 

survivors, and legs of healthy subjects when analyzed using the same number of 731 

synergies (93,94). As stated in Clark et al. (2010), “Indeed, when the data for all post-732 

stroke participants were analyzed using four modules (even when our VAF criteria could 733 

be met with fewer modules), we found that the module muscle weightings were 734 

remarkably similar between the healthy and post-stroke groups.” Thus, the primary 735 

differences between these three groups may occur in the corresponding time-varying 736

synergy activations. From this perspective, while “merged” synergies may be a more 737 

compact representation of some post-stroke walking EMG patterns (95), “merging” may 738 

actually be “mathematically masking” remaining potential within the post-stroke central 739 

nervous system. These observations suggest that it is at least reasonable to explore 740 

computational treatment design to improve walking post-stroke using similar synergy 741 

vectors between the two legs. 742 

Second, we assumed that synergy activation pairs (i.e., corresponding synergy 743 

activations in the two legs) can be classified as either impaired-compensatory, 744 

compensatory, or healthy based only on their relative amplitudes between the two legs 745 

(67,68). An impaired-compensatory synergy pair possesses a lower amplitude synergy 746 

activation on the paretic side and a higher amplitude synergy activation on the non-paretic 747 

side. The lower amplitude synergy activation on the paretic side is assumed to be 748 

impaired by the stroke and the higher amplitude synergy activation on the non-paretic 749 

side is assumed to be compensating for the corresponding impaired synergy activation. 750 

In contrast, a compensatory synergy activation pair possesses a higher amplitude 751 

synergy activation on the paretic side and a lower amplitude synergy activation on the 752 

non-paretic side. The higher amplitude synergy activation on the paretic side is assumed 753 

to be compensating for impaired synergy activations on the paretic side, while the lower 754 

amplitude synergy activation on the non-paretic side is assumed to be compensating as 755 

well. A healthy synergy activation pair possesses comparable amplitude synergy 756 

activations on the paretic and non-paretic side. These definitions emerge from the 757 

decision to use nearly identical synergy vectors for the two legs. Based on these 758 

definitions, when the subject’s impaired synergies (Right Leg 2, Right Leg 3, Right Leg 4) 759

designed with this computational method need to be evaluated experimentally. 771

# Conclusions 831 

The detailed tutorials presented in this study serve as an introduction to developing and 832 

using personalized predictive simulations to design subject-specific treatments for 833 

walking impairments using the NMSM Pipeline. The NMSM Pipeline has made predictive 834 

simulations and computational treatment design using personalized neuromusculo-835 

skeletal models more accessible than was previously possible, enabling these tutorials to 836 

be developed. The key learning outcome from these tutorials is a working knowledge of 837 

personalized neuromusculoskeletal modeling techniques within the NMSM Pipeline and 838 

OpenSim so that researchers can apply this knowledge to their own problems. These 839 

tutorials are accessible to beginning users of the NMSM Pipeline and do not require any 840 

coding knowledge, making them valuable for both clinicians and newcomers to the 841 

biomechanics field. We hope that by working through these tutorials, users will develop 842 

an enhanced understanding of the NMSM Pipeline and will be able to apply the NMSM 843 

Pipeline’s tools to their own research and clinical problems. The tutorial documents can 844 

be found in the supplementary materials of this publication (Additional File 1.pdf and 845 

Additional File 2.pdf) and on the NMSM Pipeline website https://nmsm.rice.edu. Tutorial 846 

materials on the website will be updated periodically to keep up with new functionality as 847 

new features for the NMSM Pipeline are released. 848
