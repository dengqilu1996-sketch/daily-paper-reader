Abstract 19 

When acquiring a motor skill, learners must practice the skill at a difficulty that is challenging 20 

but still manageable to gradually improve their performance. In other words, during training , the 21 

learner must experience success as well as failure. Does there exist an optimal proportion of 22 

successes and failures to promote the fastest improvements in skill? Here, we build on a recent 23 

theoretical framework for optimal machine learning, extending it to the learning of motor skills. 24 

We then designed a custom task whose difficulty dynamically changed along with human 25 

subjects’ performance, constraining the error rate during training. In a large behavioral dataset 26 

(N= 19 2), we observe d evidence that learning is greatest at around a ~30% error rate, matching 27 

our theoretical predictions. 28 

Key words: motor skill; motor learning; optimal learning; training curriculum 29

Introduction 32 

Acquiring a new motor skill involves reducing errors (Krakauer & Mazzoni, 2011; Ohlsson, 1996; 33 

Tseng et al., 2007) . Consider learning to shoot free throws in basketball: After a miss, the player 34 

uses the observed error information (e.g., “I missed to the right”) to fine -tune future attempts (e.g., 35 

re -orienting the shot slightly left). Such performance errors during motor skill learning drive 36 

improvements in both accuracy and precision and , given enough time and practice , can lead to 37 

automaticity and expertise (Haith et al., 2022; Shmuelof et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2022) .38 

39 

Importantly, one’s error rate during training can relate to if, and how well, they learn a skill. In the 40 

extreme, if one never experienced success, their motor system may have no template upon which 41 

to improve their skill (Sanger, 2004) . On the other hand, learning would be unnecessary if one 42 

always experienced success (e.g., the hoop moved to wherever the ball was thrown) . Thus, it is 43 

reasonable to assume that there is a “sweet spot” error rate that may generate the most efficient 44 

learning process (Guadagnoli & Lee, 2004) . Indeed, in the context of simple non -motor skills (e.g., 45 

perceptual decision -making) it has recently been proposed that an optimal proportion of success 46 

and failure – an optimal “error rate” – can maximize the learning rate of a perceptual skill, such as 47 

binary categorization of sensory input (Wilson et al., 2019) . Here , we extend this logic to the 48 

domain of sensorimotor skill learning. 49 

50 

In any task, errors become more likely as the difficulty of the task increases. Since the difficulty 51 

of the task can often be externally modulated, controlling an individual’s error rate during training 52 

requires dynamically controlling the difficulty of the task in response to their performance, 53 

exemplified by classic psychophysical methods like “staircasing” (Cornsweet, 1962) . But how 54

does task difficulty influence learning? Wilson and colleagues (2019) argue that learning using a 55 

gradient descent algorithm mathematically specifies an optimal error rate with very few additional 56 

assumptions. The authors considered the context of a binary discrimination task and, using their 57 

framework, predicted an optimal error rate of ~15%, a value that was validated by multiple 58 

simulations over a range of binary decision -making tasks. 59 

60 

Can a similar framework be applied to motor skill learning? The first relevant issue is the putative 61 

relationship between motor skill learning and gradient descent. It has been suggested that motor 62 

skill learning, at least in the form of gradual improvements in skill that reduce errors and improve 63 

precision (Diedrichsen & Kornysheva, 2015; Sternad, 2018) , does indeed operate via a gradient 64 

descent process (Greenstreet et al., 2025; Haith, 2025; Pierella et al., 2019) . In many motor skills, 65 

the learner begins with an initially noisy policy and gradually refines this policy, reducing the 66 

variability of their actions as their skill improves (Haith, 2025; Sternad, 2018) . For example, a 67 

novice darts player may make errors that span the whole width of the dartboard initially . But , as 68 

they improve, they gradually decrease their variability to hone in on a particular region of the 69 

board. 70 

71 

Fitts & Posner (1967) proposed a three -stage model of motor learning: the cognitive, associative, 72 

and autonomous stages. In the cognitive stage, learners must learn the goals of the task and the 73 

appropriate strategies to adopt to achieve those goals, heavily relying on explicit knowledge to 74 

achieve success in the task. In the associative stage, the learner begins to refine their actions, 75 

steadily reducing e xecution errors and variability. Finally, in the autonomous stage, the learner’s 76 

behavior has been practiced so thoroughly as to become automatic, requiring little cognitive effort. 77

To return to the darts analogy, an early novice may begin in the cognitive stage by trying to figure 78 

out the appropriate movements to launch a dart in the general direction of the dartboard. This 79 

process involves figuring out the appropriate strategy to launch the dart and aim at a particular spot 80 

on the board. After learning these strategies, the learner may eliminate any initial biases, but he 81 

will still have highly variable motor output around the region of the board he is aiming at. The 82 

subsequent associative stage marks a period of refinement, or precision improvement, where he 83 

steadily reduces that variability to hone in on the desired region of the board. It is on this stage of 84 

learning that this paper focuses .85 

86 

Here , we hypothesized that the framework proposed by Wilson and colleagues (2019) might apply 87 

to motor skill learning of the form described above , even though motor errors are both signed and 88 

continuous (rather than binary and unsigned , as in perceptual discrimination tasks ). First, we 89 

analytically extended their model to demonstrate that a roughly 32 % error rate may be the optimal 90 

error rate for motor skill learning, given some basic assumptions. After specifying this prediction, 91 

we developed a customized “Pong” task that required precise online visuomotor control and 92 

collected behavioral data on the task from a large sample of human participants (N = 194). Using 93 

a custom dynamic error -control algorithm, we modified the size of subjects’ Pong paddle on a 94 

trial -by -trial basis to impose different error rates on their performance and measured their 95 

improvements in the task. 96 

97 

Strikingly, we found reliable evidence that individuals with an error rate around ~32% showed the 98 

most learning in the task, directly in line with our model simulations. The current work provide s a99 

theoretically grounded specification of a putative optimal error rate for motor learning, and may 100

Task Design and Protocol 190 

Participants were recruited on Prolific to play a modified version of the game Pong, written using 191 

PhaserJS 3 (phaser.io). Participants controlled a 15 -pixel wide rectangular paddle, initially set to 192 

200 pixels (px) in length and fixed 10 px from the left edge of the workspace, inside a 1000 px by 193 

800 px arena enclosed within a white border. Movements of subjects’ mouse or trackpad in the 194 

coronal plane directly translated to displacements of the paddle’s vertical position (i.e., movements 195 

away from the body = up; toward = down). Participants were instructed to move the paddle to 196 

intercept a small circular puck (14 px diameter) which would move toward their paddle at an angle, 197 

bouncing off the walls of the arena. They were specifically instructed to try to cause the puck to 198 

hit the middle of the paddle, as this is the strategy most likely to result in success. This task required 199 

participants to integrate visual sensory information regarding the position of the puck into their 200 

movements of the paddle in order to successfully intercept the puck. 201 

202 

Each trial consisted of the puck crossing from 25 pixels left of the right side of the arena to the left 203 

side of the arena where the participant’s paddle was located, crossing a horizontal distance of 975 204 

px. At the start of each trial, the paddle’s vertical position was always centered, and the puck began 205

Calibration and Error -control Algorithm 217 

The experiment began with a 20 -trial calibration phase, in which the horizontal speed of the puck 218 

was calibrated to be comfortable for each participant. The vertical speed of the puck was 219 

determined by the launch angle on a given trial. Since the duration of each trial was dependent on 220 

the horizontal speed of the puck, we needed to calibrate this speed to ensure every participant had 221 

ample time to react to the puck and adjust the paddle. The speed was calibrated using a staircase 222 

procedure: if the participant failed to intercept the puck, the horizontal speed was reduced by 75 223 

px/s; if the participant successfully intercepted the puck, the speed increased by 75 px/s. The speed 224 

was initialized at 800px/s. The minimum allowed speed was 100px/s, but this was never achieved 225 

by any participant. This procedure resulted in a 72% (SD: 12%) success rate during the calibration 226 

phase. 227

Since this calibration phase often ended with a speed that slightly exceeded the comfortable range 229

baseline began in which the puck speed and the paddle size were held constant. This allowed us to 231

was not different between groups . At this point, the final 70 -trial training phase began, and the 233 

paddle size was allowed to dynamically update according to a custom algorithm that monitored 234 

the recent distribution of errors to constrain participants’ error rates. Each participant was 235 

randomly assigned to one of four groups, each with their own intended error rate: 15%, 30%, 50%, 236 

and 65%. On every trial, the puck’s final vertical position relative to the final vertical position of 237 

the center of the paddle was added to a sliding 20 -trial buffer, regardless of whether the trial was 238 

successful or not. After excluding outliers (defined as 3 times the median absolute deviation from 239 

the median; this is identical to the MATLAB rmoutliers function), we computed the root -mean -240 

squared -error (RMSE) of this 20 -trial history. From this we inferred a zero -mean Gaussian 241 

distribution with the RMSE as the standard deviation and, using the CDF of the Gaussian 242 

distribution, we computed a paddle size that would result in the intended error rate. This outlier 243 

criterion was chosen to only detect extreme outliers that could vastly impact the RMSE calculation 244 

for the duration of the 20 -trial buffer, as determined by pilot experiments. 245

A final assumption was that learning proceeded via gradient descent; in other words, learning 299 

proceeded such that participants made actions on subsequent trials to reduce their errors by a 300 

proportion of the error observed on preceding trials (as is the case in classical state -space models 301 

in motor learning ; state -space models are a variant of gradient descent ). State -space learning 302 

models, and gradient descent learning rules in general, are well established in modeling motor 303 

learning in a variety of tasks ( Thoroughman & Shadmehr 2000; Taylor & Ivry 2011; McDougle 304 

et al. 2015; Krakauer et al. 2019; Pierella et al. 2019 ). Here, we employed a linear mixed -effects 305 

model to fit each subject’s error magnitude across trials using a linear model, a quadratic model, 306 

and an exponential model. The exponential model is predicted by gradient descent if participants 307 

reduce their errors on subsequent trials by a proportion of their errors on preceding trials. Model 308 

comparison by BIC showed that the exponential model best fit the data ( ∆BIC linear: -14.9, ∆BIC 309

Discussion 390 

Here , we developed a new theoretical framework on optimal motor skill learning and employed a 391 

novel Pong -based motor skill task to provide behavioral evidence supporting our theoretical 392 

prediction that the optimal error rate for learning this and similar (one -dimensional) motor skill s393 

should be ~ 32 %. By controlling human subjects’ error rates during our Pong task, we could 394 

influence how well they learned throughout the course of training. Our theoretical predictions, 395 

which were closely based on recent work on optimal learning for binary classification tasks 396 

(Wilson et al., 2019) , were supported both by showing the strongest learning in the group with the 397 

near -optimal error rate and by revealing a significant correlation between the model predictions 398 

and the empirical data across a range of individuals’ actual error rates (Figure 3). We propose that 399 

if an error rate of approximately ~ 32 % is maintained throughout training of a simple (one -400 

dimensional ) motor skill, one may expect to observe the greatest improvements in said motor skill. 401 

This discovery echoes work on “error -less” learning paradigms, where high success rates are 402 

maintained throughout training , and the difficulty of the task increases as the learner improves 403 

(Capio et al., 2013; Maxwell et al., 2001) .404

Why does the error rate during training influence learning? 406 

In the mathematical framework proposed by Wilson and colleagues (2019) and extended here, the 407 

relationship between training difficulty and learning is a necessary consequence of tuning the 408 

parameters responsible for generating responses that reduce errors via gradient descent. Such an 409 

approach leaves a key question unanswered: how does training difficulty result in changes in 410 

learning rate? 411 

412 

The “Challenge Point” framework (Guadagnoli & Lee, 2004) proposes that an agent’s 413 

performance at a motor skill is a source of information for future learning and action attempts. 414 

Highly expected outcomes, such as success when the task is easy, yield very little information as 415 

the agent can fully predict the consequences of their action. However, highly unexpected 416 

outcomes, such as success when the task is extremely difficult, are rare but can provide a high 417 

degree of information. The “optimal challenge point” occurs when the task difficulty is calibrated 418 

to ensure a moderately high probability of success and maximally informative feedback. The 419 

balance of maximizing both the expectation of success and the amount of interpretable information 420 

available for learning results in a fixed optimal error rate during training of some skills (Wilson et 421 

al., 2019) .422 

423 

Another psychological explanation focuses on the role of reward and motivation in error -based 424 

learning. Motivation to perform well in a task can drive increased sensitivity to errors (Bengtsson 425 

et al., 2009) , which in turn can prompt faster learning (Wu et al., 2006) . Additionally, rewarding 426 

feedback tends to increase motivation (Bissonette & Roesch, 2016) and can accelerate motor 427

learning (Nikooyan & Ahmed, 2015) . Thus, the recent history of successes and failures may prime 428 

an “optimal” degree of motivation, where errors occur in concert wit h rewarded trials, keeping the 429 

agent’s error sensitivity and motivation for success high. Put another way, if the task is too easy, 430 

learning would be minimal; but, if the task is too difficult, successes are too infrequent to generate 431 

the benefits to motivation and error sensitivity that are crucial for maintaining a high learning rate. 432 

The balance of these two opposing forces could also result in an optimal error rate for learning. 433 

434 

The mathematical framework employed here does not allow us to di stinguish among these 435 

explanations, nor other higher -level psychological theories not mentioned here. The formation of 436 

internal models for motor learning requires obtaining information about how observed sensory 437 

outcomes relate to internally generated action plans (Abdelghani et al., 2008; Pierella et al., 2019; 438 

Thoroughman & Shadmehr, 2000) in line with the Challenge Point hypothesis (Guadagnoli & Lee, 439 

2004) . But reward feedback has also been shown to enhance motor learning (Galea et al., 2015; 440 

Izawa & Shadmehr, 2011; Nikooyan & Ahmed, 2015) , supporting a motivational account as well. 441 

Thus , both explanations are plausible and may reflect how different brain regions (e.g., the 442 

cerebellum and basal ganglia, respectively) contribute to the fine -tuning of motor learning -related 443 

parameters (Greenstreet et al., 2025; Seidler et al., 2013) . In general, we position our current 444 

findings at a more fundamental, algorithmic level. 445

Extensions and limitations of the current framework 447 

In our experiment, we chose a simple motor task where motor errors are generally assumed to be 448 

normally distributed. However, in some tasks, such as interval timing (Swanton & Matell, 2011) ,449 

the distribution of errors may not be normal and may follow other known distributions. As noted 450

We also restricted our consideration to a simple one -dimensional motor skill task (errors were only 458 

in the vertical dimension). However, many motor tasks involve errors in multiple dimensions (e.g., 459 

a dart or archer’s arrow may miss the bullseye in both the vertical and horizontal dimensions ),460

considered is whether learning in each dimension is fully independent, or if learning in one 463 

dimension affects learning in the others; the answer to this question would allow us to better model 464 

the error reduction processes involved in learning complex motor skills when multiple dimensions 465

the general mathematical framework demonstrated here can , in theory , be applied to such multi -469

Our results demonstrate that the error rate during training affects the rate of improvement of a 489 

simple visuo motor skill. We think this insight might be broadly applicable across applied domains. 490 

Rehabilitation paradigms, for example, have already recognized the need to adjust the difficulty 491 

of practice to optimally challenge patients and maximize the therapeutic benefit of training 492 

sessions (Lee et al., 2023; Metzger et al., 2014) . In sports coaching, the principle of progressively 493 

increasing the difficulty of practice has been widely applied and shown to facilitate improvements 494 

in skill (Marraffino et al., 2021; Wickens et al., 2013; Zahran et al., 2020) . Optimistically, these 495

Summary 499 

We demonstrate that an optimal error rate for performance improvements in a motor skill may 500 

exist and suggest that this optimal error rate is around ~ 32 %. By testing a large cohort of human 501 

participants in a novel motor skill task (a modified version of Pong), we also provide empirical 502 

support for this prediction and demonstrate the potential importance of maintaining a particular 503 

error rate throughout training to maximize performance improvements. This approach may have 504 

broader implications in other domains concerned with optimizing the acquisition, refinement, and 505 

rehabilitation of motor skills. 506
