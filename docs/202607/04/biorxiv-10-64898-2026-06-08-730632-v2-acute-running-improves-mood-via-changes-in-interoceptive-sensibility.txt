Abstract 17 

Purpose : Interoception is the sensing and interpretation of internal bodily signals and may 18 

be a key process linking exercise-induced bodily changes to mood. This study examined 19 

whether acute moderate-intensity treadmill running changes interoception and mood states 20 

and whether running-related changes in interoception statistically account for mood 21 

improvement. 22 

Methods : Twenty-seven healthy young adults completed a within-subject crossover 23 

experiment consisting of 15 min of moderate-intensity treadmill running at 50% estimated 24 

V̇O ₂peak and seated rest on separate days. Before and after each condition, cardiac 25 

interoceptive accuracy was assessed using heartbeat counting and heartbeat tapping tasks, 26 

present-moment interoceptive sensibility using a state-adapted Body Perception 27 

Questionnaire (BPQ), and mood using the Profile of Mood States. Running-related changes 28 

were calculated as ΔRUN − ΔCTL. 29 

Results : Compared with seated rest, running reduced total mood disturbance (TMD) and 30 

tension–anxiety (TA) and increased vigor–activity (VA). Running did not significantly change 31

Introduction 45 

Acute aerobic exercise has been widely studied as a strategy for improving mood, 46 

particularly by reducing anxiety. Previous studies have reported that even a single bout of 47 

exercise can significantly reduce state anxiety relative to pre-exercise levels (1). Recent 48 

meta-analytic evidence has also shown that moderate- to high-intensity exercise is 49 

associated with reduced anxiety in healthy adults (1,2,3). However, not all individuals benefit 50 

from acute exercise to the same extent (3), suggesting that the anxiolytic effects of exercise 51 

may not be uniform across individuals. Therefore, to establish exercise more firmly as a 52 

strategy for promoting mental health and reducing anxiety-related mood disturbances, it is 53 

necessary to clarify the proximal mechanisms underlying exercise-induced reductions in 54 

anxiety, as well as the factors that contribute to individual differences in these effects. 55 

A growing body of physiological evidence has identified several neurological 56 

mechanisms through which exercise may improve mood states, including the regulation of 57 

monoaminergic systems and the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis, the release of 58 

endogenous opioids and endocannabinoids, and the expression of neurotrophic factors such 59 

as brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) (4,5,6,7). Psychological mechanisms, such as 60

these mechanisms explain only certain aspects of the complex responses induced by 62 

exercise, and it remains difficult to account for, within a single pathway, the proximal process 63 

by which exercise-induced changes in bodily states are perceived, evaluated, and ultimately 64 

linked to subjective changes in mood states (10). These considerations raise the possibility 65 

that individual differences in the mood-enhancing effects of exercise may depend not only 66 

on the magnitude of physiological changes induced by exercise, but also on how these bodily 67 

changes are perceived, interpreted, and integrated into subjective emotional experience. 68 

Accordingly, the present study focused on interoception. Interoception is defined as the 69 

process by which the nervous system senses, interprets, and integrates internal signals from 70 

bodily systems, such as the cardiovascular, respiratory, and gastrointestinal systems, to form 71 

representations of bodily states (11,12). From a neuroanatomical perspective, interoceptive 72 

processing is supported by a network centered on the insular cortex (insula) and anterior 73 

cingulate cortex (ACC), both of which are deeply involved in emotion generation and 74 

subjective emotional experience (11,13,14). In particular, the integration of bodily signals in 75 

the insula has been associated with subjective anxiety (14,15), suggesting that interoception 76 

may constitute a fundamental mechanism linking bodily states to emotional experience. 77 

Acute aerobic exercise produces salient changes in internal bodily states, including 78 

increases in heart rate (HR) and respiration. Such exercise-induced bodily signals are 79 

thought to engage interoceptive pathways involving afferent feedback from the body and 80 

brain regions such as the insula and ACC, thereby influencing cognitive, motivational, and 81 

emotional processes (16). Such changes may make bodily signals more noticeable and alter 82 

how individuals perceive and attend to their bodily states, thereby leading to changes in 83 

interoceptive processing (17). Importantly, anxiety has been linked to altered interoceptive 84 

processing, particularly heightened attention to bodily sensations, negative evaluation of 85 

these sensations, and difficulties describing bodily signals and emotions (18,19). Intentional 86 

activities such as exercise may provide a context in which bodily signals are generated in a 87 

relatively clear and predictable manner. By increasing the salience and interpretability of 88 

bodily signals, exercise may alter interoception and facilitate the integration of bodily changes 89 

into subjective emotional experience, thereby contributing to changes in mood states (12,20). 90

Indeed, previous studies have suggested that acute physical activity can improve 91 

performance on heartbeat perception tasks (e.g., Ref. 21), possibly by increasing the 92 

salience of cardiac signals (22,23). However, it remains unclear whether exercise-induced 93 

changes in interoception are associated with changes in mood states, and whether 94 

interoception can account for individual differences in mood improvement after acute 95 

exercise. In particular, few studies have tested the exercise–interoception–mood pathway 96 

using a mediation model. 97 

Based on this background, the present study examined the effects of acute moderate-98 

intensity aerobic exercise, specifically treadmill running, on mood states and interoception in 99 

healthy university students. Moderate-intensity exercise is commonly recommended for 100 

health promotion (24), and the present study operationalized moderate-intensity running as 101 

50% of estimated peak oxygen uptake (V̇O ₂peak ). Treadmill running was selected because 102 

recent evidence suggests that the effects of exercise on mood may differ depending on 103 

exercise modality. For example, several studies have reported that cycling exercise 104 

increases arousal, whereas its effects on pleasant mood are relatively limited (25,26,27). In 105 

contrast, running has been shown to increase both arousal and pleasant mood (28,29). 106 

Although the mechanisms underlying these modality-specific effects remain unclear, one 107 

possible explanation is that running-specific mechanical stimulation, such as head 108 

acceleration, may contribute to changes in mood and cognition (29,30). Therefore, the 109 

present study hypothesized that changes in interoception induced by moderate-intensity 110 

running would contribute to reductions in negative mood states and broader improvements 111 

in mood. We tested this hypothesis using a quasi-randomized crossover design and 112 

examined whether running-related changes in interoception were associated with, and 113 

statistically accounted for, changes in mood states. 114

Experimental design and procedure 139 

The study consisted of a baseline assessment day and two main experimental sessions. 140 

On Day 1, participants completed the single-stage submaximal treadmill walking test 141 

(SSTWT) to estimate maximal oxygen uptake and determine the individualized treadmill 142 

workload corresponding to 50% of estimated peak oxygen uptake (V̇O 2peak ) for the RUN 143 

condition. On the same day, participants also completed the Body Perception Questionnaire– 144 

Body Awareness Very Short Form, Japanese version (BPQ-BAVSF-J), BDI-II, and 145 

anthropometric measurements of height and weight. 146 

The main experiment consisted of two conditions: a moderate-intensity running condition 147 

(RUN) and a seated resting control condition (CTL). All participants completed both 148 

conditions on separate days after the baseline assessment session. The order of the RUN 149 

and CTL conditions was quasi-randomized across participants to reduce potential order 150 

effects. The two main experimental sessions were separated by at least 48 h. 151 

To control for external influences, participants were instructed to refrain from exercise 152 

and alcohol consumption for at least 24 h before each main experimental session and to 153 

avoid caffeine on the day of each experimental session. Interoceptive measures and mood 154 

states were assessed immediately before and after each condition using the same task order 155 

and procedure across conditions. The overall experimental procedure is shown in Figure 1. 156

Exercise and control conditions 194 

In the RUN condition, participants performed 15 min of treadmill running at an 195 

individualized workload corresponding to 50% of each participant’s estimated V̇O 2peak at 0% 196 

incline. This intensity was selected because 50% V̇O 2peak is classified as moderate intensity 197 

according to the American College of Sports Medicine (36). The exercise duration was set at 198 

15 min because previous studies have suggested that at least 10–20 min of running can 199 

improve mood (28,37,38). 200 

During the RUN condition, HR and RPE were monitored every minute to verify exercise 201 

intensity. In the CTL condition, participants remained seated quietly on a chair for 15 min. 202 

The duration of the CTL condition was matched to that of the RUN condition. 203 

Interoceptive measures and mood states were assessed immediately before and after 204

Heartbeat tapping task (HTT) 219 

Although the HCT is widely used to assess interoceptive accuracy, its performance can 220 

be influenced by non-interoceptive factors, such as prior knowledge or beliefs about one’s 221 

own heart rate and estimation strategies, rather than solely by the perception of actual 222 

heartbeat sensations (40,41,42). Therefore, in addition to the HCT, the present study 223 

included an HTT, which requires participants to tap in synchrony with their perceived 224 

heartbeats and allows the temporal correspondence between cardiac events and behavioral 225 

responses to be assessed. The design of this task was based on similar procedures reported 226 

in previous studies (17,43,44,45,46). 227 

A fixation point was continuously presented at the center of the screen throughout each 228 

trial. Participants were instructed to respond by clicking the left mouse button in synchrony 229 

with their own heartbeat as accurately as possible while the fixation point was displayed. 230 

Even when they were uncertain, they were asked to respond based on their best estimate. 231 

This instruction is consistent with standard procedures commonly used in heartbeat 232 

perception tasks (17,47). Before the experimental trial, one 10-s practice trial was 233 

administered to facilitate understanding of the task procedure. After the practice trial, 234 

participants performed one 60-s experimental trial. 235

Interoceptive sensibility was assessed using the Japanese version of the Body 237 

Perception Questionnaire (BPQ) –Body Awareness Very Short Form. The BPQ was originally 238

developed in English as a measure of bodily awareness (48,49). In the present study, we 239 

used the validated Japanese version of the BPQ-BAVSF (50), a concise 12-item scale 240 

designed to assess individual differences in sensitivity to internal bodily sensations and 241 

physiological functions. The items cover bodily experiences related to respiration, 242 

cardiovascular activity, muscle tension, gastrointestinal processes, and other internal bodily 243 

cues. Responses are rated on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from “Never” to “Always.” Total 244 

scores range from 12 to 60, with higher scores indicating greater sensitivity to bodily 245 

sensations. For clarity and brevity, scores derived from the BPQ-BAVSF-J are referred to as 246 

“BPQ scores” throughout this study. 247 

The standard BPQ-BAVSF-J is administered in a trait-based format, asking respondents 248 

to rate their awareness of bodily sensations during most situations. However, because the 249 

present study aimed to examine state-related changes in interoceptive sensibility before and 250 

after the experimental sessions, the wording of the instructions was modified with permission 251 

from the first author of the Japanese version (50). In the modified version used in this study, 252 

participants were asked to rate each item according to their present-moment bodily 253 

experience at the time of assessment. For example, an item such as “My mouth being dry” 254 

was rated with reference to the participant’s current bodily state. This adaptation was 255 

intended to allow the BPQ-BAVSF-J to capture short-term fluctuations in interoceptive 256 

sensibility associated with the running intervention. 257 

To examine whether this state-based adaptation retained continuity with the original trait-258 

based construct, participants completed the original trait version of the BPQ-BAVSF-J on 259 

Day 1. Because the RUN and CTL sessions took place on either Day 2 or Day 3 depending 260 

on the participant, we examined correlations between the modified BPQ scores obtained at 261 

the pre-assessment prior to each session on Day 2 and Day 3 and the original baseline BPQ 262 

scores obtained on Day 1. The modified BPQ scores were significantly positively correlated 263 

with scores on the original version on both Day 2 and Day 3 (Day 2: r = .50, p = .009; Day 3: 264 

r = .56, p = .002). These correlations provide preliminary support that the modified version 265 

retained continuity with the construct measured by the original trait-based BPQ-BAVSF-J. 266 

Given the state-adapted nature of the modified instructions, BPQ scores in the present study 267 

are described as self-reported present-moment interoceptive sensibility. 268

Mood states 270 

Mood states were assessed using the Japanese version of the Profile of Mood States 271 

2nd Edition–Adult Short Form (POMS 2-A Short) (51,52). The POMS 2-A Short is a 35-item 272 

self-report instrument designed to evaluate transient mood states. It yields scores on seven 273 

mood dimensions: anger–hostility (AH), confusion–bewilderment (CB), depression–dejection 274 

(DD), fatigue–inertia (FI), tension–anxiety (TA), vigor–activity (VA), and friendliness (F). 275 

Participants rated each item on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 0 (“not at all”) to 4 276 

(“extremely”). 277 

Higher scores on the VA and F subscales reflect more positive mood states, whereas 278 

higher scores on the remaining five subscales indicate greater severity of negative mood 279 

symptoms. In addition to the subscale scores, an overall index of mood disturbance, the TMD 280 

score, was computed. The total mood disturbance (TMD) score was calculated as AH + CB 281 

+ DD + FI + TA − VA, with higher values representing greater TMD. 282 

Electrocardiographic measurement 283 

Electrocardiographic (ECG) signals were recorded using biosignalsplux acquisition 284 

devices (PLUX Wireless Biosignals, Lisbon, Portugal) at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz to 285 

continuously assess the electrical timing of participants’ heartbeats throughout the 286 

interoceptive accuracy tasks. The ECG sensor’s three-electrode montage, including ground, 287 

was attached to the left side of the chest, with two recording electrodes approximately 288 

positioned over the V4–V5 region and the ground electrode placed inferior to them to ensure 289 

stable signal acquisition. 290 

ECG and behavioral response data were processed offline using MATLAB code 291 

developed in-house. R-peaks were detected automatically from the ECG signal, and the 292 

detected peaks were subsequently visually inspected to verify the absence of obvious 293 

artifacts, outliers, or abnormal detections. 294 

During the SSTWT and the RUN session, instantaneous HR was monitored and 295 

recorded using a myBeat device with ECG electrodes (UNION TOOL, Tokyo, Japan) placed 296 

over the V1–V2 region. This recording was used to monitor participants’ heart rate responses 297 

during exercise and to ensure that the exercise intensity was appropriately maintained 298 

throughout the treadmill protocol. 299

Perceptual measures 300 

The HCT score was calculated across the three trials using the following formula: HCT 301 

score = 1/3 Σ (1 − |actual number of heartbeats − self-reported number of heartbeats| / actual 302 

number of heartbeats). Higher scores were interpreted as indicating greater interoceptive 303 

accuracy. 304 

For the HTT, perceptual performance was evaluated using three indices adapted from 305 

the general framework proposed by Fittipaldi et al. (44): mean distance (md), modified 306 

Schandry index (mSI), and d′. The md index quantifies the discrepancy between cardiac 307 

frequency and tapping frequency across overlapping time windows, with lower values 308 

indicating closer correspondence between heartbeat rhythm and tapping rhythm. The mSI 309 

quantifies the correspondence between the total number of detected R-peaks and the total 310 

number of taps, with higher values indicating greater response-count agreement. The d′311 

index, based on Signal Detection Theory, quantifies participants’ sensitivity in discriminating 312 

heartbeat-related signals from non-signal responses, with higher values indicating greater 313 

sensitivity. Detailed calculation procedures for these indices are provided in Supplemental 314 

Methods (see Supplemental Methods, Supplemental Digital Content 1, which provides 315 

detailed calculation procedures for the HTT-derived md, mSI, and d′ indices). 316 

Statistical analyses 317 

Statistical analyses were performed in R version 4.3.2. Interoceptive accuracy, 318 

interoceptive sensibility, and mood scores were analyzed using repeated-measures two-way 319 

ANOVAs with condition (RUN, CTL) and time (Pre, Post) as within-subject factors. These 320 

analyses were conducted using the afex package (version 1.4.1), with Type III sums of 321 

squares and partial eta squared ( ηp ²) reported as the effect size. When significant condition 322 

× time interactions were observed, Bonferroni-corrected post hoc comparisons were 323 

conducted using the emmeans package (version 1.11.2). In addition, for complementary 324 

purposes, change scores for each condition were calculated as Post − Pre, and paired t-tests 325 

were used to compare ΔRUN and ΔCTL. To examine the association between running-326 

related changes in interoception and mood, contrast scores representing running-related 327 

effects were computed as ΔRUN − ΔCTL, and Pearson’s correlation analyses were 328 

conducted between contrast scores for interoceptive measures and those for POMS 329 

outcomes. All statistical tests were two-sided, and statistical significance was set a priori at 330

Mediation analyses were performed using the mediation package (version 4.5.0) (53) to 332 

examine whether running-related changes in interoceptive accuracy or present-moment 333 

interoceptive sensibility were statistically consistent with an indirect pathway linking running 334 

to mood improvement. Because the present study used a within-subject crossover design, 335 

mediation analyses were conducted using fixed-effects linear models with participant ID 336 

included as a fixed effect. For each participant and condition, condition-specific change 337 

scores were calculated as Post − Pre. The exposure variable was condition, coded as 1 for 338 

RUN and 0 for CTL. In each mediation model, the mediator was defined as the condition-339 

specific change in an interoceptive measure, and the outcome was defined as the condition-340 

specific change in the corresponding mood score. The mediator model included condition 341 

and participant fixed effects, and the outcome model included condition, the mediator, and 342 

participant fixed effects. The total effect, average direct effect (ADE), average causal 343 

mediation effect (ACME), and proportion mediated were estimated using quasi-Bayesian 344 

confidence intervals based on 5,000 simulations. Candidate mediation models were selected 345 

based on two criteria: first, both the interoceptive measure and the mood outcome showed 346 

significant condition × time interactions, indicating running-related changes; second, running-347 

related changes in the interoceptive measure were significantly correlated with running-348 

related changes in the mood outcome. TMD was specified as the primary mood outcome, 349 

and POMS subscales meeting these criteria were treated as exploratory outcomes. Because 350 

changes in the mediator and outcome were assessed over the same pre–post interval, these 351 

mediation analyses were interpreted as preliminary and as testing whether the data were 352 

statistically consistent with an indirect pathway, rather than as providing definitive evidence 353

Verification of moderate-intensity running 357 

We first verified that the RUN condition induced moderate-intensity physiological 358 

responses. HR and rating of perceived exertion (RPE) were monitored every minute during 359 

the running session (54). During the last minute of running, average HR and RPE were 360 

141.28 ± 13.34 bpm and 12.63 ± 1.80, respectively. These values correspond to the 361 

moderate-intensity range according to ACSM-based exercise intensity criteria (36). We 362 

further examined whether the RUN condition led to increased cardiac activity at the time of 363 

the subsequent interoceptive accuracy task. A two-way repeated-measures ANOVA on the 364 

number of R-peaks recorded during the 60-s HTT revealed a significant condition × time 365 

interaction ( F (1, 26) = 68.33, p < .001, ηp ² = .724). Post hoc comparisons showed that R-366 

peak counts significantly increased between pre and post in the RUN condition (75.22 ± 9.99 367 

to 89.70 ± 11.58; t (26) = −7.83, p < .001, d = −1.51). At post, R-peak counts were significantly 368 

higher in the RUN condition than in the CTL condition (89.70 ± 11.58 vs. 71.89 ± 11.60; t (26) 369 

= 7.88, p < .001, d = 1.52). These findings indicate that moderate-intensity running led to 370 

increased cardiac activity at the time of the post-exercise interoceptive accuracy task. 371

Mediation analysis of interoceptive sensibility and mood 485 

We examined whether changes in present-moment interoceptive sensibility statistically 486 

accounted for the effect of moderate-intensity running on mood improvement (Figure5A). 487 

Because changes in the mediator and outcome were assessed over the same pre–post 488 

interval, these mediation analyses were interpreted as preliminary and as testing whether 489 

the data were statistically consistent with an indirect pathway. Mediation analyses showed 490 

that changes in BPQ score partially mediated the effect of running on reductions in TMD 491 

score (ACME = −1.36, 95% CI [−3.04, −0.01], p = .04; ADE = −2.33, 95% CI [−4.11, −0.54], 492 

p = .01; total effect = −3.69, 95% CI [−5.82, −1.56], p = .002; proportion mediated = 0.36, 493 

95% CI [0.03, 0.78], p = .04) (Figure 5B). These findings suggest that increased present-494 

moment interoceptive sensibility may partly account for the reduction in TMD after running. 495 

We also conducted an exploratory mediation analysis for TA, given its significant 496 

association with running-related changes in BPQ score. Changes in BPQ score showed a 497 

significant indirect association between running and reductions in TA score (ACME = −0.36, 498 

95% CI [−0.90, −0.01], p = .046). However, the ADE was not significant (ADE = −0.60, 95% 499 

CI [−1.32, 0.13], p = .12), and the proportion mediated did not reach conventional statistical 500 

significance (proportion mediated = 0.35, 95% CI [−0.002, 1.31], p = .05) (Figure 5C). Thus, 501 

the mediation result for TA should be interpreted as modest and exploratory. 502

Discussion 523 

The present study examined whether acute moderate-intensity running changes 524 

interoception and mood states, and whether running-related changes in interoception are 525 

associated with mood improvement. The main findings were as follows. First, moderate-526 

intensity treadmill running increased self-reported present-moment interoceptive sensibility, 527 

as indexed by BPQ scores, but did not significantly change task-based measures of cardiac 528 

interoceptive accuracy, including HCT score and HTT-derived md, mSI, and d′ . Second, 529 

running reduced TMD and TA and increased VA compared with seated rest. Third, greater 530 

running-related increases in BPQ scores were associated with greater reductions in TMD, 531 

TA, and DD. Greater running-related increases in HTT d′ were also associated with greater 532 

increases in VA. Finally, mediation analyses suggested that changes in BPQ score partly 533 

accounted for the effect of running on reduced TMD, with weaker and exploratory evidence 534 

for TA. These findings suggest that subjective awareness of bodily sensations may be one 535 

process related to mood improvement after acute running. 536 

The present study found that moderate-intensity running improved mood states, as 537 

reflected by reduced TMD and TA and increased VA. This pattern is consistent with 538 

previous evidence showing that acute aerobic exercise can reduce anxiety and improve 539 

affective states (1,4). It is also consistent with running-specific findings showing that even a 540 

brief bout of moderate-intensity running can increase pleasant mood and arousal (28,29). 541 

In the present study, the RUN condition was performed at 50% of estimated V̇O ₂peak and 542 

lasted 15 min, indicating that a relatively short bout of moderate-intensity running was 543 

sufficient to improve both negative and positive mood states. These findings support the 544 

practical value of moderate-intensity running as a simple exercise mode for improving 545 

transient mood states in healthy young adults. 546 

A key finding of the present study is that running-related increases in BPQ scores were 547 

associated with improvements in mood states and statistically accounted for part of the 548 

effect of moderate-intensity running on TMD. A similar, although more modest and 549 

exploratory, pattern was observed for TA. These findings are consistent with the possibility 550 

of an exercise–interoception–mood pathway, in which exercise-induced changes in 551 

subjective interoceptive sensibility contribute to mood improvement. Physical activity has 552

been proposed to increase visceral-afferent input and the salience of bodily signals 553 

entering the interoceptive system, thereby influencing how bodily changes are processed 554 

and integrated with cognitive, motivational, and affective responses (11,16,20,55). Thus, 555 

acute running may have produced salient bodily changes, including increased HR and 556

also consistent with interoceptive exposure accounts, which suggest that salient 559 

physiological arousal induced by physical activity may increase familiarity with bodily 560

Accordingly, subjective interoceptive sensibility may be one proximal process through 562 

which moderate-intensity running contributes to improvements in mood states (Figure 6), 563 

although it likely operates alongside other physiological and psychological mechanisms, 564 

such as neurochemical changes, autonomic regulation, self-efficacy, and attentional 565 

distraction (4,9). 566

In contrast to our initial expectation, moderate-intensity running did not significantly 579 

change task-based indices of interoceptive accuracy. No significant condition × time 580 

interactions were observed for HCT score or for the HTT-derived indices of md, mSI, and 581 

d′ . This suggests that running did not produce a robust group-level improvement in cardiac 582 

interoceptive accuracy in the present study, despite increased cardiac activity after running. 583 

This finding differs from previous studies showing that cardiac awareness or heartbeat 584 

perception performance can improve during or after exercise (21,22,23). However, those 585 

studies involved stronger exercise-induced cardiac activation, assessment during exercise, 586 

or higher-intensity physical activity than the present protocol. Thus, one possible 587 

explanation is that the bodily signals induced by the present moderate-intensity running 588 

protocol may not have been strong enough, or sufficiently specific to cardiac sensations, to 589 

improve task-based heartbeat perception. This interpretation is also consistent with the 590 

idea that improvement in heartbeat perception performance may require sufficiently 591 

increased salience of cardiac sensations. For example, Smith et al. (17) showed that 592 

healthy participants improved heartbeat tapping performance during an inspiratory breath-593 

hold condition designed to amplify cardiac sensations. Breath holding may improve 594 

heartbeat perception performance relatively directly by increasing the salience of cardiac 595 

sensations. In contrast, the post-running state is likely to involve a broader set of exercise-596 

related bodily cues, including cardiovascular, respiratory, thermal, muscular, and effort-597 

related sensations (57,58). Such broad exercise-related bodily cues may influence affective 598 

responses but may not necessarily enhance cardiac sensations in a sufficiently specific or 599 

direct manner to improve heartbeat perception performance. Thus, differences in exercise 600 

intensity, the specificity of bodily manipulation, and assessment timing may help explain 601 

why task-based interoceptive accuracy did not improve in the present study. 602 

Although moderate-intensity running did not produce a significant group-level increase 603 

in HTT d′ , the exploratory association between running-related changes in HTT d′ and VA 604 

suggests that individual differences in cardiac signal discrimination may be related to 605 

positive activation-related mood responses. Specifically, individuals who showed greater 606 

increases in d′ also showed greater increases in vigor–activity. One possible interpretation 607 

is that individuals who became more sensitive to cardiac signals after running may have 608 

experienced exercise-induced bodily activation as a more salient and energizing state. In 609

The present findings may also be considered in relation to predictive processing or 617 

active inference accounts of interoception and emotion, although the present study was not 618 

designed to directly test these frameworks. From this perspective, emotional experience is 619 

not determined solely by peripheral bodily changes themselves but by how the brain 620 

interprets, weighs, and updates internal bodily signals (59,60). Moderate-intensity running 621 

produces clear and salient changes in bodily states, such as increased HR and respiration. 622 

Importantly, these bodily changes occur in a context in which their causes are identifiable 623 

and predictable, namely the physiological demands of physical activity. One speculative 624 

interpretation is that exercise-induced bodily signals may provide relatively clear 625 

interoceptive evidence for updating the subjective representation of bodily states, rather 626 

than remaining ambiguous internal sensations. This framework may help explain why 627 

changes in BPQ scores, but not all indices of interoceptive accuracy, were associated with 628 

mood improvement: what may be critical is not simply detecting cardiac signals more 629 

accurately, but becoming subjectively aware of bodily changes in a context in which those 630 

signals can be meaningfully interpreted and integrated into emotional experience (12,20). 631 

However, because the present study did not directly measure bodily appraisal, neural 632 

prediction errors, precision weighting, or brain activity, this interpretation should be 633 

regarded as a theoretical account rather than direct evidence for predictive coding 634 

mechanisms (60,61). 635

states and increased present-moment interoceptive sensibility in healthy young adults. 637 

Specifically, running reduced TMD and TA, increased VA, and increased BPQ scores, but 638 

did not significantly alter task-based indices of cardiac interoceptive accuracy. Running-639
