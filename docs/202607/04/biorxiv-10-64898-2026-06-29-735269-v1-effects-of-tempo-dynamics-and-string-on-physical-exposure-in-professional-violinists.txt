# 1. Abstract 13 

This study examined how tempo, dynamics, and string influence upper-extremity physical exposure in 14 professional violinists and how exposure variability is distributed among musical characteristics, between-15 subject differences, and residual variability. Twelve violinists performed seven standardized scales while 16 bilateral upper-arm and wrist kinematics and shoulder and forearm muscle activity were recorded. Linear 17 mixed-effects models showed that faster tempo increased right upper-arm velocity and bilateral forearm 18 activity while reducing right upper-arm and wrist ranges of motion. Louder dynamics increased bilateral 19 forearm and right trapezius activity and right-wrist ranges of motion. Higher-posture strings increased 20 right upper-arm elevation and right shoulder muscle activity. Variance analysis identified exposures 21 predominantly related to musical characteristics, jointly related to musical characteristics and between-22 subject differences, predominantly related to between-subject differences, or mainly unexplained. These 23 findings support future exposure prediction from musical characteristics and targeted prevention through 24 repertoire-based workload management, structured recovery, and individualized technique-focused 25 strategies. 26

# 3. Introduction 33 

Occupational musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs), often referred to as playing-related MSDs (PRMSDs) in 34 musicians, are prevalent among classical musicians (Cruder et al., 2020, 2023; Leaver et al., 2011; 35 Stanhope et al., 2022; Vastamäki et al., 2020). The most common pain locations reported by university 36 level or professional musicians include the neck, shoulders, and lower back (Leaver et al., 2011). 37 Professional orchestra musicians are 50% more likely to develop back pain and 160% more likely to 38 develop shoulder pain than the general workforce (Vastamäki et al., 2020). For musicians, occupational 39 exposure includes time spent practicing alone, rehearsing with other musicians, recording music, and 40 performing in front of an audience. While the overall practicing load has been shown to be dependent on 41 experience, for violinists, it generally includes 4–6 hours per day and 17–28 hours per week (Chi et al., 42 2020) 43 Among classical musicians, upper string players, including violinists and violists, have a higher prevalence 44 of PRMSDs than musicians playing other instruments (Kok et al., 2018; Leaver et al., 2011; Nyman et al., 45 2007; Paarup et al., 2011). Playing an upper string instrument involves elevated and unsupported arms 46 performing asymmetric tasks requiring static loading, repetitive movements, and precise motor control. 47 The non-bowing arm (the left arm) stabilizes the instrument and performs repeated shoulder rotations 48 and precise finger movements to depress the strings in exact locations while producing rhythmic 49 oscillation of the fingertip to produce vibrato. The bowing arm (the right arm) performs repetitive 50 movements involving the shoulder, elbow, wrist, and fingers to draw the bow across the strings with 51 precise motor control required to produce any specific type of sound (ex. quiet vs loud, and slow vs fast). 52 Furthermore, the two upper extremities must also continuously coordinate postural support, fine motor 53 control, and force regulation to arrive at the desired sound. This bilateral coordination may increase 54 physical exposure across both sides of the upper body, particularly during technically demanding passages 55 of music. 56 Upper-extremity exposures are likely influenced by the characteristics of the music played, including the 57 speed at which notes are played (tempo), the volume of the sound generated (dynamics), and the string 58 on which the notes are played. Previous studies have examined the effects of tempo and dynamics on 59 shoulder and forearm muscle activity; however, these studies have included musicians with different 60 proficiency levels, and their results may not reflect the exposure patterns of highly skilled professional 61 musicians (Mann et al., 2021; Mann, Paarup, et al., 2023). Less is known about how these musical 62 characteristics affect kinematic physical exposures, including upper-arm and wrist postures and 63 movement velocities, exposures that have previously been associated with neck and shoulder 64 musculoskeletal disorders in other types of work (Nordander et al., 2016). Tempo is expected to influence 65 movement velocity, particularly in the bowing arm and in the fingers of the non-bowing hand. Dynamics 66 are expected to influence the amount of force required to produce sound, with playing louder, i.e., 67 fortissimo (ff), requiring greater bowing force than playing softer, i.e., pianissimo (pp). The string played 68 is expected to affect both posture and muscle activity of both the bowing and non-bowing arms, as 69 illustrated in Figure 1 . The placement order of the four strings of a violin, E, A, D, and G, dictate the playing 70 posture bilaterally, where playing on the E-string (the string with the highest pitch) requires the lowest 71 right shoulder elevation and the largest external rotation of the left shoulder, and playing on the G-string 72 (the string with the lowest pitch) requires the highest shoulder elevation and the smallest external 73 rotation of the left shoulder). Together, these biomechanical considerations suggest that musical 74 characteristics may shape violinists’ intrinsic physical loads, including upper extremity posture, movement 75

4.2. Experimental protocol 106 

Each violinist participated in a laboratory experiment, during which muscle activity and kinematics were 107 recorded in the shoulders, upper arms, forearms, and wrists. Playing performances were simultaneously 108 video recorded for reference. 109 The study followed an experimental crossover design. During the experiment, participants performed 110 seven one-octave scales (eight ascending and eight descending notes) at 120 beats per minute with a time 111 signature of 4/4. The seven scales systematically varied in three musical characteristics: tempo (2 levels: 112 whole notes [30 notes per minute] and sixteenth notes [480 notes per minute]), dynamics (2 levels: 113 pianissimo [pp] and fortissimo [ff]), and the strings in which the scales were played, operationalized using 114 scale key as a proxy (3 levels: E major, A major, and G major). 115 In this study, the four violin strings were grouped as “higher-posture” and “lower-posture” strings, based 116 on the right-arm, or bowing-arm, posture required to play them, rather than on their pitch ( Figure 1 ). The 117 E and A strings were therefore considered lower-posture strings, whereas the D and G strings were 118 considered higher-posture strings. To complete a one-octave scale, the notes can be played either across 119 two strings with the same left-hand position or on one string with a shift in left-hand position. The A-120 major scale was played on the A and E strings with the same left-hand position and was used as the lower-121 posture reference. The G-major scale was played on the G and D strings with the same left-hand position 122 and was used as the higher-posture comparison. Thus, the comparison between the G- and A-major scales 123 was treated as the primary contrast for assessing differences between higher- and lower-posture strings. 124 The E-major scale was played solely on the E string but required a shift in left-hand position on the neck 125 of the violin. It was therefore included as an additional lower-posture condition. Comparisons between 126 the G- and E-major scales were used as additional higher- versus lower-posture contrasts, whereas 127 comparisons between the E- and A-major scales were treated as secondary contrasts to examine 128 differences related to left-hand position and playing exposure within lower-posture strings. ( Table 1 ). 129 Additionally, a metronome was used throughout to ensure the speed was consistent. Participants were 130 asked to play the scales as if performing them for an audience, using their best vibrato and producing 131 their best sound. To ensure scales were played in the same manner across all participants, the fingering 132 was provided for each scale. All scales were played with smooth movements and with the bow constantly 133 on the string (called legato bowing). Participants were seated on a chair without a backrest. Music sheet 134 was placed on a stand positioned at a self-selected comfortable height and distance, and page turning 135 was not required. Participants were given sufficient time to feel warmed up and to then rest before 136 beginning the experiment. 137

4.3. Measurement of kinematics 145 

Kinematic measurements of the upper arms were obtained using two accelerometers (AX3, Axivity Ltd., 146 Newcastle, UK) recording at 25 Hz (Fan et al., 2021). The sensors were positioned beneath the deltoid 147 muscles on both upper arms. 148 Bilateral wrist kinematics were recorded using two biaxial goniometers (Biometrics Ltd., Cwmfelinfach, 149 Gwent, UK) at a sampling frequency of 1000 Hz. The goniometer was placed with one block over the third 150 metacarpal bone, and the other block positioned at the midline between the two forearm bones, as 151 presented in Simonsen et al. (Simonsen et al., 2018). 152 Upper-arm elevation was defined as inclination angles relative to a neutral vertical reference posture 153 performed by each participant while sitting with a lateral lean until the upper arm hung vertically while 154 holding a 2 kg dumbbell (Bernmark & Wiktorin, 2002). The positive direction in the elevation/depression 155 movement for the upper arm was elevation. The reference posture for the wrist (0 degrees flexion and 156 deviation) was defined by having the participants place their hands flat on a horizontal surface, with the 157 hand adjusted so that a straight line was formed between the third metacarpal bone and the forearm, as 158 presented by Nordander et al (Nordander et al., 2016). The maximal flexion/extension range of motion of 159 the wrist was determined by having the participant perform maximal wrist flexion and extension three 160 times while seated with their elbows supported on a table and the forearms positioned horizontally. 161 Maximal ulnar and radial deviation range of motion were determined for each participant through three 162 voluntary range of motion trials performed with the palm flat on a horizontal surface. The positive 163 direction in the flexion/extension movement for the wrist was defined as flexion, while that in the 164 deviation movement was defined as radial deviation. 165 

4.4. Measurement of muscle activity 166 

Muscle activity of the upper trapezius, deltoid, and forearm muscles was monitored bilaterally using 167 surface electromyography (sEMG). Self-adhesive bipolar Ag/AgCl electrodes (N-00-S/25, Ambu A/S, 168 Copenhagen, Denmark) were applied with an inter-electrode distance of 2 cm at each location. Before 169 electrode placement, the skin was shaved and cleaned with alcohol to reduce impedance. Electrodes were 170 secured using conductive gel and adhesive fixation to maintain contact during movement. The signals 171 were transmitted via actively shielded cables to a digital data logger (Oldenzaal, The Netherlands), 172 sampled at 1000 Hz per channel using a 24-bit A/D converter. 173 For the upper trapezius, electrodes were positioned 2 cm lateral to the midpoint between the C7 spinous 174 process and the acromion (Mathiassen et al., 1995). For the deltoid, electrodes were placed according to 175 the SENIAM (Surface EMG for the Non-Invasive Assessment of Muscles) guidelines for the middle/lateral 176

4.5. Data processing 193 

The EMG signals were processed through the following processing pipeline. First, the raw signals were 194 digitally bandpass-filtered between 20 and 400 Hz and then passed through a 50 Hz comb notch filter to 195 remove power-line interference. The root mean square (RMS) values of the filtered signals were 196 computed using 125-ms epochs and subsequently corrected for noise by power subtraction. The resulting 197 RMS values were normalized to the corresponding RVE level, calculated as the mean RMS value from the 198 three RVC trials, and expressed as %RVE (Mathiassen et al., 1995). The 50th percentile (P50) was 199 calculated for each muscle site and for each scale. (Fan et al., 2024; G.-Å. Hansson et al., 2000). 200 Accelerometer data were low-pass filtered at 5 Hz using a Blackman window filter and subsequently 201 converted into elevation angles for the upper arms, with positive values indicating elevation. The 50th 202 percentile was calculated as a summary metric for elevation. Ranges of motion were computed as the 203 difference between the 90th and 10th percentiles (P90 – P10) of the angle distributions. Angular velocities 204 were obtained as the first derivatives of the corresponding angular signals, as described in previous 205 studies (Fan et al., 2021; G. Hansson et al., 2001), and 50th percentile values were calculated even for 206 velocity. 207 Goniometer signals were resampled to 20 Hz, low-pass filtered at 5 Hz using a Blackman window filter, 208 and calibrated by subtracting the wrist angle measured in the neutral reference posture (G.-Å. Hansson 209 et al., 1996), yielding deviation and flexion angles, which were used to calculate the range of motion and 210 angular velocity. Consequently, data processing yielded one numeric value for each scale and physical 211 exposure measure for each participant, and these values were subsequently used in the statistical 212 analyses. 213

5.1. Effects of tempo 295 

Changing the tempo from whole notes to sixteenth notes (from slow to fast) produced clear changes in 296 kinematic and muscle activity, especially on the right side ( Figure 2, Figure 3, Table 3, Table 6 ). 5.3. Effects of string 343 

As described in the Methods, string effects were evaluated using scale key as a proxy, with the G- versus 344 A-major comparison serving as the primary contrast between higher- and lower-posture strings. 345 Comparisons involving the E-major scale were used as additional contrasts to assess whether similar 346 patterns were observed across lower-posture conditions and whether left-hand position influenced the 347 results. Overall, string-related differences were most evident on the right side, whereas left-hand-348 position-related differences were more limited and mainly observed on the left side ( Figure 2 , Figure 3 ,349 

Table 5, Table 6, Table S1 ). 350 The largest right-sided changes were observed when the G-major scale, which involved higher-posture 351 strings, was compared with the A- and E-major scales, which involved lower-posture strings ( Table 5, 352 

Table 6 ). Compared with the A-major scale, the G-major scale increased right upper-arm elevation by 19° 353 ([Q1, Q3] [17, 24]; p < 0.001). Compared with the E-major scale, the G-major scale increased right upper-354 arm elevation by 24° ([21, 29]; p < 0.001). In contrast, no significant difference was observed between the 355 A- and E-major scales, suggesting that right upper-arm elevation was mainly associated with differences 356 in strings used during the scale rather than left-hand position. 357 A similar pattern was observed for right shoulder muscle activity ( Table 5, Table 6 ). Right trapezius activity 358 increased by 34%RVE when comparing the G-major scale with the A-major scale ([12, 49]; p < 0.001) and 359 by 37%RVE when comparing the G-major scale with the E-major scale ([8, 52]; p < 0.001), with no 360 significant difference between the A- and E-major scales. Other right-sided physical exposures were either non-365 significant or small in magnitude. 366 On the left side, changes were mainly observed in wrist deviation and deltoid activity ( Table 5, Table 6 ). 367 Similar to the right-sided pattern, left wrist radial deviation increased when comparing the G-major scale 368 with the A-major scale by 7° ([4, 9]; p < 0.001) and with the E-major scale by 8° ([4, 10]; p < 0.001), while 369 no significant difference was observed between the A- and E-major scales. 370 In contrast, left deltoid activity showed a different pattern ( Table 5, Table 6 ). Moreover, when comparing the two lower-posture 373 conditions, the E-major scale was associated with a 7%RVE decrease in left deltoid activity compared with 374 the A-major scale ([-10, -2]; p = 0.002). This suggests that left deltoid activity may be influenced more by 375 left-hand position than by the strings used during the scale. # 6. Discussion 430 

This study examined how three musical characteristics—tempo, dynamics, and string—were associated 431 with upper-extremity kinematics and muscle activity in professional violinists. All three characteristics 432 affected physical exposure, but in distinct ways. 433 Faster tempo primarily affected the right, or bowing, side, increasing right upper-arm velocity and forearm 434 muscle activity while reducing right upper-arm and wrist ranges of motion. Louder dynamics mainly 435 increased muscle activity in both forearms and the right upper trapezius and increased right-wrist ranges 436 of motion. Playing on higher-posture strings mainly affected the right side, increasing right upper-arm 437 elevation and right shoulder muscle activity, whereas effects related to left-hand position were limited 438 and were observed mainly in left deltoid activity. 439 Variance analysis further showed that the sources of variability differed across exposures. Right upper-440 arm elevation velocity, bilateral forearm muscle activity, and wrist-deviation velocity were predominantly 441 related to musical characteristics. Right upper-arm elevation posture, left upper-arm elevation velocity, 442 right-wrist ranges of motion, and right shoulder muscle activity reflected contributions from both musical 443 characteristics and between-subject differences. In contrast, many wrist postures, wrist-flexion velocities, 444 and left shoulder exposures were predominantly related to between-subject differences. Finally, left 445 wrist-flexion range of motion and bilateral upper-arm elevation range of motion were characterized 446 mainly by residual variability not explained by the investigated musical characteristics or between-subject 447 differences. 448 

6.1. Effects of music characteristics on exposure 449 

Each musical characteristic influenced physical exposure in distinct patterns, suggesting different 450 underlying mechanisms. 451 Tempo primarily affected right-sided kinematics and muscle activity. Faster tempo increased right upper-452 arm velocity and muscle activity in the right deltoid and both forearms, while reducing the ranges of 453 motion of the right upper arm and wrist. These findings are partly consistent with previous studies 454 showing increased physical demand at higher tempi (Mann, Paarup, et al., 2023; Rensing et al., 2018; 455 Reynolds et al., 2014), but extend them by demonstrating concurrent reductions in ranges of motion in 456 both the right wrist and upper arm. This combination suggests a movement strategy characterized by 457 faster but more constrained motion, potentially limiting unnecessary joint movement and supporting 458 rapid and precise bowing. A notable contrast to this combination was the decrease in right trapezius 459 activity despite increased right deltoid and forearm activity. Earlier studies reported either no change or 460 increased right trapezius activity at higher tempi (Mann, Paarup, et al., 2023; Reynolds et al., 2014). 461 Because previous studies included mixed-skill populations, whereas the present study involved only 462 professional violinists, the discrepancy may reflect skill- and experience-dependent motor strategies. 463 Professional violinists may redistribute muscular effort within the shoulder complex during faster playing, 464 reducing trapezius activity while maintaining bowing performance. 465 Dynamics mainly modulated muscle activity, particularly in both forearms and the right upper trapezius. 466 This pattern partly aligns with previous findings of increased right-forearm activity and left-finger force at 467 stronger dynamics (Kinoshita & Obata, 2009; Mann, Paarup, et al., 2023; Rensing et al., 2018), but the 468 present results also showed clearer bilateral forearm engagement. Unlike tempo, stronger dynamics 469 increased the ranges and velocities of right-wrist flexion and deviation. This suggests that louder playing 470

19 may rely on wider and faster distal joint ranges of motion to help forearm and shoulder muscles generate 471 bow force and sound intensity, whereas changes in upper-arm posture were comparatively small. 472 Although string use was assessed using scales played in different keys, the results suggest that the strings 473 used during the scale were the main driver of right-sided exposure differences. The G-major scale was 474 played on higher-posture strings, namely the G and D strings, whereas the A-major scale was played on 475 lower-posture strings, namely the A and E strings. The E-major scale represented another lower-posture 476 condition, as it was played only on the E string but with two different left-hand positions. As string position 477 became higher, right-sided shoulder exposure showed a graded increase, with the G-major scale 478 producing the greatest right upper-arm elevation and right deltoid and upper-trapezius activity, followed 479 by the A-major and then the E-major scales. This is consistent with previous reports that shoulder 480 movement increases when playing shifts from the E string toward the G string (Shan & Visentin, 2003; 481 Wolf et al., 2019). In contrast, differences between the A- and E-major scales had limited effects on right-482 sided exposures, suggesting that neither left-hand position alone nor the shift between the A and E strings 483 was sufficient to substantially alter right-arm demands. 484 On the left side, the string-related pattern differed. Left wrist deviation changed when the G-major scale 485 was compared with the other keys, showing a pattern broadly consistent with the right-sided response. 486 However, left deltoid activity differed between the A- and E-major scales and increased significantly only 487 between the G- and E-major scales. This suggests that left deltoid activity may be more sensitive to left-488 hand position or instrument-stabilizing demands than to the strings used alone. 489 Thus, across musical characteristics, both similarities and contrasts emerged. Tempo and dynamics both 490 increased forearm muscle activity, indicating that both demanded force generation and control. However, 491 they differed in their kinematic patterns: an increase in tempo was associated with reduced joint ranges 492 of motion, whereas louder dynamics increased wrist ranges of motion. In contrast, string effects were 493 primarily proximal, affecting upper-arm elevation and shoulder muscle activity, and reflected positional 494 requirements rather than dynamic or temporal demands. Together, these findings suggest that the 495 investigated characteristics influenced physical exposure through largely distinct mechanisms. 496

6.3. Physical exposures and risk of developing musculoskeletal disorders 574 

The magnitude of a physical exposure is meaningful for PRMSD risk only when considered together with 575 its temporal profile, including how long, how often, and in what sequence the exposure occurs. Many risk 576 and exposure assessment studies focus on the whole-day exposure (6–8 hours) in occupations with 577 relatively homogenous work tasks. For example, proposed action levels are exposure thresholds above 578 which preventive measures should be considered to reduce the risk of work-related musculoskeletal 579 disorders (Arvidsson et al., 2021). These thresholds are based on prolonged measurements, often 580 covering an approximately 8-hour working day, whereas the present study examined short standardized 581 scale tasks. However, scales with similar musical characteristics are recurring components of violinists’ 582 daily practice, rehearsal, and performance. The observed task-specific exposures can therefore inform 583 approximate exposure scenarios for days dominated by music with similar characteristics, although they 584 should not be interpreted as directly equivalent to full-day occupational measurements. 585 Within this context, several exposures reached magnitudes comparable to those reported in physically 586 demanding occupations. Median right upper-arm elevation involving lower-posture strings was generally 587 approximately 30°–50° and increased to around 70° during the G-major scale involving higher-posture 588 strings. These values are comparable to or larger than those reported in occupations such as hairdressing 589 (29° at the 50 th percentile) and retail shelf stocking (55° at the 90 th percentile) (Forsman et al., 2017; 590 Nordander et al., 2016). High arm elevation has been associated with an increased risk of shoulder 591 disorders (Van Der Molen et al., 2017). Specifically, median right upper-arm elevation exceeded the 592 proposed action level of 30° was considered as a risk factor for developing MSDs, which was lower than 593 the right-arm elevation observed in most scales (Arvidsson et al., 2021). This indicates that elevated right-594 arm posture represents a substantial baseline exposure in violin playing, while music involving higher-595 posture strings may further increase the accumulated shoulder load. 596

6.4. Practical implications 618 

The present findings have potential implications for occupational workload management based on 619 repertoire selection and therefore strategies for preventing PRMSDsat an organizational level. 620 First, the results demonstrate that different musical characteristics impose distinct biomechanical 621 demands, meaning that physical exposure can vary substantially depending on what music piece is being 622 played, even when practice duration remains constant. This is particularly relevant in professional 623 orchestras, where rehearsal schedules are typically fixed despite variations in repertoire (Nyman et al., 624 2007). Introducing variation in practice and rehearsal schemes based on musical characteristics, such as 625 alternating between passages with different dynamics or between music played predominantly on lower 626 and higher strings, may help distribute mechanical load across rehearsal periods and reduce localized 627 fatigue and disorder risk. 628 Second, the diverse variance patterns across physical exposures indicate that different risk-mitigation 629 strategies may be required. Exposures predominantly related to musical characteristics, such as right 630 upper-arm elevation velocity and bilateral forearm muscle activity, may be partly managed through 631 repertoire variation and scheduling. Identifying these characteristic-dependent exposures also provides a 632 basis for estimating physical exposure from musical characteristics and may ultimately support exposure 633 prediction directly from sheet music. In contrast, right upper-arm elevation posture remained consistently 634 high across conditions, although its variance reflected contributions from both musical characteristics and 635 between-subject differences. Repertoire selection alone is therefore unlikely to reduce this exposure 636 substantially while conventional violin-playing techniques are maintained. 6.5. Strengths and limitations 660 

A key strength of this study is that it systematically examined the influence of three fundamental musical 661 characteristics, i.e., tempo, dynamics, and string, on kinematics and muscle activity in the upper body and 662 upper extremities of professional violinists. 663 The investigated tasks, i.e. playing scales, incorporated controlled variations in dynamics and string, thus 664 enabling assessment of the influence of each characteristic on biomechanical exposure. However, to 665 maintain a manageable experimental duration, the range of tempi was limited, and not all combinations 666 of tempo, dynamics and string were tested. Specially, the choice of strings was proxied by the scale key, 667 while in practice, there are four strings in combination with multiple main left-hand positions, which was 668 not fully tested with high resolution. As a result, the observed effects, especially the interaction effects 669 between the three characteristics, should only be evaluated to a limited extent. Future studies should 670 investigate a broader range of interactions between musical characteristics to determine whether their 671 effects on physical exposure are independent or combined. Moreover, real musical performance is 672 substantially more complex than scale playing, and the effects observed here may not fully reflect those 673 present when playing an actual repertoire. Future studies should therefore investigate the generalizability 674 of these findings in more ecologically valid musical contexts. 675 Some participants reported musculoskeletal pain within the preceding 7 days or 12 months. However, all 676 participants were active professional orchestral musicians who did not report pain to be sufficiently severe 677 to require sick leave; thus, they were able to perform as usual. In addition, the prevalence of reported 678 pain in this sample was lower than that typically observed in other populations of musicians (Ioannou et 679

# 7. Conclusion 691 

This study demonstrated that three musical characteristics, i.e., tempo, dynamics, and string, were 692 systematically associated with upper-extremity biomechanical exposures during standardized scale 693 playing by professional violinists. The associations differed across musical characteristics and exposure 694 variables. Variance analysis further revealed that some exposures were predominantly related to musical 695 characteristics, others reflected substantial contributions from both musical characteristics and between-696 subject differences, and others were predominantly related to between-subject differences or residual 697 variability. 698 Future research should validate these findings in larger samples and more complex musical contexts to 699 determine whether the observed effects are also evident during the performance of complete musical 700 pieces. Estimating physical exposure from musical characteristics may ultimately support exposure 701 prediction directly from sheet music. Moreover, understanding how musical characteristics influence 702 physical exposure may clarify mechanisms contributing to the risk of PRMSDs among violinists and support 703 the development of targeted interventions, including repertoire-based workload management, optimized 704 rehearsal and practice schedules, structured breaks and recovery, and individualized technique-focused 705 strategies. 706
